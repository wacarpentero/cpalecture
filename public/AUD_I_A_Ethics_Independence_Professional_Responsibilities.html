Ethics, Independence & Professional Responsibilities

Learning Objectives

  • Apply the AICPA Code of Professional Conduct principles and rules (integrity, objectivity & independence, due care, responsibilities to the public).
  • Use the conceptual framework to identify threats to compliance and apply appropriate safeguards.
  • Evaluate independence for attest engagements: covered members, financial interests, family/relationship issues, nonattest services, and other common pitfalls.
  • Comply with rules on confidential client information, records, fees, commissions/advertising, and acts discreditable.
  • Recognize differences in SEC/PCAOB/GAGAS contexts at a high level for exam awareness.

1) AICPA Code — Principles & Rules

  • Principles: set the ideal—act in the public interest with integrity, objectivity, due care, and quality control.
  • Rules: enforceable requirements for independence, integrity & objectivity, general standards, compliance with standards, accounting principles, confidential info, acts discreditable, fees/commissions, advertising/solicitation, form of organization & name.
  • Due care & competence: undertake only what you can competently perform; plan & supervise; obtain sufficient relevant data.
Tip: When multiple rules are in play, address independence first (if an attest engagement), then consider other rules.

2) Conceptual Framework — Threats & Safeguards

  • Identify threats (seven categories), evaluate significance, and apply safeguards to reduce to an acceptable level—or decline the engagement.
  • Examples: self‑review threat when performing bookkeeping for an attest client; familiarity threat from close relative in a key position; advocacy threat from promoting client securities.
  • Safeguards: remove/rotate the individual; separate teams; independent review; consult national office; enhance client oversight; decline prohibited nonattest services.
Documentation: For judgment calls, document the identified threats, safeguards applied, and conclusions.

3) Independence — Who & What Impairs It

Covered Members & Family

  • Covered member: attest team members; those in a position to influence; partner/manager providing >10 hours nonattest services to the attest client; partners in the office of the lead attest partner.
  • Immediate family: spouse/spousal equivalent & dependents—subject to same financial interest rules as covered member.
  • Close relatives: parents, siblings, nondependent children—watch for key positions or material financial interests known to the member.

Financial/Business Relationships

  • Impairments: direct financial interests; material indirect interests; loans outside normal lending terms; trustee/executor with authority; joint investments.
  • Generally permitted: certain normal lending relationships from a financial institution attest client (if auto‑collateralized, immaterial, and in compliance with policy), and immaterial indirect interests.
  • Employment: a partner or professional in a key position at the client impairs independence; consider cooling‑off policies; discuss job offers promptly and remove individuals from the engagement.

Nonattest Services

  • Prohibited to perform management responsibilities (authorize, oversee, custody of assets, design/implement client controls, make decisions).
  • Allowed services require: client accepts responsibility; designates a competent individual to oversee; evaluates & accepts results; you establish understanding in writing.
  • Common traps: bookkeeping, IT systems implementation, valuation services tied to material amounts—often impair independence for attest clients.

Fees & Other Issues

  • Contingent fees: prohibited for attest clients and for preparing an original/amended tax return/claim for refund.
  • Commissions/referral fees: prohibited for attest clients; permitted in certain nonattest contexts if disclosed.
  • Unpaid fees: fees overdue for more than one year before the current report date may impair independence (viewed as a loan).

4) Confidential Client Information & Records

  • Do not disclose without client consent except for: valid subpoena/court order, peer review, ethics investigation, or other legal/professional requirements.
  • Client‑provided records: return upon request.
  • Member‑prepared records: provide if needed for the client’s financial reporting objectives (fees may be charged, but do not withhold due to unpaid fees).
  • Working papers: property of the firm; disclose only as permitted by rules/law.
Common pitfall: withholding client records over a fee dispute—this can be an act discreditable.

5) Acts Discreditable & Other Professional Responsibilities

  • Examples include: failure to follow standards, failure to return client records, discrimination/harassment, solicitation of exam questions/answers, negligence in the preparation of records/financial statements, failure to file personal tax returns or remit payroll taxes on behalf of a client.
  • Advertising/solicitation: must not be false, misleading, or deceptive.
  • Firm name & form: comply with state law and AICPA rules; do not imply commissions for attest services.

6) Special Contexts (Awareness for Exam)

  • SEC/PCAOB issuers: stricter independence (e.g., partner rotation, prohibited tax services, fee disclosure, employment/cooling‑off); follow SEC/PCAOB rules when applicable.
  • GAGAS (Yellow Book): enhanced documentation for independence and nonaudit services; consider conceptual framework with specific safeguards and management’s skill/knowledge/experience.
  • State boards: can be more restrictive; always check state requirements.

7) Ethics Scenarios — Mini Drills

Scenario A — Gift & Hospitality

Senior receives playoff tickets from a client’s CFO.

  • Threats: self‑interest, familiarity.
  • Safeguards: decline or reimburse at market value; rotate individual; document.
  • Conclusion: avoid gifts that a reasonable third party would view as influencing judgment.

Scenario B — Bookkeeping for Attest Client

Firm proposes to post journal entries and prepare FS for an attest client.

  • Threats: self‑review, management participation.
  • Safeguards: prohibited unless client oversees and you avoid management functions; for many attest situations, independence would be impaired.

Scenario C — Relative in Key Position

Associate’s sibling is controller at the attest client.

  • Threats: familiarity, undue influence.
  • Action: remove the associate from team; evaluate further safeguards.

Scenario D — Unpaid Fees

Prior‑year attest fees remain unpaid beyond one year before the current report date.

  • Threat: self‑interest (loan‑like).
  • Conclusion: independence impaired unless resolved.

8) Quick Checks

  1. Direct financial interest by a covered member in an attest client? Independence impaired.
  2. Immediate family employed in a key position at the client? Independence impaired.
  3. Are contingent fees allowed for an audit client? No.
  4. Can you disclose client info to a peer reviewer? Yes—allowed by rule; confidentiality is still expected.
  5. If threats can’t be reduced to acceptable level? Decline/withdraw from the engagement.
