Ethics & Responsibilities in Tax Practice

Learning Objectives (aligned to Blueprint representative tasks)

  • Recall & apply the regulations governing practice before the Internal Revenue Service (IRS) (Circular 230): competence, due diligence, conflicts of interest, fees & solicitation rules, written advice standards, and recordkeeping.
  • Identify who is a tax return preparer and distinguish signing vs. non‑signing preparers.
  • Recall situations that give rise to preparer penalties under the Internal Revenue Code (IRC) and regulations.
  • Apply the correct penalty framework or disclosure requirement to a given client scenario.

At a Glance

Practice before the IRS — Circular 230

  • Competence Only undertake work you’re competent to perform; get help if needed.
  • Due diligence Verify information and make reasonable inquiries (esp. credits like Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC)).
  • Conflicts Disclose & obtain informed written consent; otherwise decline/withdraw.
  • Fees/ads No unconscionable fees; generally no contingent fees for original returns (limited exceptions).
  • Written advice Consider law & facts; don’t rely on unreasonable assumptions or representations.
  • Records Return client records on request; retain copies as required by law.

Preparer framework — Internal Revenue Code (IRC)

  • Who’s a preparer? Paid to prepare or substantially assist with a return/claim. Volunteers ≠ preparers.
  • Standards Reasonable basis (~20%), Substantial authority (~40%), More‑likely‑than‑not (MLTN) (>50%).
  • §6694(a) Understatement due to unreasonable position — avoid with substantial authority or with reasonable basis + adequate disclosure.
  • §6694(b) Willful or reckless conduct — higher penalty; disclosure won’t cure.
  • §6695 Misc. preparer penalties (copy, signature, Preparer Tax Identification Number (PTIN), due diligence, e‑file/records, no negotiating client checks, etc.).
  • §6701 Aiding & abetting understatement — applies even if not the preparer of record.
Exam tip: To avoid a preparer penalty for an undisclosed position, you generally need substantial authority. With adequate disclosure on Form 8275/8275‑R, a reasonable basis can suffice (not for transactions contrary to regs or tax shelters).

Examples — Apply the rules to scenarios

1) Circular 230 — Due diligence & credits

FactsAnalysis & outcome
Client claims Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC). Info suggests a dependent may not meet residency requirements. Client insists it’s fine and won’t provide documents. Circular 230 due diligence requires reasonable inquiries and documentation for credits. You must request substantiation. If not provided, you should not claim the credit. Preparer due‑diligence penalties under §6695 may apply per‑failure if you don’t complete the Form 8867 checklist.

2) Who is a preparer?

FactsConclusion
Paid tax professional drafts key schedules used in a client’s return; the firm’s partner signs the return. The drafter is a non‑signing preparer (substantial assistance for compensation). Both signing and non‑signing preparers can be penalized for unreasonable positions.

3) §6694(a) vs. §6694(b)

FactsConclusion
Client wants to deduct an expense with weak support. There are some cases in favor, but most against. You file without disclosure. Without disclosure you need substantial authority. If the support is below that threshold → potential §6694(a) penalty for an unreasonable position. If you knowingly disregard law or recklessly ignore facts → §6694(b).

4) §6695 due‑diligence failure

FactsConclusion
Preparer fails to complete and keep Form 8867 for the American Opportunity Tax Credit (AOTC) and Child Tax Credit (CTC) for several clients. Each failure may trigger a separate §6695 due‑diligence penalty (inflation‑adjusted annually). Keep checklists, worksheets, and documentation.

5) Aiding and abetting (§6701)

FactsConclusion
Consultant (not the signing preparer) provides a memo intentionally designed to understate tax. §6701 can apply to anyone who aids in understatement with knowledge the advice would be used. Applies even when no preparer penalty is asserted.

Tricky Areas (Exam Tips)

  • Disclosure forms Form 8275 (positions not contrary to regs) vs. Form 8275‑R (positions contrary to regs).
  • Standards Don’t mix up thresholds: reasonable basis < substantial authority < MLTN. Only MLTN cures most tax‑shelter issues.
  • Contingent fees Generally prohibited for preparing an original return; limited exceptions (e.g., refund claims or representation in exams where fees depend on results — know exceptions).
  • Client records Return originals promptly even if the client owes you fees (keep copies).
  • Refund checks Never endorse/negotiates client refunds — that’s a §6695 violation.
  • Reliance You may rely in good faith on client info, but make inquiries if it appears incorrect, inconsistent, or incomplete.
One‑minute rule: For undisclosed positions, mentally ask “Do I have substantial authority?” If not, either disclose (and reach reasonable basis) or don’t take the position.

Quick Practice — 2 Multiple‑Choice Questions (MCQs)

  1. Which filing avoids a §6694(a) preparer penalty when support is below substantial authority but above reasonable basis?
    A. Omit the position entirely   B. File Form 8275 with adequate disclosure   C. Rely on client representation   D. Claim “more‑likely‑than‑not” in the workpapers
    Answer: B — Adequate disclosure + reasonable basis generally avoids §6694(a) for nondisallowed positions.
  2. Which of the following is permitted under Circular 230?
    A. Charging contingent fees for an original filed return   B. Relying on obviously inconsistent client info   C. Returning client records on request   D. Endorsing a client’s refund check
    Answer: C — You must return client records promptly upon request.
