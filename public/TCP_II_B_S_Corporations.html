S Corporations

Learning Objectives

  • Identify eligibility & election rules (shareholders, stock, domestic entity, timing) and termination triggers.
  • Compute shareholder basis rollforwards (stock & direct debt) and apply loss limitations (basis → at‑risk → passive).
  • Determine tax effects of distributions using AAA, AEP and OAA ordering.
  • Explain reasonable compensation and 2% shareholder fringe benefit rules.
  • Compute exposure for built‑in gains (BIG) tax and passive investment income (PII) tax when applicable.
  • Differentiate separately stated items vs non‑separately stated ordinary income and their basis effects.

One‑page map

Eligibility → ≤100 shareholders, only individuals/estates/certain trusts, no NRAs, one class of stock, domestic corporation → File Form 2553 Basis rollforward → Beg basis + income (incl. tax‑exempt) − distributions − nondeductible − separately stated losses/deductions Loss order → Basis → At‑Risk → Passive Distribution order (with AEP) → from AAA (tax‑free to basis → capital gain if exceeds) → from AEP (dividend) → return of capital → capital gain Corporate‑level taxes (rare) → Built‑in gains (recognition window) and Passive investment income tax (AEP & PII > 25% of gross receipts) Compensation → Pay reasonable salary to shareholder‑employees; distributions are not subject to SE tax
AAA before AEP Debt basis = direct loans only Reasonable comp!

Tricky‑area tips

  • Debt basis: personal guarantees do not create basis; only direct loans from shareholder to S corp do.
  • When basis is limited, multiple loss/deduction items are allowed pro‑rata.
  • Distributions from AAA do not reduce AAA below zero; excess distributions over basis produce a capital gain.
  • BIG tax applies only up to the built‑in gain at conversion and within the recognition period.
  • PII tax requires both AEP and PII > 25% of gross receipts; sustained for 3 years can terminate S status at the start of year 4.
  • 2% shareholders: certain fringe benefits are taxable W‑2 wages to them but deductible by the S corp.

Illustrative Examples (computations)

1) Stock basis rollforward (includes tax‑exempt income and separately stated deductions)

Begin $50,000 + Ordinary income $40,000 + Tax‑exempt interest $2,000 − Distribution $30,000 − Nondeductible fines $3,000 − Charitable contribution $5,000 = Ending stock basis $54,000.

2) Loss limitation with debt basis

Stock basis $20,000; direct debt basis $10,000; loss $50,000. Allowed loss = $30,000; suspended $20,000 (basis is reduced to zero; debt basis reduced to zero).

3) AAA / AEP distribution ordering

AAA $40,000; AEP $25,000; shareholder basis $35,000; distribution $70,000. From AAA: $40,000 tax‑free to basis until basis $0 → capital gain $5,000. From AEP: $25,000 dividend. Remainder $5,000 = capital gain.

4) Built‑in gains (BIG) tax computation

Built‑in gain at conversion $300,000; asset sold during recognition period; recognized gain $250,000; corporate rate 21%. BIG tax base = min($250,000, $300,000) = $250,000 → tax $52,500.

5) Passive investment income (PII) tax

Gross receipts $800,000; passive investment income $260,000; net passive income $200,000; AEP exists. Excess over 25% = $60,000; tax base = lesser of $200,000 and $60,000 = $60,000 → 21% = $12,600.

6) Debt‑basis repayment gain

Shareholder used $10,000 of debt basis for prior losses. Next year loan is repaid before basis restoration. Repayment triggers $10,000 capital gain to shareholder.

7) Reasonable compensation reminder

If an S‑corp has $150,000 profits and pays its owner‑employee only $10,000 wages with $140,000 distributions, the IRS may reclassify part of the distributions as W‑2 wages to reach a reasonable salary.

Practice Questions

1) Which of the following disqualifies an S‑corporation election?

  1. 95 shareholders
  2. A grantor trust as a shareholder
  3. A nonresident alien individual shareholder
  4. Only one class of common stock outstanding
Answer & Explanation

C — NRAs cannot be S‑corp shareholders.

2) Basis rollforward: Begin basis $50,000; ordinary income $40,000; tax‑exempt interest $2,000; distribution $30,000; nondeductible fines $3,000; charitable contribution $5,000. Ending stock basis?

  1. $59,000
  2. $54,000
  3. $52,000
  4. $56,000
Answer & Explanation

B — Increase for income (incl. tax‑exempt), decrease for distributions, nondeductibles, and separately stated deductions → ending basis = $54,000.

3) Loss limitation: Stock basis $20,000, debt basis $10,000, current‑year loss $50,000. Allowed loss and carryforward?

  1. Allowed $30,000; carry $20,000
  2. Allowed $30,000; carry $20,000
  3. Allowed $20,000; carry $30,000
  4. Allowed $50,000; carry $0
Answer & Explanation

A — Basis limits losses to stock + direct debt basis = $30,000; excess $20,000 carries forward.

4) Which creates debt basis?

  1. Shareholder guarantees a bank loan to the S corp
  2. Shareholder advances cash directly to the S corp under a note
  3. Vendor extends credit to the S corp; shareholder co‑signs
  4. Shareholder pledges personal assets as collateral only
Answer & Explanation

B — Only a bona fide direct loan from shareholder to S corp creates debt basis.

5) Distribution with AAA & AEP: AAA $40,000; AEP $25,000; stock basis $35,000; distribution $70,000. Results?

  1. Tax‑free $35,000, Dividend $25,000, Capital gain $10,000
  2. Tax‑free $40,000, Dividend $0, Capital gain $30,000
  3. Dividend $25,000 first, then tax‑free $35,000, Capital gain $10,000
  4. All dividend $70,000
Answer & Explanation

A — Distribute from AAA first (tax‑free to basis; any excess capital gain), then from AEP as dividend, then any remainder is capital gain.

6) BIG tax: Built‑in gain at conversion $300,000. Asset sold in recognition period recognizes gain $250,000. Corporate‑level tax (21%)?

  1. $0
  2. $52,500
  3. $21,000
  4. $63,000
Answer & Explanation

B — BIG tax applies to the lesser of recognized gain and remaining built‑in gain; 21% × $250,000 = $52,500.

7) PII tax (simplified): Gross receipts $800,000; passive investment income $260,000; net passive income $200,000; AEP exists. 21% tax = ?

  1. $0
  2. $12,600
  3. $42,000
  4. $21,000
Answer & Explanation

B — PII exceeds 25% of gross receipts. Tax base is the lesser of net passive income ($200,000) or excess over 25% ($60,000). Here base $60,000 × 21% = $12,600. (Exact exam formula may specify additional limits.)

8) Which statement about termination is correct?

  1. PII > 25% alone terminates S status immediately
  2. PII > 25% for three consecutive years while AEP exists terminates S status at the start of year 4
  3. Distributions exceeding AAA terminate S status
  4. Having two classes of stock is permitted if voting rights differ
Answer & Explanation

B — The “three‑year” rule plus AEP triggers termination in the fourth year.

9) LIFO recapture on S election is:

  1. Included in the first S year
  2. Included as income in the final C year with tax payable in installments
  3. Ignored if inventory later decreases
  4. Deducted from AAA
Answer & Explanation

B — LIFO recapture is recognized in the last C‑corp year; the resulting tax is generally paid in installments.

10) Reasonable compensation: Which is most accurate?

  1. Distributions can replace all wages if profits are low
  2. Shareholder‑employees must be paid reasonable wages for services; distributions are not subject to SE tax
  3. Wages are optional if distributions are taken
  4. All S‑corp distributions are subject to payroll tax
Answer & Explanation

B — Reasonable salary is required; distributions are not subject to SE tax.

11) Basis‑limited losses with multiple items: Basis $30,000; ordinary loss $15,000; charitable contribution $20,000. How much of each passes through?

  1. Ordinary $12,857; Charity $17,143
  2. Ordinary $15,000; Charity $15,000
  3. Ordinary $10,000; Charity $20,000
  4. Ordinary $0; Charity $30,000
Answer & Explanation

A — Apply basis pro‑rata: total losses $35,000 limited to basis $30,000 ⇒ ordinary $12,857 and charity $17,143; carries: ordinary $2,143, charity $2,857.

12) Debt‑basis repayment: A shareholder used $10,000 of debt basis to deduct losses. Next year the S corp repays the $10,000 loan before income restores basis. Tax result?

  1. No income; basis is negative
  2. Capital gain to the shareholder of $10,000
  3. Ordinary income of $10,000
  4. Dividend of $10,000
Answer & Explanation

B — With no basis restoration, repayment triggers gain to the extent of prior debt‑basis reductions used for losses.
