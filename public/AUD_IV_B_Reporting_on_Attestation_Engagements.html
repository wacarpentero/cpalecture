Reporting on Attestation Engagements

Learning Objectives

  • Differentiate attestation engagement types under the Statements on Standards for Attestation Engagements (SSAE): examination, review, and agreed‑upon procedures (AUP).
  • Identify subject matters (e.g., controls at a service organization, compliance, prospective or pro forma financial information, sustainability/greenhouse gas (GHG) information) and the need for suitable criteria.
  • Apply acceptance, evidence, and reporting requirements for each engagement type, including independence and restricted‑use considerations.
  • Explain differences between assertion‑based and direct reporting, and between Type 1 and Type 2 reports where applicable (for example, SOC reports).

1) What Is an Attestation Engagement?

  • Practitioner reports on a subject matter (or an assertion about it) measured against suitable criteria (relevance, completeness, reliability, neutrality, understandability).
  • Parties: responsible party (prepares/measures), practitioner (independent CPA), and intended users.
  • Reporting can be assertion‑based (responsible party makes an assertion) or direct (practitioner measures/evaluates the subject matter).
Tip: If criteria are not publicly available, ensure they are available to intended users or restrict use.

2) Acceptance & Preconditions

  • Independence in mind and appearance; acceptance only if preconditions are met: suitable criteria, responsible party takes responsibility, subject matter is appropriate, and evidence is expected to be available.
  • Agree on terms in an engagement letter (objective, criteria, responsibilities, type of report, intended use, restrictions).
  • Materiality: defined for examinations and reviews; for AUP, materiality is not used—the specified parties evaluate sufficiency of procedures.

3) Evidence Overview by Engagement Type

Examination

  • Design and perform procedures to obtain reasonable assurance (e.g., tests of controls, substantive procedures, confirmations, observation).
  • Evaluate misstatements/deviations against materiality; consider fraud, noncompliance, and bias.
  • Written representations from the responsible party are required.

Review

  • Perform inquiries and analytical procedures; obtain limited assurance that no material modifications are needed.
  • If results indicate possible material misstatement, perform additional procedures or modify the conclusion.
  • Written representations required; independence required.

Agreed‑upon Procedures (AUP)

  • Procedures are agreed by specified parties; practitioner does not determine sufficiency.
  • Report lists procedures performed and findings—no opinion or conclusion; may include exceptions identified.
  • Distribution is restricted to specified parties; obtain written acknowledgment of suitability of procedures.

4) Reporting Elements

  • Examination (opinion‑based): Title (includes “Independent”), addressees, identification of subject matter and criteria, opinion, basis for opinion, management’s and practitioner’s responsibilities, inherent limitations, and report date/signature.
  • Review (conclusion‑based): Similar structure, but the conclusion uses negative assurance wording.
  • AUP (findings‑based): Identify specified parties, state that sufficiency of procedures is their responsibility, list procedures and findings in detail, disclaim opinion/conclusion, include restricted‑use paragraph.
Example phrases
  • Examination: “In our opinion, management’s assertion that the XYZ controls were effective throughout the period based on the [criteria] is fairly stated…”
  • Review: “Based on our review, we are not aware of any material modifications that should be made…”
  • AUP: “We performed the procedures enumerated below, which were agreed to by [specified parties]. The sufficiency of these procedures is solely the responsibility of those parties…”

5) SOC Reports (Controls at Service Organizations)

  • System and Organization Controls (SOC) reports address controls at service organizations.
  • SOC 1®: Controls relevant to internal control over financial reporting at user entities; typically restricted‑use.
  • SOC 2®: Controls relevant to security, availability, processing integrity, confidentiality, and privacy (Trust Services Criteria); restricted‑use.
  • SOC 3®: General‑use report on Trust Services Criteria—short‑form suitable for public distribution.
  • Type 1 vs Type 2: Type 1 — design of controls at a point in time; Type 2 — design and operating effectiveness over a period.
Tip: If relying on a SOC 1® report, evaluate the period of coverage, subservice organizations, and complementary user‑entity controls.

6) Other Common Attestation Subjects

Compliance Attestations

  • Examination or AUP on an entity’s compliance with specified requirements (laws, regulations, contracts) or on controls over compliance.
  • Often restricted‑use; consider governmental requirements for distribution.

Prospective/Pro Forma & Sustainability

  • Prospective financial information: examination of forecasts/projections or AUP on assumptions and computations.
  • Pro forma financial information: examination or review of the proper application of pro forma adjustments.
  • Sustainability / GHG information: examinations or reviews over metrics and disclosures against suitable criteria.

7) Restricted‑Use, Emphasis, and Other‑Matter

  • Include a restricted‑use paragraph when appropriate (AUP, SOC 1®, SOC 2®, certain compliance attestations).
  • Use emphasis‑of‑matter to highlight matters properly presented/disclosed that are fundamental; use other‑matter for relevant points not presented in the subject matter information.
  • Subsequent events and subsequently discovered facts: consider whether to dual‑date or reissue, similar to audit reporting concepts.

8) Modifications & Withdrawals

  • If unable to obtain sufficient appropriate evidence for an examination, consider a qualified or adverse opinion for misstatement, or a disclaimer/withdrawal for scope limitations.
  • For a review, modify the conclusion or withdraw if pervasive scope limitations exist.
  • In AUP, describe findings, including exceptions; if the procedures cannot be performed as agreed, disclose and consider withdrawing.

9) Documentation & Written Representations

  • Document risk assessment, procedures, evidence, and how criteria were applied; retain copies of the subject matter information.
  • Obtain and retain written representations from the responsible party tailored to the engagement and subject matter.
  • Cross‑reference report wording, restricted‑use language, and any emphasis/other‑matter paragraphs to supporting evidence.

10) Quick Checks

  1. Level of assurance for a review? Limited (negative assurance).
  2. Does an AUP provide assurance? No—only findings are reported; sufficiency of procedures is the specified parties’ responsibility.
  3. Type 1 vs Type 2 SOC? Type 1: design at a point in time; Type 2: design and operating effectiveness over a period.
  4. When is restricted use required? AUP and typically SOC 1®/SOC 2® reports.
  5. What makes criteria “suitable”? Relevant, complete, reliable, neutral, and understandable.
