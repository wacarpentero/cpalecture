Gift Taxation — Compliance & Planning

Learning Objectives

  • Recall allowable gift tax exclusions and deductions (annual exclusion, tuition/medical paid directly, marital & charitable deductions).
  • Explain the unified transfer tax system and how the annual exclusion, marital deduction, and unified credit affect planning.
  • Calculate the amount of taxable gifts for federal gift tax purposes.
  • Identify potential tax savings from gifting noncash property to individuals, including which property to gift to minimize the donor’s future estate.

Big‑picture map — Unified transfer tax

Gross gifts this year – Annual exclusion (per donee, present interests only) – Exclusions (tuition/medical paid directly) – Deductions (marital, charitable) = Taxable gifts (add to prior taxable gifts) → Apply unified credit (shares lifetime with estate tax) → Compute gift tax (rarely due for most exam problems)
Use Form 709 when required Gift‑splitting doubles annual exclusion

Key rules you must know

  • Annual exclusion applies only to present‑interest gifts (Crummey powers make trust gifts present‑interest).
  • Tuition & medical paid directly to provider are excluded (not a taxable gift).
  • Marital deduction: unlimited for U.S. citizen spouse; annual limit for non‑U.S. citizen spouse (exam will provide current amount).
  • Charitable gifts to qualifying organizations are fully deductible (not taxable gifts).
  • 529 “five‑year election”: spread a large contribution ratably over 5 years for annual‑exclusion purposes.
  • Basis to donee: carryover basis (and part of gift tax paid may increase basis); special dual‑basis rule for loss property.

Which property should donors gift?

  • To reduce the donor’s estate growth, gift assets expected to appreciate rapidly.
  • Weigh income‑tax tradeoffs: low‑basis assets shift built‑in gains to donee (carryover basis); high‑basis assets may be better held until death to obtain a step‑up (estate‑tax context).
  • Nonmarketable interests may qualify for valuation discounts (lack of control/marketability) when properly appraised.

Tricky‑area tips (exam‑focused)

  • Annual exclusion is per donee and per donor; with gift‑splitting a married couple can double it.
  • Trust gift without Crummey powers = future interest → no annual exclusion.
  • Write tuition/medical checks to the provider, not the recipient, to keep them excludable.
  • Noncitizen spouse ≠ unlimited marital deduction (use the annual limit given in the question).
  • Remember dual‑basis: if FMV < basis at gift, losses use FMV; gains use carryover basis; sales in the middle band → no gain/loss.

Illustrative Examples (computations)

1) Annual exclusion & gift-splitting

Facts: Married donors give $24,000 each to 4 children in the same year. Annual exclusion = $18,000 per donee per donor; gift-splitting elected. Per donor per child taxable = $24,000 − $18,000 = $6,000 → across 4 children = $24,000 taxable gifts per donor.

2) Tuition/medical exclusion vs cash

Facts: Donor pays $40,000 directly to a hospital for a friend and also gives the friend a $25,000 check. Excludable = $40,000 paid directly. Taxable gift = $25,000 − $18,000 = $7,000.

3) 529 plan five-year election

Facts: Single donor contributes $90,000 to a 529 plan and elects 5-year spread. Deemed gifts = $18,000 per year for five years. If the donor makes no other gifts to that beneficiary, taxable gift in Year 1 = $0.

4) Marital deduction — non-U.S. citizen spouse

Facts: U.S. donor gives a noncitizen spouse artwork worth $300,000. Annual limit given as $185,000. Taxable gift = $300,000 − $185,000 = $115,000 (no unlimited marital deduction).

5) Compute taxable gifts across multiple donees

Facts: Donor gives $50,000 cash to A, $12,000 cash to B, and transfers marketable securities FMV $40,000 to C. No gift-splitting. Taxable gifts = (A: 50,000 − 18,000) + (B: 12,000 − 12,000) + (C: 40,000 − 18,000) = $54,000.

6) Basis to donee — gain scenario

Facts: Gifted stock basis $20,000; FMV at gift $70,000; donee sells later for $90,000. Carryover basis applies → gain = 90,000 − 20,000 = $70,000.

7) Dual-basis rule — loss scenario

Facts: Gifted property basis $50,000; FMV at gift $35,000; donee sells for $30,000. Loss basis = FMV at gift → loss = 30,000 − 35,000 = ($5,000).

8) Unified computation — tying it together

Facts: Donor’s current-year taxable gifts computed above total $54,000. Prior taxable gifts = $200,000. Cumulative taxable gifts = $254,000. Gift tax (if any) is offset by the unified credit; the running total reduces credit available at death (exam provides current credit).

Practice Questions

1) You and your spouse make gifts of $180,000 split equally to your three children. Annual exclusion = $18,000 per donee per donor. Your taxable gifts this year (for you)?

  1. $0
  2. $36,000
  3. $30,000
  4. $90,000
Answer & Explanation

B — With gift‑splitting, you report half to each child: $30,000. Subtract $18,000 per child → taxable per child = $12,000; across 3 children = $36,000.

2) Grandparent pays $50,000 directly to a university for a grandchild and gives the grandchild $30,000 by check. Taxable gift?

  1. $0
  2. $12,000
  3. $30,000
  4. $80,000
Answer & Explanation

B — Direct tuition is excluded; only the cash to the student counts. Taxable = $30,000 − annual exclusion $18,000 = $12,000.

3) Donor contributes $40,000 to an irrevocable trust with no withdrawal rights. Annual exclusion?

  1. $40,000
  2. $18,000
  3. $0
  4. $22,000
Answer & Explanation

A — It’s a future‑interest gift; annual exclusion doesn’t apply. Entire $40,000 is a taxable gift (subject to unified credit).

4) 529 plan contribution of $90,000 with the 5‑year election. Annual exclusion = $18,000. Taxable gift in Year 1?

  1. $0
  2. $72,000
  3. $0
  4. $18,000
Answer & Explanation

A — Election spreads the gift as $18,000 per year for 5 years. With an exclusion of $18,000, taxable in Year 1 = $0 (zero when equal).

5) Gift of $1,200,000 to a non‑U.S. citizen spouse. With an annual limit of $185,000, taxable gift?

  1. $0
  2. $185,000
  3. $1,015,000
  4. $1,200,000
Answer & Explanation

C — No unlimited marital deduction; taxable = $1,200,000 − $185,000 = $1,015,000.

6) In one year, donor contributes $200,000 of appreciated stock to a public charity and $60,000 cash to a sibling. Taxable gifts?

  1. $0
  2. $42,000
  3. $60,000
  4. $260,000
Answer & Explanation

B — Charity is deductible (not a taxable gift). Only the family gift is a taxable gift after the annual exclusion: $60,000 − $18,000 = $42,000.

7) Donor gives stock (basis $20,000, FMV $70,000). Donee later sells for $90,000. Recognized gain?

  1. $0
  2. $70,000
  3. $20,000
  4. $50,000
Answer & Explanation

B — FMV ≥ basis at gift → carryover basis = $20,000. Gain = $90,000 − $20,000 = $70,000.

8) Gift loss property (basis $50,000, FMV $35,000). Donee sells for $40,000. Gain/loss?

  1. $0
  2. $20,000
  3. Loss $10,000
  4. Gain $5,000
Answer & Explanation

A — Dual‑basis rule: sale between $35,000 and $50,000 → no gain or loss.

9) Prior taxable gifts total $600,000; this year adds $300,000 of taxable gifts. Which statement is most accurate?

  1. Gift tax due equals tax on $900,000
  2. Unified credit offsets gift tax; cumulative taxable gifts affect how much credit remains for estate tax
  3. Annual exclusion increases with prior gifts
  4. Charitable deductions reduce prior years’ totals
Answer & Explanation

B — Gift tax uses a unified credit shared with estate tax; cumulative taxable gifts reduce remaining credit available later.

10) You gift 10% FLP interest when the underlying FMV is $1,000,000; a 25% valuation discount applies. Taxable gift this year (ignore appraisal costs)?

  1. $100,000
  2. $57,000
  3. $75,000
  4. $82,000
Answer & Explanation

B — Discounted value = $75,000; after annual exclusion $18,000 → taxable = $57,000.
