Financial Statement Ratios & Performance Metrics

Learning Objectives

  • Compute and interpret liquidity, activity (efficiency), profitability, leverage/coverage, and cash‑flow metrics.
  • Apply the cash conversion cycle and DuPont analysis to connect operating drivers to return on equity.
  • Use trend and common‑size analysis to evaluate performance over time and versus peers.

1) Liquidity

  • Current ratio and quick ratio gauge short‑term solvency; trends and peer comparison matter more than a single threshold.
  • Quality of current assets is key: large receivables or slow‑moving inventory can inflate the current ratio but not cash.

2) Activity (Efficiency)

  • Turnovers convert into days to make operating timing intuitive (for example, Days sales outstanding).
  • The cash conversion cycle = Days sales outstanding + Days inventory on hand − Days payable outstanding.

3) Profitability

  • Margins (gross, operating, net) show profitability at different stages of the income statement.
  • Return on assets and return on equity relate profit to invested capital; DuPont links margin, turnover, and leverage.

4) Leverage & Coverage

  • Debt measures (debt‑to‑equity, debt‑to‑assets) show financial risk; coverage ratios (interest coverage) show capacity to service obligations.
  • Cash‑based metrics (operating cash flow to debt) complement accrual measures.

Quick Checks

  1. What makes the quick ratio “quick”? Inventory and prepaid items are excluded.
  2. Does a higher cash conversion cycle mean better liquidity? No — lower is better (cash is tied up fewer days).
  3. Three DuPont drivers? Net margin, Asset turnover, Equity multiplier.
  4. Why pair accrual coverage with a cash‑based metric? Cash confirms capacity to pay.
