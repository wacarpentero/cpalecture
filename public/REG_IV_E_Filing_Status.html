Filing Status

Acronyms: MFJ = married filing jointly; MFS = married filing separately; HOH = head of household; QSS (also “QW”) = qualifying surviving spouse; QC = qualifying child; QR = qualifying relative; AGI = adjusted gross income.

Learning Objectives

  • Recall the filing status options and who qualifies for each.
  • Recall dependent relationship rules that affect filing status (QC/QR and “qualifying person” for HOH).
  • Identify the correct status in a given scenario (including “considered unmarried,” QSS, and tie‑breakers).

At‑a‑Glance — Filing Statuses

StatusWho qualifiesKey points / traps
Single Unmarried (or legally separated) on last day of year and not eligible for HOH or QSS. Status is determined on December 31. If spouse dies during the year and you don’t remarry, you are still considered married for that year (usually MFJ/MFS).
MFJ Married on last day of year and elect to file jointly. Joint/several liability. If spouse dies during the year, surviving spouse may still file MFJ for that year.
MFS Married but choose to file separately. Often ineligible for certain credits/deductions. Consider “considered unmarried” test for HOH if living apart last 6 months and paying > ½ cost of home for child.
HOH Unmarried (or considered unmarried) and pay > ½ cost of keeping up a home for a qualifying person. Qualifying person includes QC; can include a dependent parent even if parent does not live with the taxpayer (taxpayer pays > ½ cost of parent’s principal home).
QSS Spouse died in prior 2 years; taxpayer has a dependent child and maintains the home. Available for up to 2 tax years following the year of death, if not remarried and maintaining household for a dependent child.
Exam tip: Filing status follows a quick order: MFJ/MFS if married at year‑end → else test QSS → else test HOH → else Single.

Dependents Refresher (Why it matters)

Qualifying Child (QC)

  • Relationship: child/stepchild/foster/sibling/descendant.
  • Age: under age threshold, or student, or disabled (exam provides exact thresholds).
  • Residency: lives with taxpayer > ½ year (exceptions for school, illness, etc.).
  • Support: child did not provide > ½ of own support.
  • Tie‑breaker rules apply if multiple taxpayers can claim the same child (see below).

Qualifying Relative (QR)

  • Not a QC of any taxpayer.
  • Relationship test (or lives with the taxpayer all year).
  • Gross income test (exam will provide the threshold).
  • Taxpayer provides > ½ of the person’s support (multiple support agreements possible).
HOH qualifying person: QC (even if not a dependent due only to a multiple support agreement exception) or a dependent parent who does not need to live with the taxpayer.

Representative Tasks → Worked Examples

1) Determine HOH when supporting a parent

FactsAnalysis
Taxpayer is unmarried and pays $12,000 of a parent’s $20,000 annual living costs at a nursing home. Parent is a dependent. Parent is a qualifying person for HOH even if not living with the taxpayer. Since taxpayer pays > ½ of the cost ($12,000 > $10,000), status = HOH.

2) “Considered unmarried” (abandoned spouse) → HOH

FactsAnalysis
Married but lived apart from spouse the last 6 months. Pays $14,400 of home costs out of $24,000 total; child lived with taxpayer all year. Meets “considered unmarried” test (lived apart last 6 months and maintains the home for the child with > ½ costs). Status = HOH, not MFS.

3) QSS vs MFJ

FactsAnalysis
Spouse died this year; survivor did not remarry and has a dependent child. For the year of death, survivor may file MFJ. For each of the next 2 years, if still maintaining a household for the dependent child, status = QSS.

4) Tie‑breaker — two parents claim the same QC

FactsResult
Child lived with both divorced parents exactly 6 months each. Parent A AGI $60,000; Parent B AGI $58,000. Residency is the same → parent with higher AGI wins tie‑breaker. Parent A claims QC; filing status consequences follow (e.g., HOH eligibility).

Diagnostics — What to Check

  • Year‑end status Marital status is tested on December 31.
  • Qualifying person Parent can qualify you for HOH without living with you; others generally must live with you.
  • Considered unmarried Lived apart last 6 months + > ½ cost of home for child.
  • QSS window Two tax years after year of death if maintaining a household for a dependent child.
  • Tie‑breakers Parent over non‑parent; longer residency; higher AGI as the final tie‑breaker.

Practice Questions

  1. Parent in nursing home: Kim (unmarried) pays $15,000 of her mother’s $27,000 nursing‑home costs. Mother is Kim’s dependent. Filing status?
    Show answer
    HOH. Parent is a qualifying person for HOH even if not living with the taxpayer; Kim pays > ½ of the cost ($15,000 of $27,000).
  2. Considered unmarried: Alex (married) lived apart from spouse July–December and paid $18,600 of $30,000 home costs for a child who lived with Alex all year. Filing status?
    Show answer
    HOH. Lived apart last 6 months and paid > ½ the cost ⇒ considered unmarried for HOH.
  3. Year of death: Jamie’s spouse died in March; Jamie did not remarry and has a dependent child. Best filing status for the current year?
    Show answer
    MFJ is available for the year of death (or MFS). QSS applies to each of the next 2 years if conditions are met.
  4. AGI tie‑breaker: Child lived with Pat and Sam equally; Pat AGI $72,000, Sam AGI $75,000. Who claims the child?
    Show answer
    The parent with higher AGI, Sam ($75,000), claims the child. That may enable HOH for Sam.
  5. Multiple support: Three siblings support their grandmother: Ann $8,000, Ben $7,000, Cara $6,000 of total support $18,000. A multiple‑support agreement gives Ann the dependency claim. Filing status for Ann?
    Show answer
    HOH is allowed only if the dependent is a qualifying person. A dependent parent is a qualifying person even if not living with the taxpayer, so Ann qualifies.
  6. MFS restriction: Taylor files MFS and lived with spouse all year; Taylor has a child who lived with them all year and paid $14,000 of $26,000 home costs. Filing status?
    Show answer
    MFS. Taylor is not “considered unmarried” (lived with spouse), so cannot claim HOH.
