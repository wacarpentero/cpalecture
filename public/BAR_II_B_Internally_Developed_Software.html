Internally Developed Software

Learning Objectives

  • Recall criteria to capitalize software costs for internal‑use (ASC 350‑40) and for software to be sold/leased/marketed (ASC 985‑20 concepts).
  • Compute capitalized amounts, subsequent amortization, and journal entries.

Internal‑Use Software (ASC 350‑40)

Stages & accounting

StageExamplesAccounting
Preliminary project Conceptual formulation, alternatives, vendor selection Expense
Application development Coding, configuration, installation, testing Capitalize direct costs (payroll, external fees) and eligible interest
Post‑implementation/operations Training, maintenance, minor bug fixes Expense
  • Start capitalizing when management authorizes funding and it’s probable the project will be completed for intended use.
  • Amortize capitalized costs on a systematic basis over the shorter of useful life or obsolescence period (often straight‑line).
  • Impairment: test as a long‑lived asset group (ASC 360) when indicators exist.

Hosting (Cloud) arrangements

  • If the customer has a license to the software that it can control (possess), capitalize the license (intangible) and apply 350‑40 to implementation costs.
  • If it’s a service contract (no license), expense fees as incurred; capitalize certain implementation costs and amortize over the term of the hosting arrangement.

Typical JE

  • Dr Software in progress / Cr Cash or AP
  • Upon ready for use: Reclass to Software (intangible) and begin amortization.
Illustration — Capitalize internal‑use costs & amortize

Setup

Application‑dev payroll$180,000
External coding$70,000
Training costs$24,000 (post‑implementation)
Useful life4 years, straight‑line; ready for use on 7/1/Y1

Compute

ExpenseTraining $24,000

Software to be Sold/Leased/Marketed (ASC 985‑20 concepts)

Key points

  • Expense costs until technological feasibility (TF).
  • After TF and before product release, capitalize coding, testing, and product masters.
  • Amortize the capitalized balance by the greater of:
    • SL over estimated economic life, or
    • Revenue ratio: current revenue ÷ (current + expected future revenue)
  • Impairment/NRV: write down to the lower of unamortized cost or net realizable value.

Technological feasibility (examples)

  • Completion of a detailed program design, or
  • Working model that proves the design will meet specs.

Typical JE

  • Dr Capitalized software costs / Cr Cash (post‑TF)
  • Dr Amortization expense / Cr Accumulated amortization (periodic)
Illustration — Revenue‑ratio amortization

Setup

Capitalized cost at release$300,000
Current‑year revenue$240,000
Expected future revenue$560,000

Compute

Revenue ratio$240,000 ÷ ($240,000 + $560,000) = 30%
Amortization30% × $300,000 = $90,000
Carrying amount end of year$300,000 − $90,000 = $210,000
JE: Dr Amortization exp $90,000; Cr Accum amortization $90,000
Illustration — NRV test

Setup

Unamortized cost$210,000
NRV (future revenue − costs)$190,000

Compute

Write‑down$210,000 − $190,000 = $20,000
JE: Dr Loss $20,000; Cr Capitalized software $20,000

Practice Questions

1. Stage classification

Training costs for a new internal system should be:

  1. Capitalized during application development
  2. Expensed (post‑implementation)
  3. Capitalized and amortized over hosting term
  4. Capitalized only if material
Answer & Rationale

Answer: B. Training is post‑implementation and expensed.

2. Capitalization start

Capitalization of internal‑use software begins when:

  1. Preliminary project stage starts
  2. Management authorizes funding and completion is probable
  3. User training commences
  4. License agreement is signed, regardless of probability
Answer & Rationale

Answer: B. That’s the 350‑40 trigger.

3. Revenue‑ratio

Capitalized software to be sold is $500,000. Year 1 revenue is $250,000; future expected revenue is $750,000. Amortization for Year 1?

  1. $100,000
  2. $125,000
  3. $250,000
  4. $375,000
Answer & Rationale

Answer: B. Ratio = 250/(250+750)=25% → 25%×$500,000=$125,000.

4. Cloud hosting

A hosting arrangement without a software license is a service contract. Certain implementation costs are:

  1. Capitalized and amortized over the hosting term
  2. Expensed as incurred without exception
  3. Capitalized until technological feasibility
  4. Capitalized and amortized only if SL exceeds revenue ratio
Answer & Rationale

Answer: A. Capitalize qualifying implementation costs; amortize over the term.

5. NRV test

Unamortized capitalized cost is $140,000; NRV is $135,000. Required action:

  1. No entry
  2. Write down $5,000
  3. Write down $135,000
  4. Increase amortization to $140,000
Answer & Rationale

Answer: B. Lower of cost or NRV.
