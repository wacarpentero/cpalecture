Misstatements and Internal Control Deficiencies

Learning Objectives

  • Classify misstatements (factual, projected, and judgmental), accumulate them, and evaluate their materiality individually and in aggregate.
  • Apply qualitative factors (e.g., fraud indicators, covenant effects, trends, compliance) when evaluating whether misstatements are material.
  • Distinguish control deficiency, significant deficiency, and material weakness; determine severity using likelihood and magnitude.
  • Communicate misstatements and control deficiencies to management and those charged with governance (TCWG) appropriately and on time.
  • Document conclusions and the effect on the auditor’s report; decide whether additional procedures are needed based on the risk of material misstatement (RMM).

1) Types of Misstatements & Accumulation

  • Factual: Clear error (e.g., mis‑posting, math error).
  • Projected: From sampling results; project to the population and add an allowance for sampling risk.
  • Judgmental: Differences arising from estimates or policies (e.g., allowance method, useful lives) including indications of management bias.
  • Accumulate all misstatements identified (except clearly trivial per your posting threshold) and track corrected vs uncorrected throughout the audit.
Tip: Keep a running summary of uncorrected misstatements with columns for type, account, assertion, amount (current and prior‑period effects), and qualitative notes.

2) Evaluating Materiality — Quantitative & Qualitative

  • Compare the aggregate of uncorrected misstatements to overall materiality and performance materiality.
  • Consider qualitative factors: changes earnings trends, compliance with covenants/regulation, fraud indicators, management compensation targets, and the nature of the misstatement (e.g., concealment).
  • Consider pervasiveness (multiple elements or the financial statements as a whole) and whether disclosures are adequate.
  • Year‑end vs rollover effects: evaluate both the period‑end effect and the effect on current‑period performance (often called “iron curtain” and “rollover” approaches).
Do not ignore: Small intentional misstatements or bias patterns can be material qualitatively.

3) Dealing with Misstatements

Corrections

  • Request timely adjustments for all material misstatements and for immaterial misstatements that may become material when aggregated.
  • If management refuses to correct, evaluate the effect on the opinion and consider communications to TCWG.
  • Re‑perform analytics and other procedures if corrections change relationships or trends.

Uncorrected Misstatements

  • Assess whether uncorrected items are material individually or in aggregate.
  • Consider the effect on key ratios, compliance, and disclosures.
  • Obtain a management representation that uncorrected misstatements are immaterial, both individually and in aggregate.

4) Internal Control Deficiencies — Definitions & Severity

  • Control deficiency: A deficiency in design or operation such that a control cannot prevent or detect and correct misstatements on a timely basis.
  • Significant deficiency: Less severe than a material weakness yet important enough to merit attention by those charged with governance (TCWG).
  • Material weakness: A deficiency, or combination of deficiencies, such that there is a reasonable possibility that a material misstatement of the financial statements will not be prevented, or detected and corrected, on a timely basis.
  • Severity assessment: Evaluate likelihood (remote, possible, probable) and magnitude (could the control failure result in a material misstatement?) including compensating controls.
Common indicators of a material weakness
  • Restatement of previously issued financial statements due to error.
  • Auditor detected a material misstatement that existed and was not prevented/detected by internal controls.
  • Ineffective oversight by TCWG (e.g., audit committee).

5) Communication Requirements

  • Control deficiencies: Communicate in writing to management and those charged with governance (TCWG) by the report date; earlier if appropriate.
  • Material weaknesses: Communicate in writing and describe the deficiency, its effect, and the fact that the auditor’s purpose was not to express an opinion on internal control.
  • Misstatements: Communicate corrected and uncorrected misstatements and their potential effect; ask management to correct where appropriate.
  • Maintain professional skepticism—follow up when management asserts remediation late in the audit.
Practice: Include criteria, condition, cause, and effect in deficiency communications, with recommended remediation (responsible party and timeline).

6) Remediation & Follow‑up

  • When management remediates a deficiency before year‑end, test the design and operating effectiveness of the new/modified control for a sufficient period.
  • If remediation occurs after the balance‑sheet date but before the report date, it may not change the severity assessment for the current audit period.
  • Consider the implications for risk assessment and planned procedures in the current and next audit.

7) Effects on the Auditor’s Report

  • If uncorrected misstatements are material but not pervasive → qualified opinion (except for) for the financial statements.
  • If pervasive → adverse opinion.
  • A material weakness in internal control does not, by itself, require a modified opinion on the financial statements (unless it leads to a material misstatement) but would be a basis for an adverse opinion in a separate internal control report if engaged to report on internal control.
  • Consider emphasis‑of‑matter or other‑matter paragraphs when relevant (e.g., restatement, significant uncertainty).

8) Documentation

  • All misstatements identified (corrected and uncorrected), the basis for materiality judgments, and the conclusions reached.
  • Deficiency descriptions, severity evaluation, basis for likelihood/magnitude, and communications sent (with dates and recipients).
  • Impact on planned procedures, additional work performed, and final overall conclusion.

9) Quick Checks

  1. Three misstatement categories? Factual, projected, judgmental.
  2. Key determinants of deficiency severity? Likelihood and magnitude.
  3. Indicator that often signals a material weakness? A restatement due to error or an auditor‑detected material misstatement.
  4. Does a material weakness always require a modified opinion on the financial statements? No—only if it results in a material misstatement that is not corrected.
  5. Who must receive written communication of significant deficiencies and material weaknesses? Those charged with governance.
