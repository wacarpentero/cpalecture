Federal Laws & Regulations

Learning Objectives

  • Summarize key rules for employment taxes from both the employer and employee perspective (withholding, FICA, FUTA, self‑employment, information returns).
  • Explain the framework for qualified health plans under the Affordable Care Act (ACA): employer mandate, affordability/minimum value, premium tax credit interaction, and reporting.
  • Recall factors used to determine worker classification (employee vs. independent contractor) and the consequences of misclassification.
  • Recall bankruptcy chapters and discharge basics.
  • Summarize the Foreign Corrupt Practices Act (FCPA) anti‑bribery and books‑and‑records/internal controls provisions.
  • Identify compliance issues and the correct remedy given a scenario.

At a Glance

Employment taxes — quick map

TopicKey points
FICA (Social Security & Medicare)Shared by employer and employee; employer withholds employee share and remits both shares. Additional Medicare tax may apply to high wages (employee only).
FUTAEmployer‑paid unemployment tax; credit coordination with state unemployment taxes.
Withholding & depositsForm W‑4 drives withholding; deposit schedules (monthly/semimonthly) depend on prior liability; quarterly reconciliation on Form 941 (or annual 944 for small employers).
Information returnsForm W‑2 for employees; Form 1099‑NEC for nonemployee compensation; backup withholding may apply if payee fails to furnish a correct TIN on Form W‑9.
Self‑employment taxApplies to net earnings from self‑employment; computed on individual return; taxpayer may deduct an employer‑equivalent portion in arriving at AGI.

ACA — employer mandate snapshot

ItemKey points
Applicable Large Employer (ALE)Generally ≥ 50 full‑time equivalents. Aggregation rules may apply to related employers.
Offer of coverageALE must offer minimum essential coverage to substantially all full‑time employees and dependents or risk a potential employer shared responsibility payment (ESRP).
Affordability & minimum valueCoverage must meet an IRS indexed affordability threshold and provide minimum value. If not, an employee may qualify for a premium tax credit, potentially triggering an ESRP.
ReportingInformation reporting to the IRS/employees (Forms 1094/1095) documents offer and coverage.
Exam tip: When a payroll/ACA problem appears, line up the steps: Who’s the worker? → What forms? → Who withholds/pays? → Is the employer an ALE and was affordable MV coverage offered?

Anti‑bribery — FCPA essentials

Provisions

AreaKey points
Anti‑briberyProhibits giving anything of value to foreign officials to obtain/retain business. “Foreign official” broadly defined; includes state‑owned enterprises. Narrow exception for bona fide, reasonable promotional expenses; facilitating payments are limited and risky.
Books & recordsIssuers must keep accurate records and reasonably designed internal controls. Liability can arise without bribery (books/controls violations).
Who’s covered?Issuers, domestic concerns (US persons/companies), and anyone acting within US territory. Third‑party intermediary payments are covered.
PenaltiesSevere criminal and civil penalties for entities and individuals; debarment risks; disgorgement.

Red flags

  • Intermediaries High‑risk agents/consultants; vague services; requests for cash/off‑books payments.
  • Government touchpoints Licenses, customs, procurement, state‑owned customers.
  • Controls gaps No due diligence, missing approvals, weak travel/expense policies.
Exam tip: Focus on intent and anything of value to a foreign official for business advantage. Books‑and‑records are separate bases for liability.

Examples — Apply to scenarios

ScenarioAnswer
A 60‑FTE company offers no coverage. Several full‑time employees receive marketplace premium tax credits. Likely an ALE; potential employer shared responsibility payment may apply for failure to offer minimum essential coverage.
Company pays a “consultant” who funnels money to a foreign customs official to speed shipments. FCPA anti‑bribery risk: third‑party payments with business intent. Books/records liability if misclassified.
Software firm controls daily work, requires reports, and provides benefits to a developer labeled as “contractor.” Likely employee under common‑law factors → W‑2, employer FICA/FUTA; misclassification exposure.
Independent designer paid without a TIN on file. Backup withholding may apply; issue Form 1099‑NEC; obtain Form W‑9.

Tricky Areas (Exam Tips)

  • Employee vs contractor If the business directs the manner and means, it’s usually an employee.
  • ACA penalties Two flavors are tested: failure to offer coverage to substantially all FT employees, and failure to offer affordable MV coverage to a particular FT employee who gets a credit.
  • Forms Employee=W‑2; contractor=1099‑NEC; withholding=Form 941; W‑4 for employees; W‑9 for contractors.
  • FCPA Intent + something of value + foreign official + business purpose. Books‑and‑records can stand alone.
  • Bankruptcy Know chapter purposes and major discharge exceptions only.

Quick Practice — 5 Multiple‑Choice Questions (MCQs)

  1. Which employer is potentially subject to an ACA employer shared responsibility payment?
    A. An employer with 25 FTEs that does not offer coverage   B. An employer with ~60 FTEs that does not offer coverage and has full‑time employees who receive premium tax credits   C. Any employer that fails to provide dental insurance   D. A self‑employed individual
    Show answer
    B — Applicable Large Employers (generally ≥ 50 FTEs) must offer coverage or potentially owe an ESRP when FT employees receive premium tax credits.
  2. Which form is typically used to report nonemployee compensation?
    A. Form W‑2   B. Form 1099‑NEC   C. Form 941   D. Form 1095‑C
    Show answer
    B — 1099‑NEC reports nonemployee compensation; W‑2 is for employees; 941 is quarterly payroll tax return; 1095‑C reports offers of coverage by ALEs.
  3. Which factor best supports contractor status?
    A. Employer controls work hours and methods   B. Worker supplies own tools and can realize profit/loss   C. Worker gets benefits   D. Work is integral to the business
    Show answer
    B — Significant investment and risk of loss point toward independent contractor status.
  4. A U.S. issuer records a bribe as a “consulting fee” in its books. Which law is implicated even if bribery is not proven?
    A. Only anti‑bribery   B. Books‑and‑records/internal controls provisions of the FCPA   C. GLBA   D. The UK Bribery Act
    Show answer
    B — Inaccurate books or inadequate internal controls can independently violate the FCPA.
  5. FUTA is generally:
    A. Paid by employees   B. Paid by employers   C. Split equally   D. Paid only by self‑employed individuals
    Show answer
    B — FUTA is an employer‑paid tax (subject to credits for state unemployment taxes).
