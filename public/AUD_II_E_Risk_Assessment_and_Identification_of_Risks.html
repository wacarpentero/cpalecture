Risk Assessment & Identification of Risks of Material Misstatement

Learning Objectives

  • Identify and assess risks of material misstatement (RMM) at the financial statement and assertion levels.
  • Determine relevant assertions for significant classes of transactions, account balances, and disclosures.
  • Evaluate inherent risk factors (complexity, subjectivity, change, uncertainty, susceptibility to fraud) and control risk based on design & implementation.
  • Identify significant risks (including fraud risks) and document the linkage to further audit procedures.

At a Glance

TermMeaning (plain English)
Inherent riskHow likely an assertion could be misstated before considering controls.
Control riskRisk that a misstatement won’t be prevented/detected/corrected by controls.
RMMInherent × Control risk, assessed at FS and assertion levels.
Relevant assertionAn assertion with a meaningful chance of material misstatement (existence, completeness, rights/obligations, accuracy/valuation, presentation & disclosure).
Significant riskA risk that requires special audit consideration (often fraud, complex estimates, or unusual transactions).
ITGC vs App controlsIT general controls support the system overall (access, change mgmt, ops); application controls operate at the process/transaction level.

1) Risk Assessment Overview

  • Perform risk assessment procedures: inquiries, analytical procedures, observation/inspection, and walkthroughs.
  • Assess risks at the FS level (e.g., going concern, management integrity, pervasive IT deficiencies) and at the assertion level.
  • Consider inherent risk factors: complexity, subjectivity, change, uncertainty, susceptibility to fraud, and estimation uncertainty.
Tip: Document why each risk matters and which assertions it affects—this drives procedures in II.D.

2) Determining Relevant Assertions

By Account/Disclosure

  • Revenue: occurrence, cutoff, accuracy, presentation.
  • Receivables: existence, valuation, rights, presentation.
  • Inventory: existence, completeness, valuation, rights/obligations.
  • Disclosures: completeness and accuracy of required detail.

Simple Mapping Method

  • Identify what could go wrong (WCGW) for each class of transactions/balance.
  • Map WCGWs to assertions; mark realistic material risks as relevant assertions.
  • Link each relevant assertion to planned procedures (controls/substantive).

3) Understanding Internal Control & IT

  • Evaluate design and confirm implementation of controls relevant to the audit.
  • ITGCs: access security, change management, and computer operations.
  • Application controls: input/processing/output controls in processes.
  • Identify IT risks: unmanaged changes, weak access, reliance on untested reports, EUC spreadsheets.
Walkthrough note: Trace a transaction through the system (including IT) to confirm controls exist and are implemented.

4) Identifying Significant Risks (incl. Fraud)

  • Presume a risk of fraud in revenue recognition unless rebutted.
  • Consider management override: journal entries, estimates, unusual transactions.
  • Flag significant risks with high likelihood/magnitude or complexity (e.g., Level 3 fair value, related parties, one‑off transactions near period‑end).
  • Plan more persuasive evidence and consider specialists where appropriate.
Mini‑example

A restructuring triggers impairment testing and one‑time gains: mark valuation assertions as significant; plan independent expectation, specialist involvement, and targeted journal entry testing.

5) Assessing RMM & Setting the Foundation for Responses

  • Assess RMM at assertion level; decide on tests of controls vs substantive procedures.
  • For significant risks, design focused procedures and increase unpredictability.
  • Consider FS‑level responses when pervasive risks exist (e.g., more experienced staff, added supervision, added consultation).
Common pitfall: Listing risks without clear link to assertions and procedures. Build a simple Risk → Assertion → Procedure table.

6) Documentation

  • Identified risks and the basis (analytics, inquiries, walkthroughs).
  • Relevant assertions and WCGWs by account/disclosure.
  • Design & implementation of key controls, IT considerations, and significant risk designations.
  • Explicit linkage to planned procedures (to be covered in II.D).

7) Quick Checks

  1. What is a relevant assertion? An assertion with a realistic chance of material misstatement.
  2. Does identifying a control make it effective? No—assess design and confirm implementation.
  3. Presumed fraud risk? Revenue recognition and management override.
  4. Where do ITGCs fit? They support automated controls and system‑generated evidence.
