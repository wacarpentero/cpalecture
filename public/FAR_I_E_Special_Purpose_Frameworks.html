Special Purpose Frameworks

Learning Objectives

  • Identify common special purpose frameworks (SPFs) also known as other comprehensive bases of accounting (OCBOA): cash basis, modified cash basis, income tax basis, regulatory basis, and contractual basis.
  • Recognize typical statement titles, recognition principles, and disclosures for each framework.
  • Apply conversions and disclosures when moving between U.S. Generally Accepted Accounting Principles (U.S. GAAP) and an SPF.

At a Glance

Cash basis Statement of Cash Receipts and Disbursements;
Statement of Assets and Liabilities—cash basis (optional) 		Recognize revenues when received in cash and expenses when paid. No accruals. Accounting policies; limitations of cash basis; significant noncash events disclosed.
Modified cash basis Cash receipts/disbursements with specified modifications (e.g., capitalize long‑lived assets, accrue taxes payable). Cash basis plus clearly stated, consistent modifications. Describe each modification and measurement method.
Income tax basis Statement of Assets, Liabilities, and Equity—income tax basis;
Statement of Revenues and Expenses—income tax basis		 Follow the entity’s tax accounting: depreciation methods/lives, recognition of revenue and expense per the tax return; no deferred taxes. Policy for significant tax departures from U.S. GAAP; reconciliation of retained earnings to tax return if helpful.
Regulatory basis Statements prescribed by a regulator (e.g., insurance statutory statements, utility commission reports). Recognition/presentation per regulator; may diverge from U.S. GAAP. Identify the regulatory body and departures from U.S. GAAP; limitations on use if required.
Contractual basis Statements required by a contract or agreement (e.g., lender covenant schedules). Recognition as stipulated by the agreement. Describe the contractual basis and intended users; restriction on use may apply.

1) Presentation & Titles

  • Each statement’s title should identify the basis of accounting (e.g., “Statement of Revenues and Expenses — Income Tax Basis”).
  • Notes must describe the framework and any modifications (for modified cash).
  • Comparative statements are allowed; ensure consistency of the basis across periods.

2) Recognition Highlights

  • Cash basis: No receivables/payables, no inventory capitalization, no depreciation; disclose significant commitments and noncash activities.
  • Modified cash: Choose common modifications (capitalize and depreciate property, plant, and equipment; accrue payroll taxes payable; carry debt with amortization). Disclose each modification.
  • Income tax basis: Use tax depreciation/amortization; recognize revenues/expenses per tax laws (for example, completed contract vs. percentage‑of‑completion for eligible contractors). No deferred tax assets/liabilities.
  • Regulatory/contractual: Follow the required rules; disclose departures from U.S. GAAP and any restricted‑use intent.

3) Disclosures Common to SPFs

  • Summary of significant accounting policies, including basis of accounting and modifications (if any).
  • Narrative of significant estimates and risks, related‑party transactions, and subsequent events.
  • Explanation of how the basis affects comparability to U.S. GAAP statements.
Exam cue: An Emphasis‑of‑Matter paragraph is required in the auditor’s report to describe the special purpose framework; some bases (contractual, certain regulatory) may include restricted use wording.

Quick Checks

  1. Does income tax basis present deferred taxes? No.
  2. What must a modified cash basis report include? Clear description of each modification and the measurement method.
  3. Must SPF statement titles name the basis? Yes — always identify the basis in the title.
  4. Cash basis effect on capital assets? No capitalization (disclose significant noncash events instead).
