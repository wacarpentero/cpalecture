Materiality

Learning Objectives

  • Define and apply overall materiality, performance materiality, and tolerable misstatement (and clearly trivial threshold).
  • Set materiality considering quantitative benchmarks and qualitative factors.
  • Revise materiality as the audit progresses and link it to the evaluation of misstatements.
  • Document judgments and communicate effects to the team and those charged with governance when relevant.

At a Glance

TermMeaningTypical guideposts
Overall materialityMax misstatement (FS as a whole) that wouldn’t influence users.% of a benchmark: 5–10% PBT; 0.5–1% revenue; 1–2% assets/equity (context‑dependent).
Performance materialityLower than overall; to reduce to an appropriately low level the probability that uncorrected/undetected misstatements exceed overall materiality.Often 50–75% of overall materiality based on risk.
Tolerable misstatementApplication of performance materiality at the account/disclosure level.Allocated by account/assertion; may exceed 100% in total due to offsetting/nonoverlap.
Clearly trivialThreshold below which items need not be accumulated because they’re clearly inconsequential.Commonly 2–5% of overall materiality (firm policy).

1) Choosing Benchmarks & Starting Points

  • Benchmarks: profit before tax (PBT), revenue, total assets, net assets/equity — choose what users focus on and what’s stable.
  • Adjust for volatility/negatives: use normalized results or alternate benchmarks if losses/volatile profits.
  • Qualitative overlays: debt covenants, regulatory sensitivity, public interest, key ratios, and visibility of disclosures.
Tip: For start‑ups with negative PBT, revenue or total assets are usually more meaningful than PBT.

2) Setting Overall & Performance Materiality

Overall Materiality

  • Select benchmark and percentage informed by risk and user focus.
  • Consider prior period misstatements and current risk factors.
  • Document rationale (why this benchmark, why this %).

Performance Materiality

  • Set lower than overall to cushion aggregation/undetected errors.
  • Lower the percentage when risk is higher (e.g., weak controls, complex estimates, prior misstatements).
  • Align with tolerable misstatement allocations to accounts/disclosures.

3) Tolerable Misstatement & Allocation

  • Allocate performance materiality to significant accounts/assertions based on risk and expected misstatement.
  • Consider coverage: sum of tolerable misstatements may exceed performance materiality due to non‑congruent risks.
  • Document methodology (e.g., risk‑weighted allocation, scoping by size/risk).
Mini‑example

Overall M = 1,000; Performance M = 700. You assign TM: Revenue 300, AR 250, Inventory 250, PPE 150. Totals exceed 700, which is acceptable because misstatements are unlikely to arise concurrently across all areas at their maxima.

4) Clearly Trivial Threshold & Accumulation

  • Set a small threshold for items you don’t accumulate (e.g., 3% of overall M).
  • Accumulate all other passed/known likely misstatements by account and in aggregate.
  • Qualitative matters can never be dismissed as trivial (e.g., fraud, illegal acts, related parties).

5) Revising Materiality

  • Revise when initial assumptions change (e.g., actual results diverge, new risks emerge).
  • Update tolerable misstatement, sample sizes, and procedures accordingly.
  • Document the trigger, new amounts, and impacts on the plan.
Common pitfall: Failing to revise materiality after significant changes in performance — leading to insufficient procedures.

6) Evaluating Misstatements

  • Evaluate known and likely misstatements individually and in aggregate versus materiality.
  • Consider qualitative factors: concealment of fraud, compliance with covenants, changes in EPS trends, management bias.
  • Project sample misstatements to populations; consider sampling risk.
  • Request adjustments; if uncorrected, determine effect on the opinion.
Mini‑example

Uncorrected misstatements total 650 vs overall M 1,000. Qualitatively, 100 relates to fraud in revenue classification that flips a covenant ratio. Conclusion: likely material → request correction or modify opinion.

7) Special Considerations

Group Audits

  • Set component materiality below group performance materiality; communicate to component auditors.
  • Aggregate uncorrected misstatements across components at the group level.

Disclosures

  • Disclosures can be material by nature; evaluate adequacy even if amounts are small.
  • Consider presentation/classification effects (e.g., liquidity, going concern).

Interim Engagements

  • For reviews, materiality is used for planning and evaluation of misstatements but based on inquiry/analytics (not an audit level of assurance).

First‑year/Volatile Entities

  • Favor more stable benchmarks (assets/equity) or normalized results; consider higher risk → lower performance M.

8) Documentation

  • Benchmark selection and percentage; rationale and qualitative considerations.
  • Overall and performance materiality; tolerable misstatement allocations; clearly trivial threshold.
  • Revisions and their effects; evaluation of uncorrected misstatements and communication to TCWG.

9) Quick Checks

  1. Why is performance materiality lower than overall? To reduce the chance that aggregate misstatements exceed overall materiality.
  2. Can tolerable misstatement totals exceed performance materiality? Yes, due to nonoverlapping risks.
  3. Should “clearly trivial” items be ignored? They need not be accumulated, but qualitative red flags still matter.
  4. When do you revise materiality? When assumptions change (actuals differ, risks change).
