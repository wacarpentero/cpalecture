Confidentiality & Privacy

Learning objectives

  • Explain encryption fundamentals, techniques, and applications.
  • Recall the differences between confidentiality and privacy.
  • Identify methods to protect confidential data during design, development, testing, and implementation (e.g., obfuscation, tokenization).
  • Explain Data Loss Prevention (DLP) concepts and controls.
  • Identify financial and operational implications of a data breach.
  • Determine controls and data management practices to securely collect, process, store, transmit, and delete data in line with privacy regulations.
  • Detect design and operating deficiencies related to SOC 2® confidentiality and privacy commitments.
  • Perform walkthroughs and compare observed procedures with documented requirements.

Confidentiality vs Privacy

Confidentiality

  • Information is accessible only to authorized parties.
  • Achieved with technical controls (encryption, access controls) and administrative controls (policies, NDAs).
  • Focus: protect sensitive business data (trade secrets, financials).

Privacy

  • Lawful and fair processing of personal data.
  • Principles: notice, choice, purpose limitation, data minimization, accuracy, storage limitation/retention, integrity & security, accountability.
  • Backed by regulations and commitments in the privacy notice.
Exam tip: If the scenario mentions “data subject rights, consent, retention or deletion,” you’re in **privacy** territory. If it’s “preventing unauthorized access,” that’s **confidentiality**.

Encryption 101

Key concepts

  • At rest (disk/database), in transit (TLS), in use (app memory; harder—use enclaves/TT) .
  • Symmetric (AES‑256) vs asymmetric (RSA, ECC) + hybrid TLS.
  • Key mgmt: generation, rotation, storage (HSM/KMS), separation of duties.

Applications

  • Database/volume encryption; field‑level (e.g., SSN, PAN) with format‑preserving options.
  • Digital signatures for integrity & non‑repudiation.
  • Hashing & salting for passwords.

Related techniques

  • Tokenization: replace sensitive value with a non‑sensitive token; original stored in secure vault.
  • Obfuscation/masking: hide part of the value for lower environments (e.g., show last 4 digits only).

Data protection in the SDLC

Design

  • Privacy by design: minimize attributes, purpose bind, define retention/deletion early.
  • Classify data & assign owners; threat model data flows.

Development & testing

  • Use masked or synthesized data in non‑prod; never raw PII/PHI.
  • Apply static analysis/secret scanning; avoid hard‑coded keys.

Implementation & run

  • Encrypt at rest & in transit; protect keys in HSM/KMS; rotate keys regularly.
  • Log access to sensitive fields; alert on anomalies; restrict admin access (break‑glass).

DLP essentials

Channels

  • Endpoint (clipboard, print, USB), email, web uploads, cloud storage, SaaS.

Methods

  • Pattern matching (SSN), exact data match, fingerprinting, ML/heuristics.

Actions

  • Block, quarantine, encrypt, justify & log, coach user.

Breach impacts

Financial

  • Incident response, forensics, legal, customer notification, credit monitoring.
  • Regulatory penalties, civil damages, lost revenue/discounting.

Operational

  • Downtime, systems isolation, data restoration, customer support surge.
  • Remediation projects (re‑architecture, uplift, audits).
Quick calc example: If the estimated cost per compromised record is $165 and 20,000 records are affected, the direct cost estimate ≈ $3,300,000 (excludes fines/lost business).

Secure data lifecycle (collect → delete)

Collect & process

  • Lawful basis/consent; limit to minimum necessary.
  • Encrypt in transit; validate input; segregate duties.

Store & transmit

  • Classify & encrypt; key mgmt via KMS/HSM; role‑based access; logs & alerts.
  • Third‑party transfers: contracts, DPAs, secure channels, least privilege.

Delete & fulfill rights

  • Retention schedules; secure deletion; backups & search strategy.
  • Subject rights: access, correction, deletion, portability; track SLAs.

SOC 2®: confidentiality & privacy

Confidentiality (TSC)

  • Protect confidential information per commitments.
  • Design defects: missing encryption/DLP; no key rotation; broad admin rights.
  • Operating defects: control performed inconsistently; no evidence retained.

Privacy (TSC)

  • Notice & choice; collection; use/retention/disposal; access; disclosure to third parties; quality; monitoring & enforcement.
  • Defects: no consent tracking; retention not enforced; incomplete response to deletion requests.

Walkthroughs & testing

How to walkthrough

  • Select a control (e.g., new‑hire access provisioning) and trace end‑to‑end.
  • Compare observed steps and artifacts to the written policy/standard.
  • Document exceptions & potential impact.

Evidence

  • Approvals/tickets, logs, screenshots, DLP events, KMS audit trails.

Practice questions

1) Which option best protects PII in a non‑production test database?
  1. Encrypt the database volume only.
  2. Use production data with access strictly limited to QA staff.
  3. Tokenize or synthesize PII and mask any residual sensitive fields.
  4. Rely on application‑level access controls only.
Answer: 3. Encryption at volume level doesn’t prevent over‑privileged reads; privacy‑by‑design favors not using real PII in non‑prod.
2) A firm suffered a breach of 25,000 records. Estimated cost per record is $155. Estimate direct costs and name two operational impacts.
Answer: Direct cost ≈ $3,875,000. Operational impacts may include: extended outage/isolation, surge in support volume, restoration & validation efforts, and remediation projects.
3) During a SOC 2® review, you find MFA is disabled for admins “temporarily,” with no approvals retained. Classify the deficiency.
Answer: Operating effectiveness deficiency (control exists but was not performed/evidenced). If the control were absent, it would be a design deficiency.
4) Which DLP rule most effectively prevents accidental posting of customer PANs to public repos?
  1. Keyword “credit card.”
  2. Exact data match or pattern + Luhn validation on code pushes, blocking with justification required.
  3. Weekly scan of laptops.
  4. Email banner for external messages.
Answer: 2. Real‑time control on the risky channel with strong match method is most effective.
