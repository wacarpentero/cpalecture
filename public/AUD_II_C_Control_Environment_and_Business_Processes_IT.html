Understanding the Control Environment & Business Processes (including IT)

Learning Objectives

  • Explain the COSO Internal Control – Integrated Framework and its components/principles.
  • Understand an entity’s control environment, entity‑level controls, and business processes.
  • Identify and evaluate IT general controls (ITGCs) and application controls relevant to the audit.
  • Confirm design and implementation of relevant controls via inquiries, observation, inspection, and walkthroughs.
  • Use SOC 1® reports in audits of user entities; evaluate complementary user‑entity controls.

1) COSO Internal Control Framework

  • Objectives: operations, reporting, compliance.
  • Components: control environment, risk assessment, information & communication, control activities, monitoring.
  • Principles: 17 principles align to the components (e.g., integrity/ethics, board oversight, structure, competence, accountability, risk identification, fraud risk, change management, quality information).
  • Limitations: human error, management override, collusion, cost‑benefit constraints.
Tip: Use COSO language in your documentation to align walkthroughs and controls to recognized principles.

2) Control Environment & Entity‑Level Controls

  • Evaluate tone at the top, independence of those charged with governance, and clarity of authority/responsibility.
  • Consider entity‑level controls that address pervasive risks (e.g., period‑end close, centralized monitoring, policies over significant estimates).
  • Document how entity‑level controls affect the extent of testing at the process level.

3) Business Processes & Key Controls

Understanding Flows

  • Identify significant classes of transactions and related systems.
  • Map initiation → authorization → recording → reporting; capture master data and interfaces.
  • Spot WCGWs and the controls that address them.

Automated vs Manual Controls

  • Automated controls depend on reliable ITGCs; manual controls need precision and competence.
  • Indirect controls (e.g., reviews) require evidence of criteria, investigation, and resolution.
  • Consider preventive vs detective, and frequency (daily, monthly, ad hoc).

4) ITGCs, Application Controls & Key Reports

  • ITGCs: user access (provisioning, periodic review, privileged access), change management (SDLC, approvals, testing), and IT operations (backups, jobs, monitoring).
  • Application controls: edit checks, three‑way matches, automated reconciliations, interface controls, error handling.
  • System‑generated information: identify key reports; test logic or rely on tested application controls; consider EUCs (spreadsheets).
Walkthrough note: Walkthroughs should include IT elements (roles, configs, reports) and show evidence of design & implementation.

5) Service Organizations & SOC 1® Reports

  • Identify whether a service organization is used for significant processes (e.g., payroll, revenue).
  • Obtain and evaluate the SOC 1® report (Type 1 vs Type 2); check period coverage and subservice providers.
  • Evaluate complementary user‑entity controls and design procedures to test that the user entity performed them.
  • Use a bridge letter to cover gaps between the report period end and the audit date when needed.

6) Design & Implementation (D&I) Procedures

  • Combine inquiry with observation, inspection, and re‑performance during walkthroughs.
  • Document who performed each control, frequency, criteria used, and how exceptions are resolved.
  • If controls are relevant to the audit and reliance is planned, scope tests of controls in II.D; otherwise plan substantive procedures responsive to risks (II.E/Area III).

7) Quick Checks

  1. When are automated controls reliable? When supporting ITGCs are designed/implemented and the app logic is tested.
  2. What are entity‑level controls? Controls with a pervasive impact on the system of internal control.
  3. Type 1 vs Type 2 SOC 1®? Type 1: design at a point in time; Type 2: design & operating effectiveness over a period.
  4. Primary purpose of a walkthrough? Confirm design & implementation for significant classes of transactions.
