Cash and Cash Equivalents

Learning Objectives

  • Define cash, cash equivalents, and restricted cash under U.S. Generally Accepted Accounting Principles (U.S. GAAP), and identify common inclusions and exclusions.
  • Apply classification and presentation rules, including compensating balances, bank overdrafts, and the statement of cash flows reconciliation of cash, cash equivalents, and restricted cash.
  • Prepare a bank reconciliation and record resulting adjustments.
  • Account for petty cash establishment and replenishment; design key internal controls over cash.

At a Glance

Cash Currency, coins, demand deposits, and checks received (that are not postdated) Postdated checks, IOUs, time deposits with withdrawal restrictions; classify as receivables or investments
Cash equivalents Short‑term, highly liquid investments readily convertible to known amounts of cash with original maturity of three months or less (e.g., Treasury bills, commercial paper, money market funds without significant restrictions) Equity securities; assets with original maturity > three months when purchased; instruments with significant credit or interest‑rate risk
Restricted cash Cash legally or contractually restricted for a specific purpose (e.g., debt service, customer deposits, collateral) Present separately as current or noncurrent based on expected use; included in period‑end total of cash, cash equivalents, and restricted cash on the statement of cash flows reconciliation
Compensating balances Amounts required to be maintained with a bank If legally restricted, present separately (current or noncurrent); disclose terms
Bank overdrafts Checks written in excess of available balance Report as a current liability (do not offset cash) unless right of setoff within the same bank; changes usually appear in financing cash flows

1) Classification & Presentation (U.S. GAAP)

  • Original maturity is assessed at the date of purchase to determine cash‑equivalent status.
  • Restricted cash is presented separately on the balance sheet but is included in the reconciliation of beginning and ending cash, cash equivalents, and restricted cash on the statement of cash flows.
  • Bank overdrafts are generally current liabilities; do not net against positive cash balances unless a legal right of setoff exists with the same bank.
  • Foreign currency cash is translated at the period‑end spot rate; remeasurement or translation effects are recognized per the applicable foreign currency guidance.
Exam cue: A three‑month time deposit purchased one month before year‑end (original maturity three months) is a cash equivalent; a six‑month certificate of deposit purchased one month before year‑end is not.

2) Bank Reconciliation — Mechanics

  • Bank to book: Adjust bank statement balance for deposits in transit and outstanding checks; adjust company book balance for bank charges, interest, collections, errors, electronic transfers.
  • Record journal entries for book‑side items only (e.g., service charges, non‑sufficient funds checks, note collections).

3) Petty Cash & Internal Controls

  • Establish fund: Debit Petty cash / Credit Cash.
  • Replenish: Debit expense accounts and Cash short (or Cash over) as needed / Credit Cash; Petty cash account changes only when the fund amount is increased or decreased.
  • Controls: Segregation of duties, pre‑numbered checks, dual authorization for transfers, timely reconciliations, lockbox or electronic collections, positive pay with the bank.

Quick Checks

  1. What defines a cash equivalent? Original maturity of three months or less at purchase, highly liquid, minimal risk.
  2. Where do bank overdrafts go under U.S. GAAP? Current liabilities (no netting unless setoff right).
  3. Do you present restricted cash within cash equivalents on the balance sheet? No—present separately, but include in the cash‑flows reconciliation total.
  4. Which side of the bank reconciliation requires entries? Book side (service charges, non‑sufficient funds checks, collections, errors).
