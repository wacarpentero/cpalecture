C Corporations

Learning Objectives

  • Calculate taxable income for a C corporation.
  • Calculate the current-year net operating loss (NOL) or capital loss of a C corporation and the limitations on use in the current year.
  • Calculate allowable credits that reduce a C corporation’s tax liability.
  • Define the general concept and rationale of nexus for state and local taxation.
  • Define the general concept and rationale of apportionment and allocation for multistate businesses.
  • Calculate state taxable income using applicable apportionment factors for a given scenario.

Formation (Sec. 351)

Rule: No gain/loss to transferors or the corporation if property is transferred to a corporation by one or more persons solely for stock and the transferors are in control (≥80% of voting power and non‑voting shares) immediately after the exchange. Services don’t count toward control and are taxable as compensation.

Transferor consequences

  • Recognize gain to extent of boot received (cash, FMV property, liabilities assumed for tax‑avoidance/no‑business‑purpose can be boot).
  • Liabilities assumed by the corporation generally are not boot, except: liabilities > basis (gain limited to excess) or tax‑avoidance/no‑business‑purpose liabilities.
  • Stock basis = adjusted basis of property transferred + gain recognized − boot received.
  • Holding period tacks on for capital/1231 assets; starts new for ordinary assets.

Corporate consequences

  • No gain/loss to the corporation on issuance of its stock.
  • Corporate basis in property = transferor’s basis + gain recognized by transferor.
  • E&P is not affected by Sec. 351 nonrecognition; but boot paid can reduce E&P.
Illustration — Boot and Liabilities
A transfers property (AB $40, FMV $70) and liability $15 to NewCo for stock. No cash boot.
Control ≥80% achieved with other property contributors.

Transferor gain: liabilities are not boot unless > AB. Here, liability $15 ≤ AB $40 → no gain from liability.
Stock basis = $40 (AB) + $0 gain − $0 boot = $40.
Corporate basis = $40 + $0 recognized gain = $40.

Watch‑out: If liabilities assumed exceed the aggregate basis of property transferred by a shareholder, the excess is taxable gain to that shareholder.

Operations: Income, Deductions, Special Limits

  • Rate: Flat 21% federal tax on taxable income.
  • Start‑up and organizational costs: deduct up to $5,000 each (phase‑out begins at $50,000); remainder amortized over 180 months.
  • Business interest (Sec. 163(j)): deduction limited to the sum of business interest income + 30% of adjusted taxable income (ATI) + floor plan financing interest. Disallowed interest carries forward indefinitely.
  • Charitable contributions limit (see next section for order of operations).
  • Capital gains taxed at ordinary corporate rate; capital losses deductible only against capital gains (carryback 3 years, carryforward 5 years, short‑term character).

Exam Tip: For ATI in 163(j), post‑2021 rules use an EBIT‑like base (no add‑back for depreciation/amortization). Don’t mistakenly compute on EBITDA unless the fact pattern says otherwise.

  • Taxable income limit: DRD is limited to the applicable % of taxable income computed before DRD, NOLs, capital loss carrybacks, and (generally) before the §199A deduction (individuals only). If taking the full DRD creates or increases an NOL, the limit doesn’t apply.
  • Only domestic dividends qualify; certain dividends (e.g., from REITs) are excluded.
Illustration — DRD with TI limitation
Facts: TI before DRD and charitable contributions = $400. Dividend from 25%‑owned domestic corp = $100.
Tentative DRD = 65% × $100 = $65.
TI limit = 65% × $400 = $260 → not limiting. DRD = $65.

Watch‑out: Apply the charitable contributions limit using TI before DRD. Then compute DRD using TI after the charitable deduction is set, per the tested sequence below.

Charitable Contributions (C Corps)

  • General limit: 10% of taxable income computed before the charitable deduction, DRD, NOL carrybacks, and capital loss carrybacks.
  • Excess contributions carry forward 5 years.
  • Property gifts generally limited to the lesser of basis or FMV; certain appreciated inventory and scientific equipment have special rules (beyond scope unless provided).
Illustration — Order of operations (common REG workflow)
1) Compute TI before: charity, DRD, NOL/cap‑loss CBs, and special deductions.
2) Apply 10% limit → deduct allowed charity; carry forward excess.
3) Compute DRD using TI after step 2 (unless NOL exception).
4) Apply 163(j) interest limit and other items per facts.
5) Compute tax at 21%.

Capital Gains/Losses & NOLs

  • Capital losses only offset capital gains; carryback 3, carryforward 5 (short‑term).
  • NOLs: Post‑2017 NOLs carry forward indefinitely but are limited to 80% of taxable income (computed without the NOL deduction) in the carryforward year. Pre‑2018 rules differed (2‑year back/20‑year forward; no 80% cap).

Exam Tip: Identify the year the NOL originated. If post‑2017 (and not covered by temporary law changes), expect the 80% limitation and no carryback.

E&P & Shareholder Distributions

  • Ordering: Distributions are dividends to the extent of current E&P (pro‑rated during the year) and then accumulated E&P. Any excess reduces shareholder stock basis; amounts beyond basis are capital gain.
  • Property distributions: Corporation recognizes gain (FMV − AB) as if sold; no loss recognized. Liabilities assumed by shareholder reduce the amount distributed but not below zero.
  • E&P effects: E&P increases by recognized gains and decreases by FMV of property distributed (reduced by liabilities assumed).
Illustration — Classifying a distribution
Current E&P: 60; Accumulated E&P: 20 (positive). Distribution to A: $100 cash.
Dividend portion = up to total E&P (60 + 20) = 80 → $80 dividend.
Remaining $20 reduces stock basis; any amount beyond basis is capital gain.

Stock Redemptions

A redemption is a corporation acquiring its stock from a shareholder in exchange for property. Default treatment is a dividend under Sec. 301 unless one of the sale/exchange tests under Sec. 302 is met.

  • Sale/exchange treatment if any of the following:
    • Substantially disproportionate: After redemption, voting power < 50% and the shareholder’s percentage ownership of voting/common stock is sufficiently reduced (both the 80% of prior interest and <50% tests).
    • Complete termination of shareholder’s interest (including waiver of family attribution, if conditions met).
    • Not essentially equivalent to a dividend (facts & circumstances; usually meaningful reduction).
    • Certain partial liquidations (for non‑corporate shareholders).
  • Attribution rules (Sec. 318) can cause constructive ownership from family, partnerships, estates, trusts, and corporations.
Illustration — Substantially disproportionate
Before: A owns 60 of 100 voting shares (60%). After redemption: A owns 30 of 70 (≈42.9%).
Tests: After < 50%? Yes (42.9%). Is % after < 80% of before? 42.9% < 48% → Yes.
⇒ Sale/exchange treatment: A recognizes capital gain/loss; basis in redeemed shares is recovered.

Corporate Liquidations

  • General rule (Sec. 331/336): Shareholders treat liquidating distributions as sale/exchange → capital gain/loss; corporation recognizes gain/loss as if assets sold at FMV (losses limited for related‑party/step‑transaction scenarios).
  • Parent‑subsidiary exception (Sec. 332/337): If parent owns ≥80% of vote and value of subsidiary, neither recognizes gain/loss on liquidating distribution; parent takes subsidiary’s basis in assets (carryover basis) and assumes tax attributes.
Illustration — 332 liquidation
ParentCo owns 100% of SubCo. SubCo liquidates, distributing assets (AB $500, FMV $650).
No gain/loss to ParentCo or SubCo under Secs. 332/337. ParentCo takes carryover basis in assets.

Quick Reference (common limits & sequences)

Limits & key numbers

  • Corporate rate: 21%
  • Charitable contributions: 10% of TI (5‑year CF)
  • DRD: 50% / 65% / 100% at <20% / 20–<80% / ≥80%
  • 163(j): Generally 30% of ATI; excess CF indefinitely
  • Start‑up & organizational: up to $5,000 each (phase‑out at $50k); rest amortize 180 mos
  • Capital loss: offset capital gains only; CB 3 / CF 5 (short‑term)
  • NOLs (post‑2017): no CB, CF indefinitely; 80% TI cap

Workflow order (typical exam)

  1. Compute TI before charity, DRD, NOL/CL CBs, special deductions.
  2. Apply charitable 10% cap → determine deduction and CF.
  3. Compute DRD (apply TI limit unless NOL created/increased).
  4. Apply 163(j) and other limitations.
  5. Tax at 21%.

Tricky areas to memorize: DRD percentages and TI limit, attribution rules in redemptions, “liabilities > basis” in Sec. 351, and ordering of deductions.

Practice Questions

1. Sec. 351 formation — services and control

Three individuals form Hawk Corp. A contributes equipment (AB $50, FMV $80). B contributes cash $20. C performs services worth $10 for stock. Immediately after, ownership: A 72%, B 18%, C 10%. What is A’s recognized gain?

  1. $0
  2. $10
  3. $30
  4. $20
Answer & Rationale

Answer: A. Control test (≥80%) must be met by property transferors. C provided services, which do not count toward control. A (72%) + B (18%) = 90% ⇒ control achieved. No boot → A recognizes $0.

2. Liabilities exceed basis

J transfers property (AB $30, FMV $60) and a liability of $40 to NewCo for stock in a qualifying Sec. 351 exchange. What gain does J recognize?

  1. $0
  2. $10
  3. $30
  4. $40
Answer & Rationale

Answer: B. Gain is recognized to the extent liabilities assumed exceed the aggregate basis of property transferred: $40 − $30 = $10.

3. DRD percentage

Omega (C corp) owns 15% of Zeta (domestic C corp) and receives a $100 dividend. Which DRD applies (ignoring limits)?

  1. 50%
  2. 65%
  3. 70%
  4. 100%
Answer & Rationale

Answer: A. <20% ownership → 50% DRD.

4. Charitable contribution limit

TI before charity and DRD is $300. Charitable gifts total $50. Dividend from 25%‑owned domestic corp is $40. What is the allowable charitable deduction for the year?

  1. $30
  2. $40
  3. $50
  4. $35
Answer & Rationale

Answer: A. Charity limit is 10% of TI before DRD ⇒ 10% × $300 = $30. Excess $20 CF 5 years.

5. DRD with TI limit

Using the facts in Q4, after deducting allowed charity, what is the DRD?

  1. $20
  2. $26
  3. $39
  4. $13
Answer & Rationale

Answer: B. TI after charity = $300 − $30 = $270. DRD % for 25% ownership is 65%. Tentative DRD = 65% × $40 = $26. TI limit = 65% × $270 = $175.5 → not limiting. DRD = $26.

6. Capital losses

In Year 1, Corp has $20 capital loss and $0 capital gains. How much reduces ordinary income?

  1. $0
  2. $20
  3. $10
  4. $5
Answer & Rationale

Answer: A. Corporate capital losses offset only capital gains; no offset to ordinary income. Unused: CB 3, CF 5 (short‑term).

7. Distribution classification

Current E&P $30; Accumulated E&P $10; distribution $50 to a shareholder with $12 basis. Dividend amount?

  1. $30
  2. $40
  3. $42
  4. $50
Answer & Rationale

Answer: B. Dividend to extent of total E&P (30 + 10 = 40). Excess $10 reduces basis (to $2); any further excess would be capital gain.

8. Redemption result

Before redemption, T owns 55 of 100 voting shares (55%). After redemption of 15 of T’s shares, there are 85 shares outstanding; T owns 40/85 (≈47.1%). How is T’s redemption treated?

  1. Dividend
  2. Sale/exchange (substantially disproportionate)
  3. Sale/exchange (complete termination)
  4. Return of capital
Answer & Rationale

Answer: B. After <50% and post‑redemption % (47.1%) < 80% of pre‑redemption % (44% threshold) ⇒ substantially disproportionate → sale/exchange.
