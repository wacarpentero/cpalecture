Subsequent Events and Subsequently Discovered Facts

Learning Objectives

  • Distinguish subsequent events (between the financial statement date and the auditor’s report date) from subsequently discovered facts (after the auditor’s report date).
  • Identify Type I (adjusting) vs Type II (non‑adjusting) events and their reporting implications.
  • Perform required procedures through the report date and determine when to dual‑date or change the report date.
  • Respond appropriately when facts are discovered after report release, including communication with management and those charged with governance (TCWG).

1) Definitions & Date Framework

  • Subsequent events: events occurring after the financial statement date but on or before the auditor’s report date.
  • Subsequently discovered facts: facts discovered after the auditor’s report date that may have affected the report if known earlier.
  • Report release date: the date the auditor permits the report to be used (often distribution/public filing).
Tip: Lock these dates in your planning memo and update as they change to keep procedures aligned.

2) Type I vs Type II Subsequent Events

Type I — Adjusting

  • Provide additional evidence about conditions that existed at the balance‑sheet date.
  • Action: Adjust the financial statements.
  • Examples: settlement of litigation confirming the amount owed; subsequent receipts confirming accounts receivable collectibility; bankruptcy of a major customer due to pre‑existing financial difficulties.

Type II — Non‑adjusting

  • Relate to conditions arising after the balance‑sheet date.
  • Action: Disclose the nature and estimated financial effect (or a statement that such estimate cannot be made).
  • Examples: major business combination or disposal; casualty losses; issuance of debt or equity; significant restructuring announced after year‑end.

3) Required Procedures Through the Report Date

  • Read board and committee minutes through the report date and inquire about matters discussed at meetings for which minutes are not yet available.
  • Inquire management and, where appropriate, those charged with governance (TCWG) about subsequent events and subsequent transactions.
  • Scan the post‑year‑end general ledger (GL) for significant or unusual transactions.
  • Obtain updated written representations dated as of the report date addressing subsequent events and disclosures.
  • Perform targeted procedures where risks are identified (for example, subsequent cash receipts for valuation, loan covenant compliance after year‑end).

4) Facts Arising After Report Date but Before Release

  • Discuss the matter with management and TCWG and determine whether the financial statements need adjustment or disclosure.
  • Perform necessary procedures related to the new information.
  • Dating choices:
    • Dual‑date the report: keep the original report date and add wording such as “Report dated March 15, 20X2, except for Note X, as to which the date is April 2, 20X2.” Only the specific matter is covered by the later date.
    • Change the report date to the later date: extends responsibility for subsequent events through that later date.
  • Obtain updated written representations for the additional procedures and the later date covered.

5) Subsequently Discovered Facts After Report Release

  • Discuss with management and TCWG whether the financial statements require revision and whether users are still relying on them.
  • If revision is necessary and the client cooperates: advise management to make appropriate disclosures, revise the financial statements, and reissue the auditor’s report. Consider adding an emphasis‑of‑matter (EOM) or explanatory paragraph describing the revision.
  • If the client refuses to act: take steps to prevent future reliance on the auditor’s report—seek legal advice, notify those charged with governance (TCWG), and consider notifying known users or regulators as permitted or required by law or regulation.
  • For comparative periods, assess whether prior‑period opinions need updating or reissuance.
Red flag: Discovery of fraud or noncompliance affecting prior financial statements that were previously believed to be fairly presented.

6) Documentation & Communication

  • Dates considered (financial statement date, auditor’s report date, report release date) and the procedures performed through those dates.
  • Nature of each event/fact, evaluation (Type I vs Type II), and the basis for adjustments or disclosures.
  • Copies of updated written representations, revised financial statements, and reissued reports; communications with management and TCWG.
  • Legal counsel consultations and any actions taken to prevent reliance.

7) Quick Checks

  1. Which events are adjusted? Type I — conditions existing at the balance‑sheet date.
  2. What are your options if a fact arises before release? Dual‑date or change the report date.
  3. What if facts arise after release and management won’t revise? Take steps to prevent future reliance and consider legal obligations.
  4. Do you need new written representations when you dual‑date? Yes—covering the additional procedures and later date for that matter.
Example dual‑dating language

“Our report is dated March 15, 20X2, except for the matter described in Note X, as to which the date is April 2, 20X2.”
