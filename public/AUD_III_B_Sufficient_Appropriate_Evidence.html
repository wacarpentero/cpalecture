Sufficient Appropriate Evidence

Learning Objectives

  • Differentiate sufficiency (quantity) and appropriateness (quality: relevance & reliability) of audit evidence.
  • Select procedures (tests of controls, substantive analytical procedures, tests of details) that yield persuasive evidence for each assertion.
  • Evaluate reliability by source and nature (external vs internal, auditor‑direct vs management‑provided, electronic vs paper).
  • Apply requirements for substantive analytical procedures, external confirmations, and recalculation/reperformance.
  • Document whether evidence obtained is sufficient and appropriate to support conclusions and the opinion.

At a Glance

ConceptKey points
SufficiencyQuantity of evidence; influenced by risk of misstatement, materiality, and quality of evidence obtained.
AppropriatenessQuality of evidence: relevance (addresses the assertion) and reliability (source, nature, controls).
Reliability hierarchyAuditor’s direct knowledge > external direct > external indirect > internal (strong controls) > internal (weak controls) > inquiry alone.
Procedure familiesTests of controls; Substantive analytical procedures; Tests of details (transactions, balances, disclosures).
AssertionsExistence/occurrence, completeness, rights & obligations, accuracy/valuation/allocation, cutoff, classification, presentation & disclosure.

1) Relevance & Reliability

  • Relevance: The evidence must address the specific assertion and the WCGW. Example: confirming AR is strong for existence, weak for valuation.
  • Reliability factors: source (external vs internal), auditor’s direct knowledge, effectiveness of controls over the information, original docs vs copies, and timeliness.
  • Triangulation: combine evidence from different sources/natures to reach a persuasive conclusion.
Tip: If evidence quality is high, you may need less quantity; if quality is low, increase the quantity or seek better quality evidence.

2) Procedure Selection by Assertion

Existence/Occurrence

  • Confirmations, inspection of assets, vouching from ledger to source documents.
  • Observation of inventory count; inspection of subsequent receipts (AR existence).

Completeness

  • Tracing from source documents to ledger; search for unrecorded liabilities; cutoff tests.
  • Lease completeness search (embedded leases, service contracts).

Rights & Obligations

  • Inspect titles, contracts, debt covenants, consignment agreements; legal inquiry.

Accuracy/Valuation

  • Recalculation, reperformance, independent estimates, subsequent events review, third‑party pricing.

Presentation & Disclosure

  • Checklist‑based disclosure testing; tie‑out to trial balance; evaluate classification and wording.

3) Tests of Controls vs Substantive Procedures

  • Tests of controls evaluate operating effectiveness to support a lower CR and reduce substantive testing; appropriate when relying on automated controls or when substantive procedures alone cannot provide sufficient evidence (e.g., highly automated transactions).
  • Substantive procedures are always required (for each material class of transactions, account balance, and disclosure).
  • For significant risks, perform substantive procedures responsive to the risk; tests of controls alone are not sufficient.

4) Substantive Analytical Procedures (SAPs)

  • Develop an independent expectation (model) and define an acceptable difference.
  • Precision: depends on predictability of the relationship, data reliability, and disaggregation level.
  • Investigate differences beyond the threshold with corroborating evidence.
  • Document: expectation, factors considered, threshold, results, and follow‑up.
Mini‑example

Estimate payroll expense using headcount × average comp × months. Expectation differs by 3.5% vs 1.5% threshold → obtain corroborating evidence for bonus accrual changes.

5) External Confirmations

  • Design positive or negative confirms based on risk; control the process (selection, addressing, mailing, and receipt).
  • Address nonresponses with alternative procedures (e.g., subsequent cash receipts for AR).
  • Beware of management interception risk; maintain auditor control and consider electronic secure platforms.

6) Inspection, Observation, Inquiry, Recalculation, Reperformance

  • Inspection: examine records/assets for existence, rights, and accuracy.
  • Observation: watch a process or control being performed (e.g., inventory counts).
  • Inquiry: obtain information through questions; insufficient alone without corroboration.
  • Recalculation/Reperformance: auditor‑generated evidence; highly reliable for accuracy.

7) Using Information Produced by the Entity (IPE)

  • Identify the IPE (report/spreadsheet) and test completeness & accuracy (parameters, logic, source tie‑outs).
  • Alternatively, rely on tested application controls and effective ITGCs that produce the IPE.
  • Retain copies or re‑perform queries to evidence reliability.
IPE checklist: Who produced it? From what system? What parameters? How did you validate?

8) Sampling & Evidence Sufficiency (overview)

  • Sampling helps obtain sufficient evidence efficiently when full‑population testing is impractical.
  • Sample size increases with higher RMM, lower tolerable misstatement/deviation, and lower expected misstatement/deviation.
  • Project misstatements to the population and consider sampling risk in your evaluation.

9) Documentation & Conclusions

  • For each significant area: procedures performed, evidence obtained, results, and conclusions tied to assertions and materiality.
  • Document how evidence was sufficient & appropriate; if not, expand procedures or obtain additional evidence.
  • Capture unresolved differences, proposed adjustments, and communications with management/TCWG.

10) Quick Checks

  1. What’s the difference between sufficiency and appropriateness? Quantity vs quality (relevance & reliability).
  2. When can you rely on SAPs alone? When precision and data reliability are high and risks are appropriately addressed.
  3. Are tests of controls ever sufficient by themselves for significant risks? No — must perform substantive procedures.
  4. What makes evidence most reliable? Auditor’s direct knowledge and external sources obtained directly by the auditor.
