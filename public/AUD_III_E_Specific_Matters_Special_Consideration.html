Specific Matters that Require Special Consideration

Learning Objectives

  • Identify matters that typically demand heightened procedures beyond routine testing.
  • Design procedures responsive to related parties, accounting estimates and fair value, going concern, litigation/claims, subsequent events, and noncompliance with laws and regulations.
  • Appropriately use the work of specialists and internal auditors, and consider service organizations and group audits.
  • Recognize reporting impacts such as emphasis‑of‑matter (EOM) paragraphs, other‑matter paragraphs, or modified opinions.

1) Related Parties & Significant Unusual Transactions

  • Search broadly: organizational charts, ownership records, minutes, major contracts, confirmations, inquiries beyond finance.
  • Assess arm’s‑length terms; inspect supporting documents; scrutinize significant unusual transactions.
  • Ensure complete and accurate disclosure of relationships and transactions.
Red flag: Last‑minute side agreements or round‑trip transactions.

2) Accounting Estimates & Fair Value

  • Identify estimates with high uncertainty (impairments, credit losses, contingent liabilities, share‑based compensation, level 3 fair values).
  • Evaluate methods, data, and assumptions; consider management bias.
  • Procedures: develop independent expectations, test subsequent events, involve specialists, sensitivity analysis, model testing.
Tip: Corroborate significant assumptions with external data when available.

3) Going Concern

  • Identify indicators: recurring losses, negative cash flows, past‑due payables, covenant breaches.
  • Evaluate management’s plans (financing, cost reductions, asset sales) and their feasibility; review subsequent events.
  • Conclude on need for an emphasis‑of‑matter (EOM) paragraph and adequacy of disclosures; if inadequate, consider a modified opinion.

4) Litigation, Claims, and Assessments

  • Read minutes, obtain legal letters, and inspect correspondence with regulators and insurers.
  • Evaluate likelihood and estimability; reconcile to legal letter responses and financial statement disclosures.
  • If no response from counsel, perform alternative procedures; evaluate for a possible scope limitation.

5) Subsequent Events & Dual‑Dating

  • Read minutes through the report date; inquire management; scan the general ledger after year‑end for significant transactions.
  • Determine whether events require adjustment or disclosure.
  • If an event occurs after the original report date but before release, consider dual‑dating or updating the report date.

6) Noncompliance with Laws and Regulations (NOCLAR)

  • Understand the nature and circumstances; discuss with management and, where appropriate, those charged with governance (TCWG).
  • Evaluate the effect on the financial statements and the auditor’s report; obtain legal advice when necessary.
  • Consider whether withdrawal is necessary in extreme cases, consistent with law and regulation.
Do not overlook: Indirect effects (e.g., violations leading to penalties that affect accruals or disclosures).

7) Service Organizations — System and Organization Controls (SOC) 1®

  • Identify processes outsourced (payroll, revenue platforms); obtain the SOC 1® Type 2 report covering the period.
  • Evaluate complementary user‑entity controls and test that the client performed them.
  • Consider a bridge letter for gaps; evaluate deviations reported by the service auditor.

8) Using the Work of Specialists

  • Evaluate the specialist’s competence, capabilities, and objectivity; agree on the nature, scope, and objectives of the work.
  • Understand methods and assumptions; test the data provided to the specialist and the reasonableness of conclusions.
  • Do not refer to the specialist in the auditor’s report unless required by law or regulation.

9) Using Internal Auditors’ Work

  • Evaluate objectivity, competence, and systematic and disciplined approach of internal audit.
  • Determine whether to use their work to modify nature, timing, or extent; reperform a sample to validate quality.
  • The external auditor retains responsibility for the opinion.

10) Group Audits & Component Auditors

  • Determine component materiality and performance materiality; issue clear instructions to component auditors.
  • Evaluate sufficiency and appropriateness of component auditors’ work; resolve significant matters.
  • Reference to a component auditor in the report only when allowed or required; otherwise, the group auditor assumes responsibility.

11) Opening Balances & Initial Engagements

  • Obtain evidence that opening balances are free of material misstatement affecting the current period.
  • Evaluate consistency of accounting policies; read predecessor auditor communications when available.
  • Consider a modified opinion if unable to obtain sufficient appropriate evidence.

12) Documentation & Communication

  • For each special matter: risks identified, procedures performed, results, and conclusions, including any reporting implications.
  • Communicate significant findings to those charged with governance (TCWG) and document the content, timing, and recipients.
  • Update management representation letter to cover specific matters addressed (e.g., related parties, subsequent events, litigation).

13) Quick Checks

  1. When is an emphasis‑of‑matter (EOM) paragraph used? To draw attention to a matter appropriately presented or disclosed that is fundamental to users’ understanding.
  2. Best evidence source for litigation? Direct communication with external legal counsel.
  3. What must you do with complementary user‑entity controls in a SOC 1® report? Test that the entity performed them.
  4. When might you consider withdrawal for noncompliance? In rare, severe cases where law/regulation and ethical requirements indicate.
