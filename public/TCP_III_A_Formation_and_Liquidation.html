Formation & Liquidation of Business Entities

Learning Objectives

  • Apply §351 corporate formation rules (control ≥80%, property for stock only), compute recognized gain (boot/§357(c)), and determine shareholder stock basis and corporation’s basis.
  • Apply §721 partnership/LLC formation nonrecognition and compute outside basis including §752 liabilities.
  • Determine tax effects of corporate liquidation to outside shareholders (§331/§336) vs parent–subsidiary liquidation (§332/§337).
  • Evaluate partnership liquidating distributions (cash/property basis rules; gain triggers; §736 payments to retiring partners).
  • Recognize special considerations for S corporation formations/distributions when relevant (e.g., AAA vs AEP on liquidation).

Fast map

  • Control ≥80%, property for stock only → no gain/loss.
  • Recognize gain = lesser of boot or realized (unless §357(c)).
  • Stock basis = old basis + gain − boot − liabilities assumed.

Tricky‑area tips

  • Services ≠ property: Shares for services are taxable compensation and do not count toward §351 control.
  • Built‑in loss assets: Special rules can limit duplication of losses when multiple properties are transferred; exam may cue “FMV below basis.”
  • Boot ordering: Recognized gain is the lesser of realized gain and boot (cash + FMV non‑stock); character follows the asset.
  • Liabilities: §357(a) generally not boot; §357(c) triggers gain if liabilities assumed exceed aggregate basis; anti‑abuse under §357(b) if tax‑avoidance purpose.
  • Partnership debt: Recourse debt to partners who bear economic risk; nonrecourse shared by profit ratios (unless minimum gain rules).
  • §332 vs §331: The parent‑sub exception is common—watch for ≥80% vote & value and complete cancellation of the subsidiary stock.

Illustrative Examples (computations)

1) §351 formation with boot & liabilities

Transfer property (basis $120,000, FMV $200,000); receive stock and cash $30,000; corp assumes liabilities $20,000. Realized gain = FMV stock + boot − basis = $110,000. Recognized gain = lesser of realized gain and boot = $30,000. Shareholder stock basis = $120,000 + $30,000 − $30,000 − $20,000 = $100,000. Corporation’s basis in property = carryover + recognized gain = $150,000.

2) §721 partnership contribution with recourse debt

Contribute property basis $60,000 (FMV $100,000) with debt $30,000; 50/50 partners. Deemed cash distribution = liability relief $30,000 − partner’s share $15,000 = $15,000. Initial outside basis = $60,000 − $30,000 + $15,000 = $45,000. No gain since deemed cash ≤ basis.

3) Corporate liquidation to outside shareholder

Corp distributes property FMV $260,000 (basis $140,000); shareholder stock basis $80,000. Corp recognizes gain under §336 = $120,000; shareholder capital gain under §331 = FMV − stock basis = $180,000.

4) §332 parent–subsidiary liquidation (≥80%)

Parent owns 90% of Sub. Sub distributes all assets (FMV $500,000; basis $300,000). No gain to parent or sub if requirements met; Parent takes carryover bases totaling $300,000.

5) Partnership liquidating distribution — basis allocation

Outside basis $70,000; distribution of cash $50,000 and inventory (inside basis $30,000). Gain on cash? max(0, cash − outside) = $0. Remaining outside basis = $20,000 → basis in inventory received = $20,000; ending outside basis = $0.

6) §736 payments to retiring partner

Total payments $120,000; capital account $70,000; unrealized receivables (“hot assets”) share $20,000. §736(b) (capital interest) treated as distribution = $70,000. §736(a) remainder ordinary income (often for hot assets/goodwill in service partnerships) = $50,000.

Practice Questions

1) §351 control test: Transferors receive only stock but own 72% immediately after. Result?

  1. §351 applies — no gain or loss
  2. §351 fails — exchange is taxable
  3. §351 applies only if no liabilities are assumed
  4. §351 applies only if property FMV exceeds basis
Answer & Explanation

B — Control must be ≥80% (vote and value). At 72%, §351 doesn’t apply.

2) §357(c): A transferor contributes property with basis $40,000 subject to liabilities $70,000. Recognized gain?

  1. $30,000
  2. $0
  3. $70,000
  4. $60,000
Answer & Explanation

A — If liabilities assumed exceed aggregate basis, the excess ($30,000) is recognized as gain under §357(c).

3) §351 with boot: Using Example 1 facts, shareholder’s recognized gain equals:

  1. $30,000
  2. $110,000
  3. $0
  4. $50,000
Answer & Explanation

A — Recognized gain limited to boot received (cash and FMV non‑stock) absent §357(c) issues.

4) Stock basis after a §351 exchange (Example 1) equals:

  1. $100,000
  2. $200,000
  3. $120,000
  4. $90,000
Answer & Explanation

A — Basis = old basis + gain recognized − boot − liabilities assumed.

5) Partnership formation (§721) with debt: Contribution of basis $60,000 subject to debt $30,000 to a 50/50 partnership. Initial outside basis?

  1. $45,000
  2. $30,000
  3. $60,000
  4. $55,000
Answer & Explanation

A — Outside basis = basis − debt relief + share of partnership debt.

6) Corporate liquidation to an outside shareholder: Property FMV $260,000; shareholder stock basis $80,000. Shareholder’s recognized gain?

  1. $180,000
  2. $120,000
  3. $0
  4. $80,000
Answer & Explanation

A — Under §331, liquidation is treated as a sale of stock; gain = FMV received − stock basis.

7) In a qualifying §332 liquidation, the parent’s basis in assets received is generally:

  1. Carryover basis from the subsidiary
  2. FMV on the distribution date
  3. Zero
  4. Original cost of the parent’s subsidiary stock
Answer & Explanation

A — §334(b)(1) carryover basis applies in §332 liquidations.

8) Partnership liquidating distribution: Outside basis $25,000; cash distribution $40,000; no other property. Recognized gain?

  1. $15,000
  2. $0
  3. $40,000
  4. $-15,000
Answer & Explanation

A — Gain recognized only when cash (and deemed cash) exceeds outside basis.

9) §736 payments characterization for a service partnership most likely results in:

  1. All payments as §736(b) capital distributions
  2. Portion as §736(a) ordinary income for unrealized receivables/goodwill
  3. No income to the retiring partner
  4. All payments as capital loss to the partnership
Answer & Explanation

B — In service partnerships, goodwill is typically §736(a) unless the agreement says otherwise; unrealized receivables are ordinary.

10) Which item does not count toward §351 “property”?

  1. Accounts receivable contributed by a cash‑method taxpayer
  2. Inventory
  3. Services
  4. Machinery
Answer & Explanation

C — Services are not property; stock for services is taxable compensation and doesn’t help satisfy the 80% control test.
