C Corporations

Learning Objectives

  • Compute corporate NOL utilization and capital loss carry rules.
  • Analyze shareholder–C‑corp transactions: §351 contributions, nonliquidating & liquidating distributions, shareholder basis effects.
  • Explain key rules for consolidated returns and eliminate intercompany transactions.
  • Explain major international tax issues: income sourcing, foreign corps with U.S. ops (withholding/ECI), CFC/Subpart F/GILTI concepts, permanent establishment, branches vs subsidiaries, and IC‑DISC/FDII/BEAT high‑level requirements.

Fast maps & must‑know rules

NOLs & capital losses

  • NOL (post‑2017 general rule): Indefinite carryforward; deduction limited to 80% of taxable income (pre‑NOL). Special relief may apply if stated in the fact pattern.
  • Capital losses (C corps): Offset only capital gains; carryback 3 / carryforward 5 years (no offset to ordinary income).
  • §382 ownership‑change cap: Annual NOL use ≤ LT tax‑exempt rate × value of loss corp at change date; excess continues forward under the cap.

Distributions & corporate recognition

  • E&P waterfall: Dividend to extent of current E&P (then accumulated, if positive) → return of capital to stock basis → capital gain.
  • Noncash property distributions (§311(b)): Corporation recognizes gain as if sold at FMV; no loss allowed. Shareholder dividend to extent of E&P; basis in property = FMV.
  • Liquidation: Corporation recognizes gain/loss on distributed property; shareholder recognizes gain/loss = FMV received − stock basis.

Formations & §351

  • No gain/loss if property for stock and control ≥80% immediately after exchange.
  • Liabilities exceeding basis → gain under §357(c); shareholder stock basis = carryover basis − liabilities assumed + gain recognized.

Consolidated returns

  • Eligibility: Common parent owns ≥80% vote and value of each includible subsidiary.
  • Eliminations: Remove intercompany profits left in ending inventory; restore when sold externally. Single return on Form 1120.

International & cross‑border

  • Sourcing rules: Services → where performed; purchased inventory → where title passes; produced inventory → apportioned; interest → payer’s residence; dividends → payer’s country; royalties → property location.
  • Foreign corps with U.S. ops — withholding: U.S.‑source FDAP (e.g., dividends/interest) generally 30% withholding unless treaty‑reduced; ECI taxed on a net basis (typically no §1441 withholding).
  • Permanent establishment (PE): Fixed place of business or dependent agent habitually concluding contracts can create a PE; preparatory/auxiliary activities usually do not.

CFC & anti‑base‑erosion regimes

  • Subpart F: U.S. shareholders include pro‑rata share of certain CFC income currently.
  • GILTI: High‑level: anti‑deferral inclusion based on tested income; U.S. corps may get §250 deduction/FTC limits (facts will govern).
  • BEAT: Add‑back minimum tax on certain base‑erosion payments for large corporations.
Order: E&P → ROC → Capital gain §351 control ≥80% Services sourced where performed

Tricky‑area tips (memorize‑worthy)

  • Capital losses for corporations never offset ordinary income—only capital gains.
  • When current E&P is positive and accumulated E&P is a deficit, dividend is limited to current E&P.
  • §351 requires control ≥80% immediately after the exchange. Liabilities > basis → gain under §357(c).
  • Liquidation is sale‑like for corp and exchange‑like for shareholder.
  • Consolidated: eliminate intercompany profit left in ending inventory; restore when sold externally.
  • Sourcing: Services—where performed; Purchased inventory—where title passes; Interest—payer residence; Royalties—property location.

Illustrative Examples (computations)

1) NOL 80% limitation

Taxable income before NOL $1,000,000; NOL carryforward $600,000. 80% cap = $800,000 → allowed NOL $600,000; taxable income after NOL = $400,000.

2) Capital loss rule (corporations)

Current‑year capital loss $120,000; capital gain $50,000. Deduct only up to gains: $50,000. Carry back 3 / forward 5 the remaining $70,000.

3) §351 exchange with liability

Property basis $200,000 transferred for stock; corporation assumes $50,000 liability. No gain (liability does not exceed basis). Shareholder stock basis = $150,000; corporation basis in property = $200,000.

4) Nonliquidating distribution — cash vs property

Current E&P $40,000; accumulated deficit; cash distribution $60,000. Dividend = $40,000; remainder reduces basis then produces capital gain. If property FMV $150,000 (basis $90,000) is distributed, the corporation recognizes §311(b) gain of $60,000; shareholder dividend limited to E&P with basis in property = FMV.

5) Liquidation

Corporation distributes property FMV $500,000 with basis $200,000 to a shareholder with stock basis $300,000. Corporate gain = $300,000; shareholder gain = $200,000.

6) Consolidated intercompany profit elimination

Parent sells inventory to Sub for $120,000 (cost $90,000); 50% remains at year‑end. Intercompany profit $30,000 × 50% = $15,000 eliminated from ending inventory.

7) Sourcing

U.S. corp performs services entirely in Canada → foreign source income. Foreign corp sells inventory with title passing in the U.S. → U.S. source income.

8) CFC/Subpart F inclusion

U.S. shareholder owns 60% of a CFC with $100,000 Subpart F income. Current inclusion = 60% × $100,000 = $60,000.

9) §382 ownership‑change cap (add‑on)

Loss corp value $50,000,000; long‑term tax‑exempt rate 3%. Annual cap on NOL usage = $1,500,000.

Practice Questions

1) NOL usage: Taxable income before NOL = $1,000,000. NOL carryforward = $600,000. What is the allowed NOL deduction and taxable income after NOL (assume 80% limit applies)?

  1. Allowed $800,000; TI after = $200,000
  2. Allowed $600,000; TI after = $400,000
  3. Allowed $600,000; TI after = $300,000
  4. Allowed $0; TI after = $1,000,000
Answer & Explanation

B — 80% of $1,000,000 = $800,000. Allowed = lesser of NOL $600,000 and the limit → $600,000. Taxable income after NOL = $400,000.

2) Capital losses: Current year capital loss $120,000, capital gain $50,000, no carryovers. What is the deduction and what is carried?

  1. Deduct $120,000; carry $0
  2. Deduct $50,000; carry $70,000 (back 3 / forward 5)
  3. Deduct $0; carry $120,000
  4. Deduct $50,000; carry $50,000
Answer & Explanation

B — Corporations can deduct capital losses only up to capital gains; excess $70,000 is carried back 3 and forward 5 years.

3) §351: Shareholder transfers property (basis $200,000) to a new C‑corp for stock; corp assumes liability $50,000. Any gain recognized? What is shareholder’s stock basis? What is the corporation’s basis in the property?

  1. Gain $0; Stock basis $150,000; Corp basis $200,000
  2. Gain $50,000; Stock basis $200,000; Corp basis $250,000
  3. Gain $0; Stock basis $200,000; Corp basis $150,000
  4. Gain $150,000; Stock basis $50,000; Corp basis $350,000
Answer & Explanation

A — No boot and liabilities ≤ basis → no gain. Stock basis = basis − liabilities + gain = $150,000. Corp takes carryover basis + gain recognized = $200,000.

4) Nonliquidating distribution: Current E&P $40,000; accumulated E&P deficit $10,000. Corp distributes $60,000 to a shareholder with $30,000 basis. What is dividend income, ROC, and capital gain?

  1. Dividend $60,000; ROC $0; Gain $0
  2. Dividend $40,000; ROC $20,000; Gain $0
  3. Dividend $40,000; ROC $10,000; Gain $10,000
  4. Dividend $0; ROC $30,000; Gain $30,000
Answer & Explanation

B — With an accumulated deficit, dividends come only from current E&P ($40,000). Excess reduces basis (to $10,000); no capital gain since distribution didn’t exceed basis.

5) Liquidation: Corp distributes property (FMV $500,000, basis $200,000). Shareholder’s stock basis $300,000. Corporate gain recognized? Shareholder gain?

  1. Corp gain $300,000; SH gain $200,000
  2. Corp gain $0; SH gain $200,000
  3. Corp gain $300,000; SH gain $0
  4. Corp gain $300,000; SH gain $0
Answer & Explanation

A — Corporation recognizes gain/loss as if sold property: $300,000. Shareholder gain = FMV received − stock basis = $200,000.

6) Below‑market shareholder loan: Corporation lends $200,000 at 0% to its 100% shareholder; AFR = 4%. Annual imputed interest?

  1. $0
  2. $8,000
  3. $4,000
  4. $12,000
Answer & Explanation

B — Imputed interest ≈ AFR × principal = $8,000. Generally treated as a constructive dividend to the shareholder (to extent of E&P) and interest income to the corporation.

7) Consolidated return: Parent sells inventory to 80%-owned Sub for $120,000 (cost $90,000). Sub sells 50% to outsiders before year‑end. What intercompany profit is eliminated in ending inventory?

  1. $30,000
  2. $15,000
  3. $0
  4. $7,500
Answer & Explanation

B — Intercompany profit = $30,000; 50% remains in ending inventory → eliminate $15,000 until sold outside the group.

8) Sourcing: A U.S. corporation performs services entirely in Canada and earns $200,000. Source of income?

  1. U.S. source
  2. Foreign source
  3. Split U.S./Foreign
  4. Not taxable
Answer & Explanation

B — Services are sourced where performed → foreign source.

9) A foreign corporation buys inventory abroad and sells it to U.S. customers, with title passing in the U.S. Source of the sale income?

  1. Foreign source
  2. U.S. source
  3. Residence of seller
  4. Where manufactured
Answer & Explanation

B — For purchased inventory, source is generally where title passes → U.S. source.

10) U.S. shareholder owns 60% of a foreign corporation (a CFC). The CFC has Subpart F income of $100,000. Current inclusion for the shareholder?

  1. $0
  2. $60,000
  3. $100,000
  4. Only upon dividend
Answer & Explanation

B — U.S. shareholders include their pro‑rata share of Subpart F currently: 60% × $100,000 = $60,000 (simplified, ignoring high‑tax exceptions, etc.).

11) Which statement best distinguishes a foreign branch from a foreign subsidiary for a U.S. corporation?

  1. Branch income is reported directly by the U.S. corporation; subsidiary income is generally deferred until repatriated or included under anti‑deferral regimes.
  2. Branches always pay GILTI; subsidiaries never do.
  3. Neither affects foreign tax credits.
  4. There is no difference.
Answer & Explanation

A — Branch = flow directly to U.S. return (subject to FTCs); subsidiary = separate foreign corporation, with inclusions via dividends, Subpart F, or GILTI.

12) Which of the following is most closely associated with export‑like incentives for U.S. corporations?

  1. FDII
  2. BEAT
  3. IC‑DISC
  4. Both A and C
Answer & Explanation

D — FDII deduction (domestic corps with foreign‑derived income) and IC‑DISC regimes both incentivize export‑related activity (high‑level exam awareness).

13) §382 limit: Value of loss corporation at change date = $50,000,000; long‑term tax‑exempt rate = 3%. What is the annual cap on NOL usage under §382?

  1. $1,000,000
  2. $1,500,000
  3. $2,000,000
  4. $3,000,000
Answer & Explanation

B — §382 cap = Value × LT rate = 50,000,000 × 3% = $1,500,000 of NOLs usable per year (unused amounts can often carry forward subject to the cap).

14) Noncash distribution: Corp has E&P of $80,000 and distributes land (FMV $150,000, basis $90,000) to a shareholder whose stock basis is $20,000. What are (i) corporate gain, (ii) dividend income, (iii) shareholder capital gain, and (iv) the shareholder’s basis in the land?

  1. Gain $60,000; Dividend $80,000; Cap gain $50,000; Basis $150,000
  2. Gain $60,000; Dividend $150,000; Cap gain $0; Basis $150,000
  3. Gain $60,000; Dividend $80,000; Cap gain $70,000; Basis $90,000
  4. Gain $0; Dividend $80,000; Cap gain $50,000; Basis $150,000
Answer & Explanation

A — §311(b) corporate gain = FMV − basis = 150k − 90k = $60,000. Dividend to extent of E&P = $80,000. Distribution exceeds stock basis by 150k − 20k = 130k; after $80,000 dividend, the remainder $50,000 is capital gain. Basis of property to shareholder = FMV $150,000.
