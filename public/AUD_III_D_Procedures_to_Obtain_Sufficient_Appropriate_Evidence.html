Procedures to Obtain Sufficient Appropriate Evidence

Learning Objectives

  • Design further audit procedures that are responsive to assessed risks at the assertion level.
  • Select and execute the right mix of tests of controls, substantive analytical procedures, and tests of details.
  • Apply technique‑specific requirements for confirmations, inspection/observation, recalculation/reperformance, and inquiry.
  • Address information reliability (IPE/key reports), timing, and extent to achieve persuasive evidence.
  • Document how evidence obtained is sufficient & appropriate to support conclusions.

1) Designing Further Procedures (Nature, Timing, and Extent — N/T/E)

  • Start with a simple table: Risk → Assertion → Procedure(s) (Nature, Timing, and Extent).
  • Nature: choose controls, analytics, details—or a combination. For significant risks, include targeted substantive work.
  • Timing: interim vs year‑end; closer to period‑end increases reliability for cutoff and completeness.
  • Extent: sample size/coverage based on risk of material misstatement (RMM), materiality, and evidence quality (see III.B; sampling in III.C).
  • Unpredictability: add elements (e.g., unannounced procedures, different locations/items) especially for fraud risks.
Tip: Tie each test step to a what could go wrong (WCGW) so reviewers can see why it exists.

2) Tests of Controls (overview)

  • Confirm design & implementation first (walkthroughs). Then test operating effectiveness.
  • Methods: inquiry + observation + inspection + reperformance; period coverage must match reliance period.
  • Sampling: attribute sampling; evaluate upper deviation rate versus tolerable deviation (see III.C).
  • If exceptions exceed tolerable rate → increase control risk (CR), expand substantive procedures, and reconsider reliance.

3) Substantive Analytical Procedures (SAPs)

  • Develop an independent expectation; set a precision threshold based on predictability, data reliability, and disaggregation.
  • Accept/reject based on difference vs threshold; obtain corroborating evidence for significant variances.
  • Examples: reasonableness tests (payroll, depreciation), predictive modeling (utility usage), ratio/trend analysis.

4) Tests of Details — Transactions

Occurrence & Accuracy (vouch)

  • From ledger/journal to source docs (PO/receiving/invoice/shipping, cash proof).
  • Useful for sales/expenses; combine with cutoff testing around period‑end.

Completeness (trace)

  • From source docs to ledger; search for unrecorded liabilities; reconciling sequences.
  • Scan unmatched receiving reports and subsequent disbursements for accounts payable (AP) completeness.

5) Tests of Details — Balances & Disclosures

  • Existence: confirmations (AR, cash, investments), inventory observation, inspection of assets.
  • Valuation: price tests, aging analyses, subsequent receipts/sales, independent estimates.
  • Rights/obligations: titles, contracts, consignment agreements; legal inquiry.
  • Presentation & disclosure: checklist testing and tie‑out to TB/notes.

6) External Confirmations

  • Maintain auditor control over selection, addressing, sending, and receipt.
  • Use positive confirms for higher risk; resolve nonresponses with alternative procedures.
  • Evaluate exceptions (timing vs real differences). For AP, consider vendor statement confirmations.
  • Legal letters: inquire of external counsel for litigation/claims; follow up on no‑response.

7) Observation & Inspection

  • Inventory: attend counts; test tags; roll‑forward/back to year‑end; evaluate count controls.
  • Fixed assets: inspect major additions/disposals; verify existence and condition.
  • Observe key controls as part of TOC (e.g., review meetings, reconciliations).

8) Recalculation & Reperformance

  • Recalculate depreciation, amortization, interest, payroll, tax provisions as applicable.
  • Reperform controls or system processes (e.g., three‑way match) where feasible to obtain high‑quality evidence.

9) Journal Entry Testing & Management Override

  • Use targeted criteria (odd times/users, manual entries to revenue/estimates, round‑dollar, unusual combos).
  • Corroborate with supporting docs and approvals; review estimates for bias and unusual transactions.
Fraud cue: End‑of‑period manual entries that create earnings targets or reverse next period.

10) Subsequent Events & Going Concern Procedures

  • Read minutes through report date; scan post‑year‑end general ledger (GL); examine subsequent receipts/disbursements.
  • Evaluate going concern indicators and management’s plans; ensure adequate disclosure or modify report if needed.

11) Using Information Produced by the Entity (IPE) / Key Reports

  • Identify the report/spreadsheet; test completeness & accuracy (parameters, logic) or rely on tested application controls with effective information technology general controls (ITGCs).
  • Retain evidence of validation (screenshots/queries) and tie totals to source systems.

12) Documentation & Evaluating Sufficiency

  • Procedures performed, N/T/E, sampling parameters, IPE validation, and results at the assertion level.
  • Whether evidence is sufficient & appropriate; if not, expand tests or obtain different evidence.
  • Aggregate misstatements (factual, projected, judgmental) and compare to materiality; communicate with management/those charged with governance (TCWG).

13) Quick Checks

  1. Can tests of controls alone address a significant risk? No—targeted substantive procedures are required.
  2. Which direction tests completeness of expenses/AP? Trace from receiving reports/subsequent disbursements to the ledger.
  3. How do you increase reliability of SAPs? Use more predictive relationships and higher‑quality, well‑validated data.
  4. What is auditor control in confirmations? Auditor selects, sends, and receives responses directly.
