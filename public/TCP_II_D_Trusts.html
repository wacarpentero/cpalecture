Trusts

Learning Objectives

  • Differentiate simple vs complex trusts; identify filing and year‑end rules (Form 1041; calendar year; exemptions).
  • Compute DNI and the distribution deduction; determine amounts taxable to beneficiaries vs the trust.
  • Apply rules for tax‑exempt interest allocation within DNI and to beneficiaries (exclusion ratio).
  • Identify capital gains treatment (generally corpus, excluded from DNI unless allocated/required to distribute).
  • Explain grantor vs nongrantor trusts and when the grantor is taxed on trust income.
  • Apply §663(b) 65‑day election, separate share rule, specific bequests exclusion.
  • Describe QSST vs ESBT treatment for S‑corporation stock held in trust.
  • Recognize charitable deduction under §642(c) and key differences from individual §170.

One‑page map

Simple trust → must distribute all fiduciary accounting income (FAI); no corpus distributions; no charitable deduction; exemption $300. Complex trust → anything not simple; exemption $100. Estates → $600. DNI ≈ taxable income before distribution deduction and exemption, excluding capital gains allocated to corpus, + tax‑exempt income net of allocable expenses. Distribution deduction = min(actual distributions (excl. specific bequests), DNI). Beneficiaries taxed up to DNI received (by character proportionally); Trust taxed on retained DNI and capital gains (unless included in DNI). 65‑day election → treat distributions made within first 65 days as prior‑year distributions. Grantor trust → items taxed to grantor (Subpart E). QSST passes S‑corp income to beneficiary; ESBT pays its own tax on S‑income. Charitable deduction (§642(c)) → for amounts of gross income paid for charitable purposes pursuant to the instrument; no % limits like §170.
DNI drives who pays Capital gains ≈ corpus 65‑day election

Tricky‑area tips

  • DNI vs FAI: FAI is a state‑law/accounting concept; DNI is a tax concept that caps how much income beneficiaries must include.
  • Tax‑exempt interest travels to beneficiaries net of allocable expenses via the exclusion ratio.
  • Capital gains usually stick to the trust; watch exceptions (allocated to income by instrument/local law, or required to distribute).
  • Specific bequests of a sum of money or specific property are excluded from DNI if paid in not more than 3 installments.
  • QSST vs ESBT: QSST—one income beneficiary; S‑income taxed to that beneficiary. ESBT—S‑income taxed at trust level under special rules.

Illustrative Examples (computations)

1) Simple trust — DNI & distribution

Interest $20,000, qualified dividends $10,000, tax‑exempt interest $5,000, trustee fees $3,000 (pro rata). DNI = taxable items net of allocable expense + tax‑exempt net = $32,000. FAI (approx) = $32,000. A simple trust distributes FAI; beneficiary includes up to DNI, and the trust claims a distribution deduction up to DNI.

2) Complex trust — capital gain excluded from DNI

Interest $25,000, dividends $15,000, LT capital gain $12,000 allocated to corpus; fees $4,000; distribution $30,000. DNI (no tax‑exempt items, CG excluded) = $36,000. Distribution deduction = min(DNI, distributions) = $30,000; trust retains $6,000 of DNI. Character to the beneficiary: interest ≈ $18,750, dividends ≈ $11,250.

3) 65‑day election

Prior‑year DNI $40,000; distributions made in that year $25,000. On Feb 10 next year, trustees pay $10,000. Without election → deduction $25,000. With §663(b) election → deduction $35,000 (limited by DNI).

4) Exclusion ratio — tax‑exempt interest share to beneficiary

DNI total $36,000 with $6,000 tax‑exempt. Beneficiary receives $18,000. Exempt ratio = 6000/36000; Beneficiary’s tax‑exempt portion ≈ $3,000; taxable portion ≈ $15,000.

Practice Questions

1) Which describes a simple trust?

  1. Must distribute all income currently and makes no charitable contributions
  2. Can accumulate income and make charitable contributions
  3. Has no beneficiaries
  4. May distribute corpus but not income
Answer & Explanation

A — Simple trusts must distribute all income currently, may not make charitable contributions, and generally do not distribute corpus.

2) Compute DNI: Interest $30,000, qualified dividends $12,000, tax‑exempt interest $8,000, trustee fees $5,000 (allocate pro rata to all income items). What is DNI?

  1. $45,000
  2. $41,000
  3. $48,000
  4. $35,000
Answer & Explanation

A — DNI = (taxable income $42,000 − allocable expense $4,200) + (tax‑exempt $8,000 − allocable expense $800) = $45,000.

3) Distribution deduction & trust tax: Distributions $35,000 and DNI $45,000. What is the distribution deduction and how much DNI remains taxable to the trust?

  1. Deduction $35,000; Trust retains $10,000
  2. Deduction $20,000; Trust retains $25,000
  3. Deduction $45,000; Trust retains $0
  4. Deduction $0; Trust retains $45,000
Answer & Explanation

A — Distribution deduction = min(distributions, DNI) = $35,000; trust is taxed on the retained portion $10,000.

4) Beneficiary taxability when DNI contains tax‑exempt interest: From Q2, tax‑exempt portion of DNI is allocated by ratio. A beneficiary receives $20,000. What amount is taxable to the beneficiary?

  1. $16,800
  2. $20,000
  3. $15,000
  4. $10,000
Answer & Explanation

A — Taxable share = distribution × (1 − exempt ratio). Exempt ratio = 7200.00 / 45000 of DNI; taxable ≈ $16,800, exempt ≈ $3,200 reported on Schedule K‑1 as tax‑exempt interest.

5) 65‑day election: Prior‑year DNI $40,000; distributions paid in that year $25,000. The fiduciary pays $10,000 on Feb 10 of the following year and makes the §663(b) election. Prior‑year distribution deduction becomes:

  1. $35,000
  2. $25,000
  3. $25,000
  4. $30,000
Answer & Explanation

A — Election lets the trust treat distributions within the first 65 days as prior‑year payments; deduction rises to $35,000 (still limited by DNI).

6) Personal exemptions for Subchapter J: Which is correct?

  1. Simple trust $300; Complex trust $100; Estate $600
  2. Simple trust $100; Complex trust $300; Estate $600
  3. All trusts $600
  4. Trusts have no personal exemptions
Answer & Explanation

A — Simple $300, complex $100, estate $600.

7) Which statement about grantor trusts is most accurate?

  1. Trust income is always taxed to the trust
  2. Trust items are taxed to the grantor if the grantor retains certain powers or benefits
  3. Beneficiaries always pay tax on undistributed income
  4. Grantor trusts cannot hold S‑corp stock
Answer & Explanation

B — Under §§671–679, specified powers/benefits cause the grantor to be treated as owner and taxed on trust income.

8) QSST vs ESBT: Which is correct?

  1. QSST must have a single income beneficiary; S‑income taxed to that beneficiary
  2. ESBT S‑income is taxed to the beneficiary
  3. QSST can have multiple income beneficiaries
  4. Neither trust can hold S‑corp stock
Answer & Explanation

A — QSST requires one income beneficiary and passes S‑income to that beneficiary. ESBT pays its own tax on S‑income under special rules.

9) Charitable deduction under §642(c) is allowed when the trust:

  1. Pays or sets aside amounts out of gross income for charitable purposes pursuant to the instrument
  2. Makes any contribution of corpus regardless of instrument terms
  3. Uses the individual §170 percentage limits
  4. Claims only if a simple trust
Answer & Explanation

A — §642(c) applies to amounts of gross income paid or set aside for charity as authorized by the governing instrument; different from §170.

10) Capital gains treatment: A trust realizes LT capital gain of $20,000 allocated to corpus; it distributes income of $15,000 to the beneficiary. Which is most accurate?

  1. The $20,000 capital gain is typically taxed to the trust and excluded from DNI
  2. The capital gain is always part of DNI
  3. The beneficiary must include the capital gain because there was a distribution
  4. The gain is deductible to the trust
Answer & Explanation

A — Capital gains are usually allocated to corpus and excluded from DNI unless the instrument/local law allocates them to income or requires distribution.
