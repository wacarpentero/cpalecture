Planning for & Using the Work of Others

Learning Objectives

  • Plan whether and how to use the work of internal auditors, specialists, and component auditors.
  • Evaluate the competence, capabilities, and objectivity of others whose work may be used.
  • Set clear instructions, communication, and review procedures; determine limits on direct assistance by internal auditors.
  • Document responsibilities and conclusions; understand if/when to reference others in the auditor’s report.

At a Glance

AreaKey points to remember
Internal auditors (AU‑C 610)May use their work after evaluating competence, objectivity, and a systematic approach; direct assistance by IA is restricted and not allowed in some jurisdictions. Auditor directs, supervises, and reviews any such work.
Auditor’s specialist (AU‑C 620)Engage when expertise beyond accounting/auditing is needed. Agree on scope and responsibilities; evaluate the adequacy of the specialist’s work as audit evidence.
Management’s specialistEvaluate competence/objectivity and the methods, assumptions, and data used; consider engaging your own specialist when needed.
Component auditors (AU‑C 600)Assess competence/independence; issue clear instructions (materiality, risks, required work); review component work and determine sufficiency for the group opinion.
ReportingOrdinarily do not refer to specialists or component auditors unless required by law/regulation or to explain a division of responsibility in group audits.

1) Planning: Deciding to Use the Work of Others

  • Consider engagement risks, complexity (e.g., valuation, actuarial, IT), geography (components), and resources/timing.
  • Perform preliminary evaluations of others’ competence, capabilities, and objectivity.
  • Define roles early in the overall strategy and reflect in the audit plan (see II.A).
Tip: For high‑risk estimates (e.g., Level 3 fair value), plan for an auditor’s specialist at the outset to avoid late surprises.

2) Using Internal Auditors (AU‑C 610)

  • Evaluate IA function: competence (qualifications, QA), objectivity (organizational status/policies), and systematic & disciplined approach (methodology, documentation).
  • Using IA work: align with audit objectives; test IA work for adequacy; perform additional procedures in higher‑risk areas.
  • Direct assistance: If permitted, the external auditor directs, supervises, and reviews IA work and does not use IA for areas with significant judgment/high RMM.
Mini‑example

IA tested three‑way match controls in AP. The external auditor re‑performs a sample of IA’s tests, finds them reliable, and reduces control testing extent — but still performs substantive procedures due to completeness risk.

3) Using Specialists (AU‑C 620)

Auditor’s Specialist

  • Engage for areas like valuation (Level 3), actuarial liabilities, environmental obligations, complex IT.
  • Agree on nature, scope, objectives, confidentiality, and reporting format.
  • Evaluate the specialist’s work and address inconsistencies with other evidence.

Management’s Specialist

  • Evaluate competence/objectivity of the specialist used by management.
  • Test methods, significant assumptions, and source data; consider your own specialist if needed.
  • If results conflict with evidence, investigate and resolve; adjust risk assessment/procedures.

4) Group Audits & Component Auditors (AU‑C 600)

  • Group strategy: understand the group structure, significant components, and consolidation process.
  • Instructions: communicate materiality by component, identified risks, required procedures, reporting package templates, and timelines.
  • Review: evaluate summaries and selected working papers; consider different frameworks/languages/time zones.
  • Reporting: reference component auditors only if required/permitted and when dividing responsibility per standards.
Checklist: Component scoping, materiality allocation, risk alignment, sufficiency of evidence, and consolidation procedures reviewed.

5) Documentation & Communications

  • Basis for using IA work or direct assistance; tests of IA work performed.
  • Engagement terms/evaluation of specialists; review of their work and conclusions reached.
  • Group instructions, component materiality, review of component work, and sufficiency of evidence for the group opinion.
  • Key communications with management and TCWG about reliance on others.

6) Quick Checks

  1. When is direct assistance by internal auditors acceptable? Only where permitted and not for high‑judgment/high‑risk areas; auditor must direct, supervise, and review.
  2. What’s the difference between an auditor’s specialist and a management’s specialist? One is engaged by the auditor; the other provides evidence used by management — both must be evaluated.
  3. Who is responsible for the group opinion? The group engagement partner/team, even when component auditors perform work.
  4. Should specialists be named in the auditor’s report? Generally no, unless required by law/regulation or to explain division of responsibility.
