Reporting on Compliance

Learning Objectives

  • Identify the main frameworks for compliance reporting: Government Auditing Standards (GAGAS, often called the “Yellow Book”), Uniform Guidance for federal awards (2 CFR 200, “Single Audit”), and compliance audits under AICPA auditing standards (AU‑C 935).
  • Describe the required reports and opinions for each framework and when to use restricted‑use language.
  • Apply modification decisions (qualified, adverse, disclaimer) for compliance opinions and communication requirements for internal control over compliance.
  • Document compliance findings, questioned costs, and follow‑up, and understand how they affect the auditor’s report.

1) Frameworks & When They Apply

  • Government Auditing Standards (GAGAS): Required for many governmental audits and certain grant audits; adds reporting on internal control over financial reporting and on compliance and other matters to the financial statement audit.
  • Uniform Guidance (2 CFR 200) — Single Audit: Required for entities that expend federal awards at or above the threshold; auditor opines on compliance for major programs and reports on internal control over compliance.
  • AU‑C 935 — Compliance Audits: Applies when auditing an entity’s compliance with laws, regulations, rules, contracts, or grants; often used for state programs or bond covenants.
  • Engagements under attestation standards: When only specific procedures are requested, use agreed‑upon procedures with restricted use.

2) GAGAS Reporting with a Financial Statement Audit

  • Issue a separate report that describes the scope of testing of internal control over financial reporting and compliance and other matters, and any findings.
  • Do not express an opinion on internal control or on compliance.
  • Include inherent limitations wording and the purpose of the report; indicate that the report is an integral part of the audit conducted in accordance with GAGAS.
  • Communicate significant deficiencies/material weaknesses and instances of noncompliance to those charged with governance.

3) Uniform Guidance (Single Audit) — Required Reports

Compliance Report for Each Major Program

  • Express an opinion on whether the entity complied, in all material respects, with the types of compliance requirements applicable to each major program.
  • Identify the criteria (Uniform Guidance and program‑specific requirements).
  • Include a Basis for Opinion and describe the audit was conducted in accordance with auditing standards generally accepted in the United States of America and Government Auditing Standards.

Report on Internal Control over Compliance

  • Describe the scope of testing and any significant deficiencies or material weaknesses in internal control over compliance.
  • No opinion on internal control is expressed.
  • Include instances of material noncompliance and questioned costs (known and likely).
  • SEFA report: Report on the Schedule of Expenditures of Federal Awards (SEFA) in relation to the financial statements (if engaged); identify any significant issues with preparation or completeness.
  • Schedule of findings and questioned costs: Present findings with criteria, condition, cause, effect, and recommendation; follow the Uniform Guidance format and include a summary of auditor results.
  • Auditee sections: Management’s corrective action plan and summary of prior audit findings must be prepared by the auditee.

4) AU‑C 935 Compliance Audits (Non‑Federal)

  • Express an opinion on compliance with specified requirements; identify the requirements and the applicable criteria.
  • Issue a separate Report on Internal Control over Compliance describing the scope and any significant deficiencies/material weaknesses; do not express an opinion on internal control.
  • Use restricted‑use language when required by the circumstances (for example, reports intended only for specified parties).

5) Opinions, Modifications, and Restricted Use

  • Unmodified opinion: Compliance, in all material respects, with applicable requirements.
  • Qualified opinion: Material noncompliance that is not pervasive to the program or subject matter.
  • Adverse opinion: Material and pervasive noncompliance.
  • Disclaimer of opinion: Scope limitation (for example, inability to obtain sufficient appropriate evidence).
  • Restricted use: Include when the report is intended only for specified parties (for example, AUP or certain contract compliance audits). Single Audit reports are generally not restricted unless required by law/regulation.

6) Findings, Questioned Costs, and Follow‑up

  • Each finding should include criteria, condition, cause, and effect, plus a recommendation.
  • Questioned costs: Known questioned costs and a best estimate of likely questioned costs must be identified; relate them to the requirement.
  • Communicate findings to management and those charged with governance; obtain and include management’s corrective action plan when applicable.
  • Follow up on prior findings; report status (resolved, repeated, or partially corrected).

7) Documentation Essentials

  • Program identification, criteria, risk assessment, sampling, and procedures performed for each requirement.
  • Evidence supporting compliance testing results, internal control over compliance evaluations, and the basis for opinion(s) issued.
  • Schedules supporting questioned costs and communications with management and those charged with governance.
  • Copies of the issued reports and distribution lists; evidence of submission to oversight portals when required.

8) Quick Checks

  1. Does a Single Audit require an opinion on internal control over compliance? No—the auditor reports deficiencies but does not opine on internal control.
  2. When is restricted‑use language required? Agreed‑upon procedures and certain non‑federal compliance audits intended for specified parties.
  3. Material noncompliance limited to one requirement of a major program—opinion? Qualified for that major program.
  4. Are GAGAS compliance reports generally restricted? No—unless law or regulation requires restriction.
  5. What belongs in a finding? Criteria, condition, cause, effect, and recommendation (plus questioned costs when applicable).
