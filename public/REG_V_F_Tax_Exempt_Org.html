Tax‑Exempt Organizations

Learning Objectives

  • Recall common types of tax‑exempt organizations: §501(c)(3) public charities & private foundations, §501(c)(4) social welfare, §501(c)(6) business leagues, §501(c)(7) social clubs, and §527 political organizations.
  • Identify contribution deductibility rules and lobbying/political activity limitations by type.
  • Apply the basics of unrelated business taxable income (UBTI), exclusions, and filing requirements.
  • Know key filings & compliance: Forms 1023/1024, 990 series, 990‑T, public support tests, and automatic revocation.
  • Recognize private foundation excise tax rules at a high level.

Common Types & Key Differences

TypePurpose / ExamplesDonor deductibilityLobbying / Political
§501(c)(3) (charitable, educational, religious, scientific) Public charities (e.g., schools, hospitals) and private foundations. Yes, subject to §170 limits (public charities generally higher limits than PFs). No campaign intervention. Lobbying limited (substantial‑part test or 501(h) expenditure election).
§501(c)(4) Social welfare Civic leagues, advocacy orgs focused on community welfare. No charitable deduction for contributions. Lobbying permitted; some political activity allowed if not primary.
§501(c)(6) Business leagues Chambers of commerce, trade associations. No charitable deduction; members may deduct dues only if not for lobbying/political. Some lobbying allowed; political activity limited.
§501(c)(7) Social clubs Clubs for pleasure/recreation; member‑supported. No charitable deduction to the club. Limited nonmember income or investment income may be taxable as UBTI; political activity not typical.
§527 Political organizations Party committees, PACs. No charitable deduction; donors may have separate disclosure rules. Political purpose is primary; exempt under §527, not §501(c).

Organized & Operated: §501(c)(3) requires both the organizational test (proper purpose in governing documents) and the operational test (no private inurement, primarily exempt activities).

Getting/Keeping Exemption

Applications

  • Form 1023 (or 1023‑EZ if eligible) for §501(c)(3).
  • Form 1024 or 1024‑A for many §501(c) types (e.g., (c)(4)).
  • Employer ID required; adopt organizing docs with required purpose & dissolution clause (for (c)(3)).

Public charity vs private foundation

  • All §501(c)(3) orgs are PFs unless they qualify as public charities (e.g., churches, schools, hospitals, or those meeting public support tests under §509(a)(1)/(2) or supporting orgs under §509(a)(3)).
  • PFs subject to special excise rules; public charities face intermediate sanctions (§4958) on excess benefit transactions.
  • 990 series filing & disclosure: 990‑N (≤$50,000 receipts), 990‑EZ (generally <$200,000 receipts and <$500,000 assets), Form 990 (full). Private foundations file 990‑PF regardless of size. Due the 15th day of the 5th month after year‑end; extensions via Form 8868. Failure to file for 3 consecutive years → automatic revocation.

Unrelated Business Taxable Income (UBTI)

  • Definition: Income from a trade or business, regularly carried on, not substantially related to the organization’s exempt purpose.
  • Common exclusions: Volunteers, convenience of members/patients/students, sale of donated goods, certain research, qualified sponsorship acknowledgments (not advertising).
  • Income exclusions/limits: Dividends, interest, and most royalties excluded. Rents from real property generally excluded (exceptions for personal property, services, and debt‑financed property under §514).
  • $1,000 specific deduction against UBTI; Form 990‑T required if gross UBTI ≥ $1,000. Separate computation by trade under §512(a)(6) (“silo” rule).
Illustration — Advertising vs acknowledgment
A charity publishes a program listing donors’ names and logos (no calls to action). → Generally a qualified sponsorship acknowledgment (excluded from UBTI). If the message promotes prices or urges purchasing, it becomes advertising → likely UBTI.

Private Foundations — Snapshot

  • 1.39% excise tax on net investment income.
  • Self‑dealing (§4941): strict rules prohibit transactions between PF and disqualified persons (e.g., sale/lease, loans, compensation beyond reasonable).
  • Minimum distributions (§4942): generally must distribute a minimum amount annually for charitable purposes.
  • Excess business holdings (§4943), jeopardizing investments (§4944), taxable expenditures (§4945) (e.g., lobbying, certain grants to individuals) can trigger excise taxes.

Quick Reference

What donors can deduct?

  • §501(c)(3): Yes (subject to AGI limits and substantiation rules).
  • (c)(4), (c)(6), (c)(7), §527: No charitable deduction (some dues rules for (c)(6)).

Political activity

  • (c)(3): No campaign intervention; limited lobbying.
  • (c)(4): Some campaign activity allowed if not primary; lobbying ok.
  • §527: Primarily political.

Practice Questions

1. Contribution deductibility

Which contribution is generally deductible as a charitable contribution for the donor?

  1. Gift to a §501(c)(6) trade association
  2. Gift to a §501(c)(3) public charity
  3. Gift to a §501(c)(4) social welfare org
  4. Gift to a §501(c)(7) social club
Answer & Rationale

Answer: B. Charitable deductions generally apply only to gifts to §501(c)(3) organizations (subject to §170 limits).

2. UBTI definition

Which is most likely UBTI?

  1. Interest income from bank deposits
  2. Volunteer‑run thrift store selling donated goods
  3. Advertising sold in the charity’s magazine
  4. Qualified sponsorship acknowledgment listing donor names only
Answer & Rationale

Answer: C. Advertising is an unrelated trade or business; the others are generally excluded.

3. Public charity vs PF

A new §501(c)(3) org that does not meet a public support test is classified as a:

  1. Public charity
  2. Private foundation
  3. §501(c)(4)
  4. §527
Answer & Rationale

Answer: B. §501(c)(3) orgs are private foundations unless they qualify as public charities.

4. Filing thresholds

Which organization must file Form 990‑PF?

  1. Any §501(c)(3) public charity with receipts over $200,000
  2. Any §501(c)(4) with assets over $500,000
  3. Any private foundation, regardless of size
  4. Political organizations under §527
Answer & Rationale

Answer: C. All private foundations file 990‑PF.

5. Campaign activity

Which statement about political activity is correct?

  1. §501(c)(3) orgs may spend unlimited amounts on lobbying.
  2. §501(c)(4) orgs cannot lobby.
  3. §501(c)(3) orgs are prohibited from campaign intervention.
  4. §527 orgs cannot engage in political activity.
Answer & Rationale

Answer: C. (c)(3)s cannot participate in political campaigns; lobbying is limited.
