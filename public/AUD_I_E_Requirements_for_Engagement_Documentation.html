Requirements for Engagement Documentation

Learning Objectives

  • Explain the purpose of engagement documentation and core requirements for content, form, and sufficiency.
  • Identify rules for timely assembly, retention, confidentiality, and changes after the report date.
  • Apply documentation expectations for key areas: risk assessment, sampling, estimates, consultations, and use of specialists.
  • Contrast documentation requirements across AU‑C/PCAOB audits, SSAE attest engagements, and SSARS reviews/compilations.

At a Glance

TopicKey requirements
PurposeProvide a record of the basis for the report and evidence that the engagement was planned and performed in accordance with standards.
ContentEnable an experienced auditor, with no prior connection, to understand procedures performed, evidence obtained, results, and conclusions — including who performed/reviewed and when.
AssemblyComplete administrative file assembly on a timely basis after report date (e.g., 60 days AU‑C / 45 days PCAOB firm policy); no deletion of existing documentation after assembly — only additions with explanation.
RetentionRetain for period set by standards/regulation (e.g., at least 5 years AU‑C; 7 years PCAOB) or firm policy if longer.
ConfidentialityWorking papers are the firm’s property; release only with client permission, subpoena, peer review, or professional/legal requirements.

1) Core Principles of Documentation

  • Sufficiency: Enough detail for someone else to re‑perform the logic and understand significant professional judgments.
  • Clarity & organization: Indexing, cross‑referencing, sign‑offs, and clear tickmark legends; separate planning, fieldwork, and completion sections aid review.
  • Timeliness: Prepare documentation contemporaneously; late documentation must indicate when and by whom it was prepared/reviewed.
  • Modifications after report date: Do not delete; document reasons for additions/changes, when made, and who approved; consider impact on the report.
Quality cue: Significant findings/issues must include the facts, the auditor’s response, and the basis for conclusions.

2) What Must Be Documented (Typical Audit File)

Planning & Risk Assessment

  • Understanding of the entity and environment, including controls.
  • Materiality, performance materiality, and thresholds for trivial misstatements.
  • Identified risks (including fraud risks) and linkage to overall/area responses.
  • Use of analytics, data analytics, and reliance on internal audit where applicable.

Procedures & Evidence

  • Nature, timing, extent of procedures; sampling approach and parameters.
  • Evidence obtained (copies/abstracts/summaries) and how reliability was addressed.
  • Results, deviations/misstatements found, and evaluation versus materiality.
  • Work of specialists, component auditors, and use of service organization reports.

Significant Findings & Conclusions

  • Matters involving complex judgments (e.g., going concern, estimates, revenue recognition).
  • Consultations and the conclusions reached; differing views and their resolution.
  • Basis for modifications to the report, EOM/OM paragraphs, and key communications to TCWG.

Completion

  • Subsequent events review, management representation letter, and final analytical procedures.
  • Overall review notes cleared; engagement quality review (if applicable) completion.
  • Assembly completion documentation and retention schedule reference.

3) Documentation by Engagement Type

  • Attestation (SSAE): document the subject matter/criteria, procedures performed (exam/review/AUP), findings or conclusions, intended users, and restrictions on use when applicable.
  • Reviews (SSARS): document inquiries, analytics, unusual items followed up, and the basis for the review conclusion.
  • Compilations (SSARS): engagement letter, knowledge of the industry/client, and the compilation report; no assurance evidence required.

4) Common Pitfalls (Inspection Themes)

  • Backfilling: creating documentation after the fact without dating appropriately.
  • Insufficient linkage: risks identified do not clearly tie to procedures performed.
  • Over‑reliance on management schedules without documenting evaluation of reliability.
  • Poor sampling documentation: missing rationale for parameters or projection of misstatements.
Tip: If it influenced your risk assessment or opinion, it belongs in the file — with enough context to be understood independently.

5) Quick Checks

  1. Primary purpose of engagement documentation? Basis for the report and evidence of compliance with standards.
  2. Who should be able to understand the file without prior connection? An experienced auditor.
  3. Can existing documentation be deleted after file assembly? No — additions require explanation; deletions are prohibited.
  4. Minimum retention period to remember? AU‑C 5 years (or longer by law/policy); PCAOB 7 years.
