Planning an Engagement

Learning Objectives

  • Develop an overall audit strategy and a detailed audit plan that direct the nature, timing, and extent of procedures.
  • Plan team composition, timing, supervision/review, and resource needs (including specialists and component auditors).
  • Establish preliminary materiality and incorporate it into planning decisions (see II.D for details).
  • Design the planning analytics approach to identify areas of focus.
  • Plan required communications with TCWG about scope, timing, and significant risks (see I.F).
  • Embed quality considerations (direction, supervision & review; EQR triggers; consultations).

1) Strategy vs Plan

  • Overall strategy sets scope, timing, and direction considering engagement specifics (entity size/complexity, deadlines, locations, IT environment).
  • Audit plan operationalizes the strategy into procedures at the assertion level and evolves with new information.
Tip: Write a one‑page strategy memo and link it to the audit program index for traceability.

2) Team, Timing & Resources

  • Assign team members with appropriate competence and ensure independence confirmations.
  • Define milestones (planning, interim, year‑end, reporting) with review checkpoints.
  • Plan for specialists (valuation, actuarial, IT) and component auditors where applicable (see II.F).
Mini‑example

A complex revenue model and Level 3 valuations drive the need for an IT specialist to evaluate system‑generated reports and a valuation specialist for models and assumptions.

3) Materiality Interface (Preview of II.D)

  • Set preliminary materiality (overall and performance) and a clearly trivial threshold.
  • Use materiality to scope significant accounts/disclosures and influence sample sizes.
  • Plan to revise materiality if actual results differ materially from expectations (see II.D).

4) Planning Analytics

  • Develop independent expectations for key metrics; compare to prior periods/peers/budgets.
  • Flag unusual relationships and volatility to be explored in risk assessment (II.E).
  • Introduce elements of unpredictability based on preliminary fraud considerations.

5) Using the Work of Others (Planning Stage)

  • Consider the internal audit function and whether to use its work (evaluate competence/objectivity and approach).
  • Define engagement terms for auditor’s specialists and plan evaluation of management’s specialists (see II.F).
  • For group audits, prepare component instructions (materiality, risks, required procedures, reporting).

6) Communications Plan (TCWG & Management)

  • Communicate planned scope & timing, significant risks (as identified), and the planned approach to materiality.
  • Discuss independence, use of specialists/component auditors, and reliance on internal audit.
  • Set expectations for deliverables, deadlines, and information requests.

7) Quality & Project Management

  • Plan direction, supervision, and review cadence with clear review notes workflows.
  • Decide whether an EQR is required by policy/regulation and build time for it pre‑report date.
  • Identify consultation points (complex estimates, going concern, unusual transactions).
Quality cue: Build a short RACI for key workstreams (who’s Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, Informed).

8) Documentation

  • Overall strategy memo and detailed audit plan (linked to programs).
  • Team assignment, independence confirmations, timeline/milestones, and budget.
  • Preliminary materiality and its impact on scope; planning analytics and identified focus areas.
  • Use of others, TCWG communication plan, and quality/EQR considerations.

9) Quick Checks

  1. What’s the difference between strategy and plan? Strategy = scope/timing/direction; Plan = detailed procedures.
  2. Why set preliminary materiality during planning? It drives scoping, sampling, and focus.
  3. When should you involve specialists? At planning, for complex or high‑risk areas.
  4. What must you communicate to TCWG early? Scope, timing, significant risks, independence, and planned approach.
