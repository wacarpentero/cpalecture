Derivatives & Hedge Accounting (ASC 815)

Learning Objectives

  • Identify characteristics of freestanding and embedded derivatives that require recognition at fair value.
  • Identify criteria to qualify for hedge accounting and when to apply fair value vs cash flow hedge models.
  • Recall presentation of gains/losses for swaps, options, and forwards.
  • Use given inputs (rates, notionals, FV changes) to prepare JEs for an interest rate swap that qualifies as a FV or CF hedge.

Derivative Basics

What is a derivative?

  • Underlying (e.g., interest rate, price), notional or payment provision.
  • Requires little/no initial net investment.
  • Net settlement or readily convertible to cash.

Recognition/measurement

  • All derivatives on B/S at fair value.
  • Gains/losses → earnings unless hedge accounting is applied.

Embedded derivatives

  • Bifurcate from host and account as derivative if: (1) not clearly and closely related to host; (2) instrument not measured at FV through earnings; and (3) a freestanding instrument with the same terms would be a derivative.
  • Examples often tested: equity‑linked features in debt, FX clauses not in the functional currency of either party’s primary economic environment.

Hedge accounting — documentation & effectiveness

  • Formal designation/documentation at inception: risk being hedged, hedging instrument, hedged item/transaction, method to assess effectiveness.
  • Highly effective expectation (qualitative or quantitative). Ongoing monitoring is required.
  • Types: Fair value (FV) hedge, Cash flow (CF) hedge, and Net investment hedge (not covered in detail here).

Hedge Models & Presentation

Fair value hedge

  • Hedges exposure to changes in the fair value of a recognized asset/liability (e.g., fixed‑rate debt).
  • Derivative FV changes → earnings.
  • Adjust carrying amount of hedged item for the hedged risk → earnings (basis adjustment).

Cash flow hedge

  • Hedges exposure to variability in cash flows (e.g., forecasted interest payments on variable‑rate debt).
  • Effective portion of derivative FV change → OCI (AOCI); reclassified to earnings when hedged cash flows affect earnings.
  • Ineffective portion → earnings.

Worked Illustrations (Interest Rate Swaps)

1) Fair value hedge — fixed‑rate debt

Setup (Year‑end)

Debt$1,000,000 fixed‑rate notes (6%)
SwapPay variable / receive fixed to hedge debt’s fair value
Derivative FV change$18,000 gain (asset)
Hedged item FV change$17,000 loss for risk hedged

Journal entries

Recognize derivativeDr Derivative asset $18,000; Cr Gain on FV hedge $18,000
Adjust debt (basis)Dr Loss on FV hedge $17,000; Cr Debt (FV adjustment) $17,000
Net earnings impact = $1,000 gain (ineffectiveness). Carrying amount of debt increases by $17,000.
2) Cash flow hedge — variable‑rate debt

Setup (Quarter‑end)

Debt$1,000,000 variable‑rate
SwapPay fixed / receive variable
Derivative FV change$12,500 gain; effective portion $12,000, ineffective $500
Net swap settlement received$3,000

Journal entries

FV changeDr Derivative asset $12,500; Cr OCI — CF hedge $12,000; Cr Gain on hedge ineffectiveness $500
SettlementDr Cash $3,000; Cr Interest expense $3,000
Reclass from AOCIDr OCI — CF hedge $12,000; Cr Interest expense $12,000
Interest expense is reduced by both the settlement and the AOCI reclass for the period’s hedged cash flows.

Practice Questions

1. Qualifying for hedge accounting

Which is required at hedge inception?

  1. Prospective and retrospective quantitative testing only
  2. Formal designation and documentation of the hedging relationship
  3. Derivative must be an exchange‑traded instrument
  4. No ineffectiveness permitted
Answer & Rationale

Answer: B. Documentation at inception is mandatory; effectiveness can be assessed qualitatively or quantitatively.

2. Embedded derivative

A host debt contract includes a clause requiring payments indexed to a commodity price unrelated to the debtor’s operations. The debt is not measured at FV through earnings. What is the likely treatment?

  1. No separation; account entirely as debt
  2. Bifurcate the embedded feature and account as a derivative at FV
  3. Elect OCI presentation
  4. Capitalize as an intangible asset
Answer & Rationale

Answer: B. Not clearly & closely related; meets bifurcation criteria.

3. Presentation check

For a highly effective cash flow hedge, the period‑end change in derivative FV is recorded to:

  1. Earnings
  2. OCI (AOCI)
  3. Deferred revenue
  4. APIC
Answer & Rationale

Answer: B. Effective portion goes to OCI, reclassified to earnings with the hedged cash flows.
