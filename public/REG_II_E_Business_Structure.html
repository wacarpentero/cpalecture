Business Structure

Learning Objectives

  • Summarize the processes for formation and termination of various business entities.
  • Summarize the legal characteristics of sole proprietorships, partnerships (GP/LP/LLP), LLCs, C corporations, and S corporations.
  • Explain the rights, duties, legal obligations, and authority of owners and management across entity types.
  • Identify the appropriate structure or consequence given a specific scenario (liability, tax, continuity, transferability).

Veil piercing: Even limited‑liability entities can expose owners if there’s undercapitalization, commingling, fraud, or failure to follow formalities.

Examples — Apply to scenarios

ScenarioAnswer
Two engineers want pass‑through tax, limited liability, and flexibility on unequal profit splits. LLC taxed as partnership suits: limited liability + operating agreement can set profit/loss allocations.
Founders plan to raise venture capital; investors require preferred stock and vesting. C corporation preferred for institutional investors; flexible equity classes and perpetual life.
Existing sole proprietor wants another co‑owner without personal liability for each other’s torts. Consider LLP or LLC to limit liability between owners.
Family business wants one class of stock, pass‑through, and wages to owner‑employees. S corporation if eligibility met; watch reasonable compensation rules.

Tricky Areas (Exam Tips)

  • Agency In a GP, any partner’s apparent authority can bind the firm; in corporations, the officers act as agents under board authority.
  • S corp limits One class of stock and eligible shareholders only; exceeding limits terminates S status (watch QSST/ESBT trusts).
  • LLC tax Check the default classification regs: single‑member = disregarded; multi‑member = partnership; elections to C/S available.
  • Veil piercing Undercapitalization + commingling + misuse of the entity are classic indicators.
  • Distributions Partnerships: per agreement; Corporations: only when declared; unlawful dividends may create director liability.

Quick Practice — 6 MCQs

  1. Which structure provides limited liability and default pass‑through taxation for multiple owners?
    A. C corporation   B. LLC (multi‑member)   C. Sole proprietorship   D. GP
    Show answer
    B — Multi‑member LLC is taxed as a partnership by default and offers limited liability.
  2. Who manages a corporation’s day‑to‑day operations?
    A. Shareholders   B. Partners   C. Officers   D. Directors
    Show answer
    C — Officers manage daily operations under the board’s authority.
  3. Which fact pattern most risks veil piercing?
    A. Accurate minutes and adequate capital   B. Commingled funds and severe undercapitalization   C. Profitable operations   D. Public company listing
    Show answer
    B — Commingling and undercapitalization are classic veil‑piercing factors.
  4. Which statement about S corporations is TRUE?
    A. They can have multiple classes of stock   B. They are limited to eligible shareholders and generally ≤100 owners   C. They pay an entity‑level tax like C corps   D. They cannot pay wages to owner‑employees
    Show answer
    B — S corps have eligibility limits including shareholder type and count; one class of stock only; reasonable wages to owner‑employees are required for services.
  5. A GP partner signs a supply contract within the ordinary course. Is the partnership bound?
    A. Yes, due to apparent authority   B. No, only if unanimous consent   C. No, partners aren’t agents   D. Only if limited partners approve
    Show answer
    A — Partners are agents of the partnership; acts in the ordinary course bind the firm unless the third party knows of a restriction.
  6. Which filing forms the entity?
    A. Articles of organization form an LLC; articles of incorporation form a corporation.   B. Partnership agreement is always filed with the state.   C. Bylaws form a corporation.   D. Operating agreement forms an LLC.
    Show answer
    A — State filings: articles of organization (LLC) and articles of incorporation (corp). Bylaws/operating agreements govern but do not form the entity.
