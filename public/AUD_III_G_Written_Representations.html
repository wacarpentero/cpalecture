Written Representations

Learning Objectives

  • Explain the purpose of written representations and how they complement, but do not replace, other audit evidence.
  • Identify required representations and additional, engagement‑specific representations based on risks and circumstances.
  • Determine the appropriate timing, dating, and signatories for the representation letter.
  • Evaluate the effect of refusal to provide representations on the auditor’s report.
  • Document the representations obtained and relate them to significant findings and conclusions.

1) Purpose & Role of Written Representations

  • Written representations are necessary audit evidence that confirm oral responses and acknowledge management’s responsibilities.
  • They do not replace obtaining sufficient appropriate evidence from other procedures.
  • They address areas where evidence is inherently limited (intent, completeness of information provided, recognition and measurement decisions).
Tip: Use a cumulative representation matrix to map each required representation to the related procedures and findings.

2) Timing, Dating & Periods Covered

  • Obtain the signed letter on the date of the auditor’s report.
  • Representations cover all periods presented in the financial statements.
  • If the auditor’s report is reissued or dual‑dated, update or obtain additional representations for the subsequent events covered.

3) Who Signs & What They Acknowledge

  • Signed by those with overall responsibility for the financial statements—typically the chief executive officer (CEO) and chief financial officer (CFO) or equivalent.
  • They acknowledge responsibility for preparation and fair presentation and for internal control relevant to that preparation.
  • They confirm that they have provided all information and access requested (records, minutes, communications, and other matters).

4) Required Representations (Core)

  • Financial statements: prepared and fairly presented in accordance with the applicable framework; all transactions recorded; disclosures complete.
  • Internal control: management is responsible for design and implementation; disclosure of deficiencies known to management, including significant deficiencies and material weaknesses.
  • Fraud: disclosure of known or suspected fraud affecting the entity involving management, employees with significant roles in internal control, or others where fraud could have a material effect.
  • Compliance: disclosure of known noncompliance with laws and regulations and investigations.
  • Related parties: identity of related parties and all related‑party relationships and transactions are properly recorded and disclosed.
  • Uncorrected misstatements: belief that the effects are immaterial, individually and in the aggregate; include an attached schedule of uncorrected items.
  • Estimates: assumptions are reasonable and appropriately disclosed.
  • Subsequent events: events requiring adjustment or disclosure have been considered and addressed up to the report date.
  • Going concern: plans and disclosures related to conditions or events that raise substantial doubt have been communicated.

5) Additional, Engagement‑Specific Representations

  • Use of service organizations and performance of complementary user‑entity controls.
  • Information produced by the entity (IPE) used as audit evidence is complete and accurate when appropriate.
  • Litigation, claims, and assessments are complete and properly disclosed; all correspondence with legal counsel provided.
  • Environmental, social, or regulatory commitments (if material) have been disclosed.

6) Effects of Refusal or Doubtful Reliability

  • If management refuses to provide the representations, or if the representations are not reliable (e.g., concerns about integrity), this constitutes a scope limitation.
  • Perform alternative procedures where possible; if sufficient appropriate evidence cannot be obtained, consider a disclaimer of opinion or withdrawal.
  • Inform those charged with governance (TCWG) immediately; document all discussions and attempts to obtain the letter.
Red flag: Last‑minute narrowing of scope in the letter or removal of required representations.

7) Form & Content Considerations

  • Use entity letterhead, date the letter as of the report date, and address it to the auditor.
  • Include specific references to the financial statement periods and the applicable financial reporting framework.
  • Avoid vague wording; tailor additional representations to identified risks and significant matters.

8) Documentation

  • Retain the original signed letter (or secure electronic equivalent) in the audit file.
  • Cross‑reference each representation to the related audit procedures and conclusions.
  • Document any inconsistencies between representations and other evidence and how they were resolved.

9) Quick Checks

  1. When is the representation letter dated? On the date of the auditor’s report.
  2. Does the letter replace other evidence? No—representations complement but do not replace other audit evidence.
  3. Must uncorrected misstatements be attached? Yes—include a schedule and management’s assertion of immateriality.
  4. What if management refuses to sign? Scope limitation → consider disclaimer or withdrawal and inform those charged with governance (TCWG).
Appendix: Sample headings for a representation letter
  • Financial statements and responsibilities
  • Completeness of information provided
  • Fraud and noncompliance with laws and regulations
  • Related parties
  • Uncorrected misstatements (attached schedule)
  • Estimates and fair value measurements
  • Subsequent events
  • Going concern

Note: Tailor the letter to the engagement; avoid including representations that contradict known facts.
