Specific Areas of Engagement Risk

Learning Objectives

  • Recognize common high‑risk areas in audits and the assertions they affect.
  • Design responsive procedures (tests of controls and substantive procedures) tailored to each risk.
  • Apply professional skepticism to significant estimates, unusual transactions, and fraud risks.
  • Document risk‑to‑procedure linkage and conclusions with sufficient clarity for review.

1) Revenue Recognition (ASC 606)

  • Identify significant classes of revenue and contract types (variable consideration, multi‑element, bill‑and‑hold, consignment).
  • Evaluate controls: contract approval, price/term changes, cut‑off, returns/allowances.
  • Procedures: read contracts; test cut‑off around period‑end; confirm terms with customers; test variable consideration and rights of return; analytics on margins and returns.
  • Fraud focus: target journal entries, unusual manual credits/shipments, and channel stuffing indicators.
Mini‑example

Quarter‑end spike in shipments with extended payment terms → expand cut‑off and confirmations; assess whether control over goods transferred.

2) Accounting Estimates & Fair Value (AU‑C 540)

  • Identify estimates with high estimation uncertainty (impairments, CECL/credit losses, contingent liabilities, stock comp, level 3 fair values).
  • Evaluate methods, data, and assumptions; consider management bias (optimism, changes without justification).
  • Procedures: develop independent expectations, test subsequent events, involve specialists, perform sensitivity analysis.
Tip: Corroborate management’s significant assumptions with external data when available.

3) Related Parties & Significant Unusual Transactions (AU‑C 550/240)

  • Perform a broad search for related parties: org charts, ownership records, minutes, key contracts, confirmations, and inquiries beyond finance.
  • Evaluate terms for arm’s‑length; inspect supporting documents; scrutinize significant unusual transactions.
  • Ensure complete & accurate disclosure of relationships and transactions.

4) Going Concern (AU‑C 570)

  • Identify events/conditions that raise substantial doubt (recurring losses, negative cash flows, overdue payables, covenant breaches).
  • Evaluate management’s plans (financing, cost reductions, asset sales) and their feasibility.
  • Consider period of evaluation and subsequent events; determine report effects (GC section/emphasis or modification if inadequate disclosure).

5) Inventory: Existence & Valuation

  • Observe physical counts; test tags; perform roll‑forward/roll‑back if not at year‑end; consider cycle counts.
  • Price testing to vendor docs/BOM; test standard cost updates; evaluate overhead rates and variances.
  • Assess obsolescence (aging, slow‑moving, NRV tests) and consignment/rights.

6) Financial Instruments & Cash

  • Confirm banks and investments; bank recs testing; cutoff for cash receipts/disbursements.
  • For derivatives: obtain trade confirms, pricing sources, hedge documentation and effectiveness assessments.
  • Classification and measurement (held‑to‑maturity, FVOCI/FVTPL), impairment/expected credit losses.

7) Leases (ASC 842)

  • Completeness: search for embedded leases (service contracts, outsourcing); compare to fixed asset registry and rent expense analytics.
  • Recompute ROU assets and lease liabilities; evaluate discount rate and lease term (renewal options).
  • Evaluate classification, modifications, and required disclosures.

8) Income Taxes (ASC 740)

  • Reconcile tax provision to trial balance; test current and deferred tax computations.
  • Evaluate valuation allowance and uncertain tax positions; review returns and correspondence with authorities.
  • Check presentation/disclosure (rate reconciliation, NOLs, UTP roll‑forward).

9) Subsequent Events & Contingencies (AU‑C 560/450)

  • Read minutes through the report date; inquire of legal counsel; scan general ledger post‑year‑end; inspect subsequent receipts on AR and cash disbursements for liabilities.
  • Evaluate whether events require adjustment or disclosure; obtain updated management representations.

10) Service Organizations (SOC 1®)

  • Identify processes outsourced (payroll, revenue platforms); obtain SOC 1® report.
  • Check coverage period and complementary user‑entity controls; design procedures to test those controls at the user entity.
  • Consider bridge letters for gaps; evaluate deviations reported by the service auditor.

11) Reliance on IT & System‑Generated Information

  • Identify key reports used as evidence; test report logic or rely on tested app controls/ITGCs.
  • Assess end‑user computing (spreadsheets) risks and perform validation of formulas/data integrity.

12) Opening Balances & Initial Audits (AU‑C 510)

  • Obtain evidence that opening balances contain no material misstatements affecting the current period.
  • Evaluate consistency of accounting policies; review predecessor auditor communications when available.

14) Documentation

  • Risk rationale, affected assertions, and chosen procedures (with N/T/E and sampling basis).
  • Results, evaluation versus materiality/performance materiality, and required communications.
  • Conclusions, including implications for the report and subsequent period planning.

15) Quick Checks

  1. Presumed fraud risk area? Revenue recognition (and management override).
  2. Best procedure for unrecorded liabilities? Subsequent disbursement testing.
  3. What validates a key report used as evidence? Test of report logic or reliance on effective app controls/ITGCs.
  4. When do you reference component auditors/specialists in the report? Only when required/permitted by standards or law.
