Licensing & Disciplinary Systems

Learning Objectives

  • Understand & explain the role and authority of state boards of accountancy (licensing, renewal, discipline).
  • Identify key players and what they regulate: state boards, National Association of State Boards of Accountancy (NASBA), American Institute of CPAs (AICPA), Public Company Accounting Oversight Board (PCAOB), and Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC).
  • Apply the concepts of initial licensure (education‑exam‑experience‑ethics), continuing professional education (CPE), and mobility/reciprocity under substantial equivalency.
  • Describe a typical disciplinary process (complaint → investigation → consent order/hearing → sanctions → appeal) and match sanctions to scenarios.

At a Glance

Who regulates what?

BodyWhat they do
State BoardIssues/renews CPA licenses; sets state rules (within statute); enforces discipline (reprimand, fine, probation, suspension, revocation).
NASBACoordinates among boards; supports Uniform Accountancy Act (UAA) model rules; National Candidate Database.

PCAOBRegisters and inspects audit firms of SEC issuers; may sanction firms/individuals.
SECOversight of public company reporting; can bar practitioners from practicing before the SEC.

Licensure basics (varies by state)

  • Education Typical 150‑hour requirement with accounting/business core.
  • Exam Uniform CPA Examination.
  • Experience Supervised experience (duration/setting varies).
  • Ethics Separate ethics exam/course in many states.
  • CPE Ongoing hours (e.g., 120/3 yrs with annual/subject‑area rules; check state).
  • Mobility “Substantial equivalency” allows practice across state lines; follow host state rules for firm permits/attest.
Exam tip: State boards license & discipline. AICPA sets professional ethics for members. PCAOB/SEC regulate public company audits. When a scenario mixes them up, anchor on who has legal authority over the license → the state board.

Disciplinary Process — What Happens?

StageWhat to remember
ComplaintFiled by client, peer, regulator, or through self‑reporting of certain events (e.g., criminal conviction). Board notifies the licensee.
InvestigationBoard/AG staff requests documents, interviews witnesses. Licensee can respond. Confidential until formal action.
ResolutionConsent order (settlement) or proceed to an administrative hearing with due process.
SanctionsRemedial education, fine, reprimand, probation with monitoring, suspension, or revocation; may include reporting to NASBA/AICPA/PCAOB/SEC as applicable.
AppealLicensee may seek reconsideration or judicial review per state law.
Exam tip: Know which sanction is likely: e.g., minor CPE shortfall → remediation/fine; firm audit failures on public clients → PCAOB + state board actions; criminal fraud → revocation.

Examples — Map scenario to action

ScenarioLikely outcome
CPA fails to meet CPE by year‑end but completes shortage within 60 days after notice. Board may impose fine and require remedial CPE; license generally remains active after compliance.
CPA convicted of a felony unrelated to work. Must report to the board (in many states). Board can suspend or revoke after hearing based on moral turpitude/statutory criteria.
Public company audit with quality deficiencies found by PCAOB inspection. PCAOB can sanction firm/partners; state board may also discipline (separate jurisdiction). SEC orders possible for egregious issues.
CPA signs return while license is lapsed. Practicing without a valid license → discipline (fines/suspension) and possible cease‑and‑desist.
Conflict of interest not disclosed; no client consent. Violation of AICPA Code and likely state rule → board discipline; AICPA can also sanction members.

Tricky Areas (Exam Tips)

  • License vs. Membership Only the state board can suspend/revoke your CPA license. AICPA can only affect membership.
  • Mobility “Substantial equivalency” ≠ automatic for attest/firm permits — check host‑state requirements.
  • Confidentiality vs. Privilege CPAs have a duty of confidentiality; privilege is limited and varies by state (and is different from attorney‑client).
  • Multiple regulators One action can trigger others (e.g., PCAOB finding → state board inquiry). Think “parallel consequences.”
  • Due process Complaints → notice → opportunity to respond/hearing; consent orders resolve many cases without a hearing.

Quick Practice — 2 Multiple‑Choice Questions (MCQs)

  1. Which body can revoke a CPA’s license to practice?
    A. AICPA   B. NASBA   C. State Board of Accountancy   D. PCAOB
    Answer: C — Only the state board has licensing authority.
  2. A CPA licensed in State A wants to perform attest services in State B under mobility rules. What is the best first step?
    A. Rely on AICPA membership for permission   B. Check State B’s firm/attest permit requirements   C. File with the SEC   D. Register with NASBA for a PTIN
    Answer: B — Mobility often covers non‑attest services; attest may require a host‑state firm permit or notice filing.
