Information Systems

Learning Objectives

  • Explain core IT infrastructure: operating systems, servers, network components, endpoints.
  • Differentiate cloud models (IaaS, PaaS, SaaS) and deployment (public, private, hybrid).
  • Summarize cloud service provider roles & the shared‑responsibility model.
  • Explain how the COSO framework addresses cloud governance.
  • Summarize ERP/accounting information systems and how modules interact.
  • Apply COSO to blockchain risks and controls.
  • Determine changes to business processes (e.g., RPA, outsourcing) to improve performance.
  • Reconcile actual process steps and data/tech used to the documented process (flowchart/BPMN).
  • Recall business continuity/disaster recovery concepts; compute availability.
  • Determine appropriate backup types and evaluate RPO/RTO.
  • Explain change management purpose, environments (dev/test/stage/prod), testing, and conversion methods.
  • Perform walkthroughs and identify SOC 2® Trust Services Criteria (processing integrity/availability) control gaps.
Exam Tip: Don’t mix up RPO (max acceptable data loss) with RTO (max acceptable downtime). Availability % is about uptime, not throughput.

3) Availability, DR & Backups

Availability calculation

30‑day month has 43,200 minutes. If downtime = 95 minutes, availability =

(Total − Downtime) ÷ Total = 99.7801%

Exam Tip: “Five nines” (99.999%) allows ~5.26 minutes of downtime per year.

RPO / RTO scenario

  • Backups every 120 minutes → RPO = 120 minutes.
  • Last backup was 150 minutes ago → NOT within RPO.
  • Recovery steps time = 205 minutes; RTO target = 240 minutes → Meets RTO.
Tricky: Incremental vs. differential backups change restore time; differentials speed recovery but grow each day.

4) Change Management

Core concepts

  • Purpose: ensure only authorized, tested, approved, and documented changes reach production.
  • Environments: dev → test/QA → stage → prod (segregated access).
  • Testing: unit, integration, system, user acceptance, regression; evidence retained.
  • Conversion: direct, parallel, phased, pilot — pick based on risk tolerance.
  • Patch management: inventory, risk rank (CVSS), test, approve, deploy, verify.

Walkthrough (what to inspect)

  1. Change ticket → business approval → technical review.
  2. Evidence of testing and segregation (no developers promoting to prod).
  3. Back‑out plan and monitoring post‑release.
  4. SOC 2®: processing integrity and availability criteria mapping.

Practice Questions

1. SLA compliance — availability (calculation)

A SaaS vendor’s SLA requires 99.95% monthly availability. In a 31‑day month the system was down 120 minutes. Is the SLA met?

Show answer & explanation
Total minutes = 31×24×60 = 44,640. Availability = (44,640 − 120) ÷ 44,640 = 99.731% ⇒ Not met (shortfall ≈ 0.219%). Penalty/credit may apply per contract.
2. Backups & RPO (calculation)

Backups occur every 3 hours. A failure happens at 5:36 PM; last successful backup finished at 3:00 PM. What is the actual data loss and does it meet an RPO of 3 hours?

Show answer & explanation
Time since backup = 2 hours 36 minutes = 156 minutes < 180 minutes (RPO). Meets RPO; expected data loss ≤ last 156 minutes of transactions.
3. Shared responsibility

In a PaaS arrangement, who is primarily responsible for database patching and who owns application user access?

Show answer & explanation
Provider patches platform components (DB engine). The customer owns application user access and data controls.
4. AIS journal entry control (calculation)

An ERP posts the following cash disbursement entry: Dr A/P $5,000, Cr Cash $4,900, Cr Inventory $100. Is the entry balanced?

Show answer & explanation
Debits = $5,000; Credits = $5,000 → Balanced. (Perpetual system reduces Inventory for the purchase discount.)
5. Change management — environment segregation

Which control failure is most severe?

  1. Developers lack access to production logs.
  2. Developers can deploy to production using a break‑glass account without approval.
  3. Users test only in dev, not QA.
  4. Tickets omit business owner sign‑off.
Show answer & explanation
B. Direct developer access to deploy to production without approval violates segregation of duties and change approval; it presents the highest risk of unauthorized changes.
