Terms of Engagement

Learning Objectives

  • State the preconditions for an audit and when an engagement should be declined or withdrawn.
  • Identify required and common elements of an engagement letter and how terms differ for recurring vs initial audits.
  • Explain management’s responsibilities vs the auditor’s responsibilities and the financial reporting framework.
  • Recognize acceptance/continuance considerations, including independence, ethical and resource requirements.

1) Preconditions for an Audit

  • Acceptable financial reporting framework (FRF).
  • Management responsibilities acknowledged: preparation/presentation of FS; design, implementation, and maintenance of relevant controls; providing access to information and persons within the entity.
  • Ethics/independence: firm concludes independence will be maintained; consider nonattest services and safeguards.
Decline or withdraw if: pervasive scope limitation; unacceptable FRF; management lacks integrity; independence impaired; or the firm lacks competence/resources.

2) Agreeing on Terms — Engagement Letter

Purpose: record the agreed terms to reduce misunderstanding and support quality execution.

  • Objective & scope of the audit (including reference to the framework).
  • Responsibilities of the auditor (obtain reasonable assurance, comply with GAAS/PCAOB, maintain independence, communicate with TCWG).
  • Responsibilities of management (FS, internal control, access to information, written representations).
  • Form and content of reports expected (FS opinion and any other reports).
  • Other terms as applicable: timing, specialists, component auditors, reliance on internal auditors, use of service organizations, fees and billing, electronic communication, embargo of report, etc.
Mini‑example

Client requests the auditor to omit inventory observation due to safety rules. The auditor documents the request and explains that a scope limitation would likely preclude an unmodified opinion; if alternative procedures aren’t feasible, the engagement may be declined.

3) Initial vs Recurring Audits

  • Recurring: reconsider need to remind or revise the letter if circumstances change (e.g., new FRF, change in reporting, miscommunication in prior period).
  • Initial: perform client acceptance procedures; communicate with predecessor auditor (with client permission); consider opening balances and comparatives; evaluate independence threats from assisting in FS preparation.
Tip: A new letter is recommended when there’s a significant change in scope, reporting, or entity circumstances.

4) Acceptance & Continuance

  • Evaluate integrity of management and those charged with governance.
  • Confirm ability to comply with ethical requirements, including independence.
  • Assess firm competence and resources, including specialists where needed.
  • Consider engagement risk including significant related parties, complex estimates, or going concern indicators.
Quality control link: Document key acceptance/continuance conclusions and approvals per firm policies.

5) Changes in Engagement (Audit → Review/Compilation)

Allowable only if: justified by a change in circumstances, a misunderstanding of services, or restriction beyond the auditor’s control—and not to mislead users.

  • If change is appropriate: issue report for the revised engagement; do not refer to original audit procedures.
  • If inappropriate to change: consider withdrawing and communicating to TCWG; do not issue a review/compilation report.
Mini‑example

After year‑end, lender drops covenant requirement for audited FS. Client requests a review instead. If no misrepresentation risk exists and scope is sufficient for a review, a change may be acceptable.

6) Quick Checks

  1. List one precondition that must be present before accepting an audit: Management acknowledges responsibility for the FS and access to information.
  2. What section of the engagement letter sets expectation about auditor’s level of assurance? Objective & scope / form of report.
  3. When is it acceptable to change from an audit to a review? When justified by changed circumstances or misunderstanding, not to mislead users.
  4. One reason to decline an engagement at acceptance: Independence is impaired.
