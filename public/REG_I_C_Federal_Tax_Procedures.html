Federal Tax Procedures

Learning Objectives

  • Explain the audit and appeals process for tax matters and the levels of the judicial process (U.S. Tax Court, U.S. District Court, U.S. Court of Federal Claims; appellate review).
  • Summarize when disclosure is required for a tax position and what forms are used; recall foreign account reporting requirements.
  • Identify common taxpayer penalties and apply them to scenarios.
  • Recall the authoritative hierarchy for federal tax purposes.

At a Glance

Audit → Appeals → Court (high level)

StageKey points
ExaminationCorrespondence, office, or field. Information Document Requests (IDRs). At conclusion, IRS issues report/30‑day letter (proposed changes).
AppealsFile within 30 days. Independent Office of Appeals seeks settlements based on hazards of litigation.
Notice of DeficiencyIf no agreement → 90‑day letter. File in U.S. Tax Court within 90 days to stop assessment (no prepayment).
Other forumsPay then sue in U.S. District Court (jury available) or U.S. Court of Federal Claims (no jury).
Exam tip: Tax Court is a prepayment forum. District Court/Court of Federal Claims require full payment first.

Substantiation & Disclosure

  • Adequate records Keep receipts, logs, or other evidence. For meals/travel, contemporaneous logs are expected.
  • Disclosure forms Form 8275 (insufficient authority) and Form 8275‑R (contrary to regulation).
  • Foreign accounts FBAR (FinCEN Form 114) if aggregate foreign financial accounts exceed $10,000 at any time in the year. FATCA (Form 8938) thresholds vary by status/residency — check current instructions.
Exam tip: Disclosure can help avoid certain preparer penalties but does not fix willful taxpayer fraud.

Statute of Limitations (SOL)

SituationAssessment SOLNotes
General rule3 yearsFrom the later of due date or filing date.
Substantial omission of income6 yearsGenerally >25% omission of gross income (special rules apply for foreign assets).
Fraud or no returnUnlimitedNo statute bars assessment.
Refund claimsLater of 3 years from filing or 2 years from paymentFile Form 1040‑X/1120‑X within the period.
Exam tip: If a 90‑day letter is issued, the SOL is tolled while the petition is pending in Tax Court.

Judicial Forums & Authoritative Hierarchy

Forum selection

ForumPay first?Other features
U.S. Tax CourtNoNationwide; judge (no jury). Specialized in tax. Good for cash‑constrained clients.
U.S. District CourtYesLocal venue; jury available; must fully pay and file refund claim first.
U.S. Court of Federal ClaimsYesNationwide; no jury; often used for large claims against the U.S.

Examples — Apply to scenarios

ScenarioAnswer
Taxpayer cannot afford to pay a deficiency but wants to challenge the IRS determination. File a petition in U.S. Tax Court within 90 days of the notice of deficiency (no prepayment required).
Client has three foreign bank accounts: $4,000, $3,500, and $3,000 peak balances. Aggregate is $10,500 > $10,000 → FBAR required (FinCEN 114). Consider FATCA Form 8938 if applicable to overall assets.
Return filed timely. Two years later, IRS asserts a deficiency due to 30% omitted income the IRS discovered. Six‑year SOL applies for substantial omission of gross income. Assessment is generally still timely.
Taxpayer paid a disputed amount last year and now wants to litigate with a jury. File a refund claim, then sue in U.S. District Court after denial or 6 months of IRS inaction.

Tricky Areas (Exam Tips)

  • 30‑ vs 90‑day letters 30‑day lets you go to Appeals; 90‑day is the statutory notice for Tax Court.
  • Burden of proof Usually on the taxpayer; can shift if IRS introduces new matter or with credible evidence plus substantiation.
  • Disclosure vs. authority Disclosure cures some preparer penalties (reasonable basis), but not taxpayer fraud penalties.
  • Refund claim clock The “later of 3 years from filing or 2 years from payment” rule is a classic distractor.

Quick Practice — 3 Multiple‑Choice Questions (MCQs)

  1. Which forum allows a taxpayer to contest a deficiency without first paying it?
    A. U.S. District Court   B. U.S. Court of Federal Claims   C. U.S. Tax Court   D. State Court
    Answer: C — U.S. Tax Court is a prepayment forum.
  2. Which statement about the statute of limitations is correct?
    A. It is 3 years in all cases   B. It is 6 years for substantial omission of gross income   C. It is 1 year if no return is filed   D. It always starts on the filing date
    Answer: B — 6 years for substantial omission; unlimited for fraud/no return; runs from later of due date or filing.
  3. When is an FBAR required?
    A. Any foreign account   B. When any single account exceeds $10,000   C. When the aggregate value of foreign accounts exceeds $10,000 at any time in the year   D. Only when Form 8938 is filed
    Answer: C — The threshold is an aggregate $10,000 at any time during the year.
