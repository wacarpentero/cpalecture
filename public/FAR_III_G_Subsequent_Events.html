Subsequent Events

Learning Objectives

  • Identify subsequent events and recall the appropriate accounting treatment (recognized vs. nonrecognized).
  • Calculate required adjustments to financial statements and/or the note disclosures based on identified subsequent events.
  • Derive the impact to financial statements and required note disclosures, including the period for evaluation and reissuance considerations.

At a Glance

TypeDefinitionAction
Recognized (Type I) Provides additional evidence about conditions that existed at the balance sheet date. Adjust the financial statements (amounts/estimates).
Nonrecognized (Type II) Relates to conditions that arose after the balance sheet date. Disclose nature and estimate of financial effect (or state it cannot be estimated).
Evaluation period From balance sheet date to the date the FS are available to be issued (or issued for SEC filers). Disclose the date through which subsequent events were evaluated.
Reissuance When reissuing FS, evaluate subsequent events through the reissuance date for recognition/disclosure in the reissued FS. Do not recognize events that occurred between original issuance and reissuance if not appropriate; disclose if necessary.

1) Recognized Event — Litigation Settlement (Type I)

FactsConclusion & Computation
A lawsuit existed as of 12/31/X1. At 12/31, the company accrued $60,000 for loss. On 2/10/X2 (before FS issued), the case settles for $85,000. Settlement gives more evidence about the liability existing at 12/31/X1 → Type I (recognized). Increase the accrual by $25,000.

Entry (to adjust X1 FS)

  • DrLitigation loss (expense)$25,000
  • CrAccrued litigation liability($25,000)

2) Recognized Event — Customer Bankruptcy (Type I)

FactsConclusion & Computation
Accounts receivable from a major customer totals $500,000 at 12/31/X1 with an existing allowance of $10,000. On 1/20/X2, customer files for bankruptcy; expected recovery is 20%. The bankruptcy confirms pre‑existing financial difficulty → Type I. Required ending allowance = 80% × 500,000 = $400,000 → additional bad debt expense required = $390,000.

Entry (to adjust X1 FS)

  • DrBad debt expense$390,000
  • CrAllowance for doubtful accounts($390,000)

3) Nonrecognized Event — Plant Fire After Year‑End (Type II)

FactsConclusion & Disclosure
On 2/05/X2, a fire destroys a manufacturing facility. The facility was fully operational at 12/31/X1. Event arose after the balance sheet date → Type II. No adjustment to X1 amounts. Disclose the nature of the event and an estimate of the financial effect (or state it cannot be estimated).
Disclosure Example language: “On February 5, 20X2, a fire damaged the Company’s Springfield plant. The Company is assessing insurance recoveries; the estimated net loss ranges from $0 to $2.0 million.”

4) Nonrecognized Event — Equity Issuance (Type II)

FactsConclusion
On 1/15/X2, the Company issues common stock for cash. No equity issuance plans existed at 12/31/X1. New condition after year‑end → Type II. Disclose nature and proceeds; no adjustment to 12/31/X1 equity.

5) Going Concern Considerations — Subsequent Events

ScenarioEffect
Negative events occur after year‑end that alleviate substantial doubt raised at 12/31. Still evaluate going concern as of 12/31 using information available through issuance; if doubt is alleviated by subsequent events, disclose basis and impact.
Adverse post‑year‑end events create substantial doubt not present at 12/31. Generally a Type II disclosure (no adjustment) unless the condition existed at year‑end.

Quick Recap

TopicCore points
Recognized vs. Nonrecognized Type I (pre‑existing conditions) → adjust; Type II (new conditions) → disclose.
Evaluation window From balance sheet date through the date FS are available to be issued; disclose this date.
