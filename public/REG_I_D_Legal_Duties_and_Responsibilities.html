Legal Duties & Responsibilities

Learning Objectives

  • Summarize a tax return preparer’s common‑law duties to clients and third parties (care, loyalty, contractual duties) and related liabilities.
  • Identify scenarios that violate those duties (negligence, fraud, breach of contract) and the impact on third‑party liability.
  • Summarize rules for privileged communications, confidentiality, and privacy in tax practice.
  • Identify when communications are privileged and when disclosure/consent is required instead.

At a Glance

Core common‑law duties

  • Duty of care Perform with the skill and prudence of a reasonably competent CPA; follow professional standards and make reasonable inquiries.
  • Duty of loyalty Avoid conflicts; obtain informed written consent when permitted.
  • Contract Honor engagement terms (scope, deliverables, deadlines); communicate limitations.

Liability to third parties for negligence (state law tests)

TestWho can sue?
Privity (e.g., Ultramares)Only client in privity.
Near‑privity (e.g., Credit Alliance)Known third party relied upon, accountant aware and intended reliance.
Restatement (Section 552)Limited, intended class of third parties.
Foreseeability (minority)Any foreseeable third party — broadest exposure.

Confidentiality, privacy, and privilege

  • Confidentiality AICPA Code & state rules: don’t disclose client info without consent or legal duty.
  • Tax confidentiality statutes Internal Revenue Code §§7216/6713 restrict tax return information use/disclosure by preparers; written consent often required.
  • Privacy/Safeguards Gramm‑Leach‑Bliley Act (GLBA) & FTC Safeguards Rule: maintain information security programs.
  • Privilege Attorney‑client privilege extends to federally authorized tax practitioners (IRC §7525) for non‑criminal tax matters (not return prep, not state matters; subject to crime‑fraud exception).
Exam tip: Privileged ≠ confidential. Confidential client info may still be compelled; privilege protects communications for legal advice within its limits.

Examples — Duties & Liability

ScenarioAnalysis & outcome
Engagement letter states compilation only. CPA performs minimal inquiry yet issues a “review‑level” conclusion by mistake. Bank relied on it. Duty of care breached; misrepresentation. Third‑party exposure depends on jurisdictional test (near‑privity/Restatement). Proper labeling and scope communication avoid this.
CPA ignores obvious inconsistencies in client records and claims a large deduction. Failure to exercise due care; negligence (and possibly preparer penalties). If intentional → fraud (scienter, reliance, damages).
CPA represents two spouses with diverging interests in an audit without consent. Conflict of interest (duty of loyalty). Must disclose and obtain informed written consent, or withdraw.
CPA shares client tax data with a software vendor for marketing without consent. Violates IRC §§7216/6713; requires prior written consent for non‑tax‑administration uses. Also a confidentiality breach.

Exam tip: State evidentiary privileges vary. The REG exam focuses on the federal tax practitioner privilege (IRC §7525) and classic attorney‑client principles.

Tricky Areas (Exam Tips)

  • Privilege scope §7525 applies only to non‑criminal federal tax matters. Not state tax cases; not return preparation.
  • Third‑party tests Link the fact pattern to the correct standard: privity → client only; near‑privity → known third party; Restatement → intended class; foreseeability → broad.
  • Consent mechanics For §§7216/6713, use written, knowing, voluntary consents; disclose purpose and recipients; retain per rules.
  • Engagement letters Define scope, responsibilities, reliance limits, and dispute resolution—often saves points on “violation?” questions.
  • Negligence elements Duty, breach, causation, damages—check each box.

Quick Practice — 3 Multiple‑Choice Questions (MCQs)

  1. Which standard most broadly exposes a CPA to third‑party negligence claims?
    A. Privity   B. Near‑privity   C. Restatement (Section 552)   D. Foreseeability
    Answer: D — Foreseeability permits any foreseeable third party to sue.
  2. Which communication is protected by the federal tax practitioner privilege (IRC §7525)?
    A. A memo used solely to prepare a return   B. A discussion with an attorney‑directed CPA about legal strategy in a civil IRS exam   C. Notes planning a cash‑skimming scheme   D. Advice in a state tax audit
    Answer: B — Non‑criminal federal tax matter under attorney direction.
  3. A CPA emails marketing materials to clients using their tax return information without written consent. What rule is likely violated?
    A. GLBA only   B. IRC §7525   C. IRC §§7216/6713   D. Work‑product doctrine
    Answer: C — Unauthorized use/disclosure of tax return information.
