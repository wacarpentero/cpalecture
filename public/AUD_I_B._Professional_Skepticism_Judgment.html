Professional Skepticism & Professional Judgment

Learning Objectives
  • Explain and apply the concepts of professional skepticism and professional judgment.
  • Identify common biases, threats, and incentives that impede skepticism and sound judgment.
  • Use a practical, repeatable judgment framework and document conclusions clearly.

1) Core Concepts

1 Professional Skepticism

Mindset: a questioning mind, alertness to fraud/error indicators, and a critical assessment of audit evidence. Maintain neutrality—neither assume honesty nor dishonesty. Apply throughout planning, fieldwork, and reporting.

Exam Tip: When two pieces of evidence conflict, the skeptical response is to investigate the contradiction, reassess risk, and obtain more reliable evidence.

2 Professional Judgment

Definition: the informed, deliberate process of reaching audit conclusions when standards permit choices (e.g., materiality, sample size, nature/timing of procedures, evaluation of misstatements).

Qualities: competence, independence, objectivity, awareness of biases, consultation, and documentation that makes the logic auditable.

2) Biases & Impediments to Skepticism

Know the usual suspects—expect these to appear in MCQs disguised in short scenarios:

  • Anchoring: staying too close to prior‑year conclusions (e.g., reusing last year’s allowance percentage without updating inputs).
  • Confirmation bias: seeking evidence that supports management’s story while overlooking contradictory data.
  • Availability/recency: overweighting the most recent or memorable client events.
  • Overconfidence: overestimating your estimate accuracy or control design effectiveness.
  • Halo effect: allowing positive views of management to seep into evidence evaluation.
  • Incentive threats: deadline pressure, fear of losing the client, or budget constraints that nudge toward premature sign‑off.
Red Flag Patterns: significant estimates with management bias indicators, unusual or one‑off transactions near period‑end, revenue recognized without strong corroboration, and evidence coming solely from the client’s internal sources.

3) A Practical Judgment Framework

  1. Define the issue (assertion & risk). What could be misstated and how?
  2. Gather & triage evidence: prioritize reliability (external & direct > internal & indirect); resolve contradictions.
  3. Generate alternatives: list plausible explanations and procedures for each.
  4. Debias: name your bias risk (e.g., anchoring) and perform a counter‑analysis (e.g., independent expectation).
  5. Conclude & document: state your conclusion, why it’s reasonable, what would change it, and who you consulted.
Documentation cue: An experienced auditor with no prior connection should be able to re‑perform your logic from the file alone.

4) Mini‑Examples (Exam‑style)

Revenue Cutoff

Facts: Shipments on 12/30 terms FOB shipping point; some invoices dated 01/02. Prior year cutoff issues existed.

Skeptical move: Inspect BOLs and carrier tracking from 12/29–01/03; confirm selected customers; reconcile to GL. Consider increased control risk and expanded sample at year‑end.

Why?

Conflicting dates suggest risk of premature or deferred recognition. External evidence (carrier confirms) outweighs internal documents.

Estimate: Allowance for Credit Losses

Facts: Management reused last year’s loss rate despite new customer mix and deteriorating macro indicators.

Skeptical move: Build an independent expectation using aging, roll‑rates, and post‑year‑end cash; challenge model inputs; involve a specialist if complexity is high.

Bias to watch

Anchoring and confirmation—management clings to prior inputs; auditor must rebut with independent data.

Related‑Party Transaction

Facts: Material year‑end sale to an entity with a director overlap; unusual terms.

Skeptical move: Read minutes/filings; obtain contract directly from the counterparty; evaluate substance over form; consider disclosure adequacy and revenue criteria.

Why?

Heightened fraud risk and pressure to meet targets warrant higher‑reliability evidence from outside the entity.

Going Concern Signals

Facts: Tight liquidity, covenant breaches cured by waivers received after year‑end.

Skeptical move: Inspect signed waivers from lenders; assess mitigating plans’ feasibility (budgets, committed financing) and whether substantial doubt remains for one year after issuance.

5) Quick Checks

  1. Most skeptical evidence? Third‑party confirmations obtained directly by the auditor vs. internally generated schedules.
  2. Conflict in evidence? Investigate; increase risk assessment; obtain more persuasive evidence.
  3. Bias spotted? Document it and perform targeted counter‑procedures.
  4. Judgment made? Write: issue → alternatives → rationale → conclusion → sensitivity (what could change it).

6) Practice Questions (with answers)

  1. An appropriate skeptical response to contradictory evidence is to:
    a) accept the more recent item; b) average both; c) obtain additional, more reliable evidence and reassess risk; d) disregard the contradiction as immaterial.
    Answer

    c) Obtain more reliable evidence and reassess risk.

  2. Which situation most threatens skepticism?
    a) Tight reporting deadline and significant scope change; b) Inexperienced staff paired with strong supervision; c) High control reliance with robust tests of controls; d) Use of external confirmations.
    Answer

    a) Time/budget pressure is a classic incentive threat.

  3. Professional judgment is most clearly required when:
    a) Copying prior‑year programs; b) Selecting tolerable misstatement; c) Footing a schedule; d) Tying out confirmations.
    Answer

    b) Materiality and tolerable misstatement are judgment calls.

  4. Which bias best describes favoring management’s explanation after a long relationship?
    a) Availability; b) Overconfidence; c) Halo/affinity; d) Anchoring.
    Answer

    c) Halo/affinity can blunt skepticism.

  5. The first skeptical action when a key invoice looks altered is to:
    a) Ask the controller to reprint it; b) Increase sample size; c) Obtain the document directly from the third party; d) Note it for management letter only.
    Answer

    c) Seek independent, higher‑reliability evidence.
