Accounting Changes & Error Corrections

Learning Objectives

  • Calculate the required adjustment to the financial statements for an accounting change (change in accounting principle or change in accounting estimate) or an error correction, and determine whether it requires prospective or retrospective application.
  • Derive the impact to the financial statements and related note disclosures of an identified accounting change or an error correction.

At a Glance

TypeWhat it isHow to account
Change in accounting principle Switching from one acceptable principle to another (e.g., FIFO to weighted‑average). Retrospective: restate prior periods and adjust beginning retained earnings for earliest presented period. Exception: change to LIFO is prospective due to impracticability.
Change in estimate New information changes amounts (e.g., useful life, salvage value, expected credit losses). Prospective: affect current and future periods only. A depreciation method change is treated as a change in estimate made with a change in principle → prospective.
Change in reporting entity Changing the group of entities reported (e.g., consolidated vs. separate). Retrospective restatement to present all periods as if the new entity existed.
Error correction Mathematical mistakes, misapplication of principles, or oversight of facts. Prior‑period adjustment: restate prior periods and adjust beginning retained earnings net of tax.

1) Change in Accounting Principle — Retrospective

Example 1 — FIFO to Weighted‑Average for Inventory

DataAmount
Ending inventory at 12/31/X2 under FIFO$260,000
Ending inventory at 12/31/X2 under weighted‑average$245,000
Income tax rate25%

Cumulative effect at 1/1/X3 (beginning of year of change) reduces inventory by $15,000 and retained earnings net of tax.

Opening adjustment at 1/1/X3

Purpose: record the cumulative‑effect adjustment for a change in accounting principle at the beginning of the period of change (comparatives restated).
  • DrRetained earnings$11,250
  • DrDeferred tax asset$3,750
  • CrInventory($15,000)
Present comparative financial statements as if weighted‑average had always been used. Disclose the nature and quantitative effects.

2) Prospective Changes in Estimate

Example 2 — Useful life revision for Equipment

FactsComputation
Equipment cost $200,000; straight‑line over 10 years, no salvage. After 3 full years, remaining life is revised to 5 more years. Accumulated depreciation after 3 years = $60,000 ⇒ carrying amount at 1/1/X4 = $140,000 ⇒ new depreciation = $140,000 ÷ 5 = $28,000 per year from X4 forward.

Example 3 — Change in Depreciation Method (Straight‑line to Double‑declining)

FactsComputation
Cost $120,000; straight‑line 10 years, no salvage. After 3 years, switch to double‑declining balance at start of Year 4. SL depreciation = $12,000 per year ⇒ accumulated after 3 years = $36,000 ⇒ carrying at 1/1/Y4 = $84,000. DDB rate = 2 ÷ 10 = 20% ⇒ Year‑4 depreciation = 20% × $84,000 = $16,800.

Record current‑year depreciation (prospective)

  • DrDepreciation expense$16,800
  • CrAccumulated depreciation($16,800)

3) Error Corrections — Prior‑Period Adjustment

Example 4 — Expense that should have been Capitalized (Prior Year)

FactsComputation
In X2, $50,000 for equipment installation was expensed. It should have been capitalized and depreciated over 10 years straight‑line, no salvage. Correction is made in X4 financial statements (tax rate 25%). Correct SL depreciation for X2–X3 = $50,000 × 2/10 = $10,000.
Net book value at 1/1/X4 should be $40,000.

Opening adjustment at 1/1/X4

  • DrEquipment$50,000
  • CrAccumulated depreciation($10,000)
  • CrRetained earnings (net of tax)($30,000)
  • DrDeferred tax asset$10,000

Example 5 — Revenue cut‑off error (accrued revenue omitted prior year)

Facts
Services of $40,000 were performed on 12/30/X2 but billed in X3. No revenue or receivable was recorded in X2. Tax rate 25%.

Opening correction at 1/1/X3

  • DrAccounts receivable$40,000
  • CrRetained earnings (net of tax)($30,000)
  • CrDeferred tax liability($10,000)

4) Change in Reporting Entity — Retrospective

Example 6 — Presenting Consolidated Statements

Beginning in X3, the company presents consolidated financial statements including a previously unconsolidated subsidiary. Restate prior periods to present all periods on a consistent basis.

No direct retained earnings entry is typically required apart from normal consolidation adjustments; the comparative financial statements are restated. Include a disclosure describing the change and that prior periods were revised.

Additional Scenarios (common twists)

Example 7 — Change in principle to LIFO (impracticability) — Prospective

What to doNotes
Start a new LIFO layer structure as of 1/1/X3; compute cost of goods sold under LIFO for X3 forward. Disclose the nature of the change and that prior periods were not restated due to impracticability.

Example 8 — Change in estimate: Bad‑debt rate revision

FactsComputation
Allowance method. Prior policy estimated 2% of ending receivables. New information at 12/31/X3 supports 3%. Ending receivables $900,000. Allowance balance (credit) before adjustment $12,000. Required allowance = $900,000 × 3% = $27,000 ⇒ adjustment needed = $27,000 − $12,000 = $15,000 bad‑debt expense in X3.
  • DrBad‑debt expense$15,000
  • CrAllowance for credit losses($15,000)

Example 9 — Inventory overstatement in prior year and EPS impact

GivenComputation
At 12/31/X2, ending inventory was overstated by $25,000. Tax rate 25%. Weighted‑average shares for X2 were 50,000. Reported X2 net income was $200,000; reported basic EPS was $4.00. After correction, cost of goods sold increases $25,000; net income decreases by $18,750 ⇒ revised X2 net income = $181,250. Revised basic EPS = $181,250 ÷ 50,000 = $3.63.

Example 10 — Classification error (statement of cash flows)

In X2, $2,000,000 of principal payments on a note were classified as operating activities instead of financing. No effect on net income or retained earnings — restate the X2 statement of cash flows only and disclose the change in classification.

Quick Recap

RuleUse it fast
Principle vs. Estimate Principle → retrospective (adjust beginning retained earnings) except change to LIFO → prospective. Estimate → prospective.
Method change Depreciation method change is a change in estimate made with a change in principle → prospective.
Error Restate prior periods and adjust beginning retained earnings net of tax.
Disclosures Nature and reason; method (prospective/retrospective); line‑item and per‑share effects; amount of beginning retained earnings adjustment.
