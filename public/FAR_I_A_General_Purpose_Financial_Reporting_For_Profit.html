General‑Purpose Financial Reporting: For‑Profit Business Entities

Learning Objectives

  • Explain the objective of general‑purpose financial reporting under U.S. Generally Accepted Accounting Principles (U.S. GAAP) set by the Financial Accounting Standards Board (FASB).
  • Identify the elements of financial statements (assets, liabilities, equity, revenues, expenses, gains, losses, comprehensive income) and the primary statements.
  • Apply the qualitative characteristics of decision‑useful information (relevance and faithful representation; enhancing characteristics).
  • Distinguish measurement bases and the fair value hierarchy, and understand presentation of comprehensive income and earnings per share (EPS) for public entities.

1) Reporting Entity & Financial Statement Presentation

  • A reporting entity is an organization that issues general‑purpose financial statements for external users; present consolidated statements when control exists.
  • Balance sheet classification: Segregate current and noncurrent assets and liabilities; working capital = current assets − current liabilities.
  • Income statement formats: single‑step (all revenues/expenses) or multi‑step (gross profit, income from operations, etc.). No “extraordinary items” under U.S. GAAP.
  • Comprehensive income: one continuous statement or two separate statements (statement of earnings + statement of comprehensive income).
  • Public entities: present basic EPS (and diluted when applicable) on the face of the income statement for income from continuing operations and for net income.

2) Qualitative Characteristics & Materiality

  • Relevance: Predictive value, confirmatory value, and materiality (would it influence user decisions?).
  • Faithful representation: Complete, neutral, and free from error (to the extent practicable).
  • Enhancing: comparability (including consistency), verifiability, timeliness, understandability. Cost constraint applies.
Exam cue: When relevance and faithful representation conflict, consider the purpose of the information and whether disclosure can remedy limitations.

3) Measurement Bases & Fair Value

  • Historical cost / amortized cost: Original transaction price adjusted for allocations (e.g., depreciation, amortization, impairment).
  • Fair value: Exit price from an orderly transaction between market participants at the measurement date. Hierarchy levels 1–3 based on input observability.
  • Present value / expected cash flows: Discounted cash‑flow techniques often used for long‑term receivables/liabilities and impairment testing.
  • Net realizable value: Estimated selling price in the ordinary course of business less costs of completion/disposal (e.g., accounts receivable, inventory LCM for some methods).

4) Other Comprehensive Income (OCI)

  • OCI items bypass net income and accumulate in AOCI within equity until reclassified or settled.
  • Common OCI items for for‑profit entities include: unrealized gains/losses on certain debt securities classified as available‑for‑sale, foreign currency translation adjustments, effective portion of cash flow hedges, and certain pension plan adjustments.
  • Present tax effects for each component or in aggregate; disclose reclassification adjustments from AOCI to net income.

5) Earnings per Share (EPS) — Basics

  • Basic EPS = (Net income − preferred dividends) ÷ weighted‑average common shares outstanding.
  • Diluted EPS reflects the potential dilution from convertible securities, options, warrants, and contingently issuable shares (if they are dilutive).
  • Only public entities present EPS; nonpublic entities generally do not.

Illustrative Examples (selected)

Example 1 — Working Capital & Current Ratio

Data: Current assets $420,000 (including inventory $160,000). Current liabilities $280,000. Long‑term debt $500,000.

Working capital = 420,000 − 280,000 = $140,000.

Current ratio = 420,000 ÷ 280,000 = 1.50.

Long‑term debt does not affect current ratio unless current portion exists.

Example 2 — Comprehensive Income

Data: Net income $250,000. Unrealized gain on available‑for‑sale debt securities $30,000. Cash flow hedge effective gain $12,000. Foreign currency translation loss $5,000. Ignore income taxes.

OCI = 30,000 + 12,000 − 5,000 = $37,000.

Comprehensive income = Net income 250,000 + OCI 37,000 = $287,000.

Presentation: Either in one continuous statement or a separate statement following the income statement.

Example 3 — Basic EPS

Data: Net income $120,000. No preferred stock. 100,000 common shares outstanding on 1/1. On 4/1, the company issued 20,000 new shares for cash. No other changes.

Weighted‑average shares = 100,000 × (3/12) + 120,000 × (9/12) = 25,000 + 90,000 = 115,000.

Basic EPS = 120,000 ÷ 115,000 = $1.04 per share (rounded).

Example 4 — Fair Value Hierarchy

Scenario: A bond investment is valued using a price quoted by a pricing service based on observable market yields for similar bonds.

Inputs are observable but not quoted for the identical instrument → Level 2 fair value measurement (not Level 1).

6) Disclosure Highlights

  • Significant accounting policies (basis of consolidation, revenue recognition methods, inventory cost methods, depreciation, etc.).
  • Fair value measurements by level for recurring and nonrecurring items; valuation techniques and inputs used.
  • Composition of OCI and changes in AOCI by component.
  • Related‑party transactions, contingencies, and subsequent events.

Quick Checks

  1. Which qualitative characteristic includes comparability and timeliness? Enhancing characteristics.
  2. Is a quoted price on an active exchange Level 1 or Level 2? Level 1.
  3. Does comprehensive income include owner transactions? No—owner investments and distributions are excluded.
  4. When is EPS required? For public entities, presented on the face of the income statement.
