Partnerships

Learning Objectives

  • Explain formation rules (§721 nonrecognition; partner §722 outside basis; partnership §723 inside basis; capital vs profits interests).
  • Compute partner outside basis including §752 liabilities (recourse vs nonrecourse) and apply loss limits (basis → at‑risk → passive).
  • Analyze distributions (current vs liquidating; cash and property; marketable securities; §731, §732 basis, and gain triggers).
  • Allocate income with §704(c) when built‑in gain/loss exists; understand hot assets (§751) recharacterization.
  • Identify effects of guaranteed payments, §707 transactions, and sale of a partnership interest (incl. liabilities in amount realized).
  • Recognize when a §754 election allows inside basis step‑up under §§743(b)/734(b).

One‑page map

Formation → §721 no gain/loss; Basis: Partner outside = basis of property + cash − liability relief + share of partnership liabilities (§752); Partnership inside (carryover) = §723 Liabilities → Recourse to those who bear economic risk of loss; Nonrecourse shared by profit ratios (unless minimum gain rules) Loss limits → Basis → At‑Risk → Passive Distributions → Cash gain trigger if cash & marketable securities > outside basis; Property basis to partner = carryover (limited to outside); No partnership gain (exceptions exist) Sales of interest → Amount realized includes cash and debt relief; Hot assets create ordinary income Election → §754 permits buyer‑specific step‑up (§743(b)) on purchase or transferee death K‑1 items → Guaranteed payments are deductible by partnership, ordinary income to recipient, and do not increase basis for the recipient
Order: Basis → At‑Risk → Passive §752 debt matters Hot assets = ordinary

Tricky‑area tips

  • Debt relief upon contribution is a deemed cash distribution to the partner; add back the partner’s share of partnership debt to compute net relief.
  • Outside basis includes both recourse and nonrecourse shares; at‑risk generally excludes nonrecourse (except qualified nonrecourse real estate).
  • Cash distributions trigger gain only when they exceed outside basis; property distributions generally do not cause gain (watch hot assets/marketable securities).
  • §704(c): first allocate built‑in gain/loss to the contributing partner; then split residual per agreement.
  • Sale of an interest: include debt relief in amount realized and carve out hot assets as ordinary income.
  • With a §754 election, only the transferee gets a special basis adjustment; it doesn’t change common inside basis.

Illustrative Examples (computations)

1) Formation with liability relief — computing initial outside basis

Contribute property basis $60,000 (FMV $100,000) with $30,000 recourse debt; 50/50 partners. Deemed cash distribution (debt relief) = $30,000 − $15,000 (partner’s share added back) = $15,000. Initial outside basis = $60,000 − $30,000 + $15,000 = $45,000 (no gain since deemed cash ≤ basis).

2) Basis rollforward during the year

Begin $45,000 + Ordinary income $40,000 − §1231 loss $10,000 − Cash distribution $20,000 + Increase in liabilities $5,000 (Guaranteed payment received $12,000 — does not increase basis) = Ending outside basis $60,000.

3) Cash distribution exceeding basis

Outside basis $60,000; cash distribution $70,000 ⇒ Gain under §731 = $70,000 − $60,000 = $10,000; new basis = $0.

4) Property distribution (no gain)

Outside basis $50,000; partnership distributes land (inside basis $30,000; FMV $80,000). Partner’s basis in land = carryover (limited by outside) = $30,000. New outside basis = $50,000 − $30,000 = $20,000.

5) §704(c) built‑in gain on sale

Contributed asset basis $40,000 (FMV $100,000) sold for $120,000. Total gain = $120,000 − $40,000 = $80,000. Built‑in gain at contribution = $60,000 → allocate to contributor. Residual $20,000 split 50/50 = $10,000 each to both partners.

6) Guaranteed payment impact

Ordinary income before GP $100,000; GP to Partner A $30,000; two equal partners. Ordinary income after GP = $70,000 → A’s distributive share = $35,000. A reports GP $30,000 separately; A’s basis increases by $35,000 (not by the GP).

7) Sale of partnership interest with liabilities and hot assets

Outside basis $60,000 (incl. $15,000 liabilities). Sells for cash $100,000; relieved of $15,000 debt. Amount realized = $100,000 + $15,000 = $115,000 → Total gain = $115,000 − $60,000 = $55,000. Of this, §751 hot assets = $20,000 ordinary; remainder $35,000 capital.

8) §754 / §743(b) step‑up

Buyer pays $150,000 for interest when buyer’s share of inside basis is $100,000. Special step‑up with §754 = $150,000 − $100,000 = $50,000 allocated to underlying assets.

9) Disguised sale two‑year presumption

Partner contributes property with basis $80,000; receives $190,000 within two years. Treated as sale proceeds $190,000 with gain = $110,000.

10) §199A QBI from a partnership

Ordinary business income before GP $200,000; GP to this partner $30,000; partner owns 50%. QBI uses OBI after GP and excludes the GP received. OBI after GP = $170,000 → QBI share = 50% × $170,000 = $85,000.

Practice Questions

1) Formation: Partner A contributes property (basis $60,000, FMV $100,000) with a recourse liability of $30,000 assumed by the partnership (two equal partners). A’s recognized gain and initial outside basis?

  1. Gain $0; Basis $45,000
  2. Gain $15,000; Basis $45,000
  3. Gain $0; Basis $60,000
  4. Gain $30,000; Basis $30,000
Answer & Explanation

A — §721 nonrecognition. Deemed cash distribution from debt relief = $30,000 − A’s share $15,000 = $15,000. Basis = $60,000 − $30,000 + $15,000 = $45,000. No gain because deemed cash ($15,000) ≤ basis.

2) Basis rollforward: Begin $45,000; K‑1 ordinary income $40,000; §1231 loss $10,000; guaranteed payment received $12,000; cash distribution $20,000; increase in share of liabilities $5,000. Ending outside basis?

  1. $60,000
  2. $56,000
  3. $48,000
  4. $72,000
Answer & Explanation

A — Basis increases for distributive income and liability increases, decreases for losses and distributions; guaranteed payments do not increase basis for the recipient. Ending basis = $60,000.

3) Loss limitation (basis only): Stock basis $30,000, recourse $10,000, nonrecourse $20,000, current loss $70,000. Allowed and carryforward?

  1. Allowed $60,000; Carry $10,000
  2. Allowed $30,000; Carry $40,000
  3. Allowed $50,000; Carry $20,000
  4. Allowed $70,000; Carry $0
Answer & Explanation

A — Basis available = $60,000; allowed up to basis; excess $10,000 carries forward (then apply at‑risk/passive limits).

4) Current distribution: Outside basis $60,000; cash distribution $70,000. Recognized gain and ending basis?

  1. Gain $10,000; End basis $0
  2. Gain $0; End basis $10,000
  3. Gain $5,000; End basis $0
  4. Gain $10,000; End basis $10,000
Answer & Explanation

A — Cash exceeding outside basis triggers gain under §731: $70,000 − $60,000 = $10,000; basis cannot go below zero.

5) Property distribution: Outside basis $50,000; partnership distributes land (inside basis $30,000, FMV $80,000). Partner’s basis in the land and new outside basis?

  1. Land basis $30,000; Outside basis $20,000
  2. Land basis $80,000; Outside basis $0
  3. Land basis $20,000; Outside basis $30,000
  4. Land basis $50,000; Outside basis $0
Answer & Explanation

A — §732(a): carryover basis limited by outside basis → land $30,000 and outside basis reduced to $20,000.

6) §704(c) sale: Contributed asset (basis $40,000, FMV $100,000) later sold for $120,000. How is the $80,000 gain allocated between the contributing partner and the other 50% partner (traditional method)?

  1. Contrib $60,000; Other $10,000
  2. Contrib $10,000; Other $60,000
  3. Contrib $40,000; Other $40,000
  4. Contrib $70,000; Other $10,000
Answer & Explanation

A — Built‑in gain ($60,000) first to contributor; residual $20,000 split $10,000 each.

7) Guaranteed payment: Partnership has $100,000 ordinary income before a $30,000 payment to A. Two equal partners. A’s K‑1 shows ordinary business income of ___ and GP of ___; A’s basis increases by ___?

  1. $35,000; $30,000; $35,000
  2. $35,000; $30,000; $65,000
  3. $50,000; $30,000; $50,000
  4. $0; $30,000; $30,000
Answer & Explanation

A — GP reduces partnership ordinary income; A’s distributive share = $35,000. GP is ordinary income to A but does not increase basis, so basis rises only by $35,000.

8) Sale of interest with hot assets: Outside basis $60,000 (incl. $15,000 liabilities). Sells for cash $100,000; relieved of liabilities $15,000. Total gain? Ordinary vs capital?

  1. Total $55,000; Ordinary $20,000; Capital $35,000
  2. Total $55,000; Ordinary $0; Capital $55,000
  3. Total $40,000; Ordinary $20,000; Capital $20,000
  4. Total $75,000; Ordinary $30,000; Capital $45,000
Answer & Explanation

A — Amount realized includes debt relief → $115,000 − basis $60,000 = $55,000. §751 hot assets recharacterize $20,000 as ordinary; remainder capital.

9) §754 election: Buyer pays $150,000 for a partnership interest; buyer’s share of inside basis is $100,000. Special §743(b) adjustment?

  1. $0
  2. $50,000 step‑up to be allocated to assets
  3. $25,000 step‑down
  4. Only affects other partners
Answer & Explanation

B — With §754, the transferee gets a $50,000 special basis step‑up (purchase price − share of inside basis).

10) Disguised sale: Partner contributes property (basis $80,000) and receives cash $190,000 within two years. Presumed disguised sale proceeds and gain?

  1. Proceeds $190,000; Gain $110,000
  2. Proceeds $0; Gain $0
  3. Proceeds $110,000; Gain $30,000
  4. Proceeds $190,000; Gain $0
Answer & Explanation

A — Two‑year presumption treats it as a sale: proceeds $190,000, gain $110,000.

11) Which triggers ordinary income under §751 (“hot assets”)?

  1. Unrealized receivables and inventory
  2. Land held more than one year
  3. Cash distributions
  4. Goodwill for a service partnership only
Answer & Explanation

A — Hot assets include unrealized receivables and substantially appreciated inventory.

12) §707(a) transaction most likely occurs when:

  1. A partner provides legal services to the partnership for a fixed fee outside their capacity as a partner
  2. A partner receives a proportionate profits interest for a capital contribution
  3. A partner receives their distributive share of income
  4. A guaranteed payment is made
Answer & Explanation

A — Transactions with a partner not acting in a partner capacity are treated as occurring between the partnership and a non‑partner.

13) Which amounts are generally subject to self‑employment tax for an individual partner?

  1. Guaranteed payments for services and the distributive share of a general partner’s trade/business income
  2. Distributive share of a limited partner’s trade/business income
  3. All long‑term capital gains reported on Schedule K‑1
  4. Tax‑exempt income
Answer & Explanation

A — GP for services and a general partner’s distributive share are generally SE income; limited partners’ distributive shares are typically excluded (facts can vary for LLCs).

14) §199A QBI: Partnership ordinary income before guaranteed payments is $200,000; GP to this partner = $30,000; this partner owns 50%. What is this partner’s QBI (ignore thresholds/limits)?

  1. $85,000
  2. $100,000
  3. $115,000
  4. $95,000
Answer & Explanation

A — QBI is based on ordinary business income after guaranteed payments and excludes the GP received. OBI after GP = $170,000; 50% share = $85,000.
