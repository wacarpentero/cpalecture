Sampling Techniques

Learning Objectives

  • Distinguish attribute sampling (tests of controls) from variables sampling (substantive testing).
  • Explain MUS/PPS vs classical variables sampling (mean‑per‑unit, ratio, difference) and when to use each.
  • Determine sample size given tolerable and expected deviation/misstatement, population size/variability, and risk levels.
  • Project results to the population, compute allowance for sampling risk, and evaluate against tolerable misstatement/deviation.
  • Apply proper selection methods (random/systematic), stratification, and dual‑purpose testing where appropriate.

1) Attribute Sampling (Controls)

  • Objective: Estimate deviation rate of a control to support control reliance.
  • Key risks: Risk of assessing control risk too low (beta) and too high (alpha).
  • Sample size ↑ when: higher confidence needed (lower beta risk), higher EDR, lower TDR, or larger population.
  • Evaluate: Compute sample deviation rate and upper deviation rate; if upper ≤ TDR → rely on control; else increase CR and perform more substantive work.
  • Dual‑purpose tests: One sample tests both control deviations and monetary misstatements in the same items.
Example

TDR 5%, EDR 1%, beta risk 5% → sample size ≈ few dozens to 90+ depending on tables. If 2 deviations in 60 items ⇒ SDR 3.3%; upper deviation may exceed 5% → likely cannot rely.

2) Monetary Unit Sampling (MUS / PPS)

  • Concept: Each dollar is a sampling unit; larger items have higher chance of selection (systematic with a random start).
  • When to use: Existence/overstatement risk (A/R, revenue); few expected misstatements; many small items.
  • Inputs: Tolerable misstatement (TM), expected misstatement (EM), risk of incorrect acceptance (RIA), population book value.
  • Evaluate: Project misstatements (tainting × sampling interval) + allowance for sampling risk ⇒ upper misstatement limit (UML); if UML ≤ TM ⇒ accept.
  • Pros/cons: Efficient for overstatements; struggles with understatements/zero/negative balances without tweaks.
Mini‑glossary
  • Sampling interval = population book value ÷ sample size.
  • Tainting = (misstatement ÷ book value of item), capped at 100%.

3) Classical Variables Sampling

Mean‑per‑Unit (MPU)

  • Estimate population audited value = sample audited mean × population size.
  • Precision/allowance based on sample standard deviation and confidence factor.
  • Best when population variability is known/estimable and misstatements are two‑sided.

Ratio & Difference Estimation

  • Ratio: Apply audited/book ratio from sample to population book value.
  • Difference: Apply average misstatement per item × population size.
  • Work well when audited and book amounts are strongly correlated (ratio) or when misstatements are fairly constant per item (difference).

4) Sample Selection & Stratification

  • Selection: random (preferred), systematic (ok if no hidden pattern); avoid haphazard bias.
  • Stratification: split population into bands (e.g., items > TM individually tested; remaining bands by size); reduces variability and sample size.
  • Block/cluster sampling generally discouraged for audit objectives due to bias risk.
Tip: Always test individually significant items (e.g., > sampling interval or near TM) outside the sample.

6) Evaluating Results & Projecting Misstatements

  • Controls: compute upper deviation rate and compare to TDR.
  • MUS: compute upper misstatement limit (projection + allowance) and compare to TM.
  • Classical: compute point estimate ± precision; see if likely misstatement exceeds TM.
  • Classify misstatements: factual, projected, judgmental; aggregate across populations and compare with materiality.
Common pitfalls: ignoring understatements in MUS; failing to individually test large items; not adjusting for population changes after extraction.

7) Documentation

  • Objective, population, sampling method, parameters (TM/TDR, EM/EDR, risk levels), selection method, and rationale.
  • Items selected, work performed, misstatements/deviations found, and how results were projected.
  • Allowance/precision, overall conclusion vs tolerable thresholds, and effect on further procedures.

8) Quick Checks

  1. Best method when many zero balances and overstatement risk? MUS/PPS.
  2. Risk names for controls vs substantive? Controls: assessing CR too low/high; Substantive: incorrect acceptance/rejection.
  3. What always gets tested outside the sample? Individually significant items.
  4. Effect of higher expected misstatement on sample size? Increases.
