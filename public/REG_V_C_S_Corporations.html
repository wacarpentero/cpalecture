S Corporations

Learning Objectives

  • Recall who can be an eligible shareholder, the single‑class‑of‑stock rule, the 100‑shareholder limit, and when S status begins/ends.
  • Identify situations in which S status would be revoked or terminated and how to fix them.
  • Calculate ordinary business income (loss) and separately stated items and understand per‑share, per‑day allocation.
  • Compute and track the AAA and determine the tax treatment of distributions with and without accumulated E&P.
  • Compute a shareholder’s stock basis and debt basis, apply the basis → at‑risk → passive loss limits, and carryovers.
  • Recognize corporate‑level taxes that can apply: built‑in gains (BIG) tax, passive investment income (PII) tax, and LIFO recapture on election.

Eligibility & Election

Eligibility (Sec. 1361)

  • Domestic corporation; ≤100 shareholders.
  • Eligible shareholders: individuals (U.S. citizens/residents), estates, certain trusts (QSST/ESBT), and certain tax‑exempt orgs. Not eligible: partnerships, corporations, nonresident aliens.
  • Single class of stock: identical rights to distribution & liquidation proceeds (voting differences allowed).
  • QSub: 100%‑owned subsidiary may elect to be disregarded.

Election (Sec. 1362)

  • File Form 2553 by the 15th day of the 3rd month of the tax year (generally March 15 for calendar‑year corps) or within relief period.
  • Effective date: beginning of the year if timely; otherwise start of next year (unless late‑election relief).
  • Termination: (1) Revocation by >50% of shares; (2) Eligibility failure (e.g., NRA becomes a shareholder); (3) Excess PII with accumulated C E&P for three consecutive years (see PII tax below).

Watch‑out: Disproportionate distribution/waterfall preferences can create a second class of stock. Written agreements matter.

Operations & Separately Stated Items

  • S corps are pass‑throughs: no entity‑level regular tax. Items flow to shareholders on Schedule K‑1.
  • Separately stated: capital gains/losses, 1231, charitable contributions, §179, interest income, dividends, credits, foreign taxes, etc.
  • Non‑separately stated: ordinary business income (loss) after trade/business revenues and deductible expenses.
  • Allocation is per‑share, per‑day unless a permitted interim closing of books applies (ownership change).
  • Reasonable compensation: Shareholder‑employees must be paid reasonable W‑2 wages for services; wages are subject to payroll taxes. Distributions aren’t subject to SE tax.
  • Fringe benefits: >2% shareholders generally include employer‑paid health and certain fringes in wages (with possible above‑the‑line deduction for health insurance).
Illustration — Per‑share, per‑day
A owns 100% from Jan 1–Jun 30; sells 50% to B on Jul 1. Full‑year ordinary income = $120. Per‑share, per‑day allocation (no book close): • A: 6 months × 100% × $120/12 = $60 plus 6 months × 50% × $120/12 = $30 → $90 • B: 6 months × 50% × $120/12 = $30

Stock & Debt Basis

Stock basis annual adjustments (order)

  1. Increase by: income items (taxable & tax‑exempt), separately stated income, and excess depletion.
  2. Decrease by: distributions (not below zero).
  3. Decrease by: nondeductible expenses (incl. 50% meals) and oil & gas depletion.
  4. Decrease by: deductible losses and deductions.

Distributions in excess of stock basis → capital gain.

Debt basis (shareholder loans)

  • Direct loans from shareholder create debt basis. Guarantees do not until paid.
  • Losses reduce stock basis first, then debt basis.
  • Later income restores debt basis first (to the extent previously reduced), then increases stock basis.
Illustration — Basis & debt restoration
Year 1: Basis $20; makes $10 loan (debt basis $10). K‑1 loss $40. → Stock basis ↓ to $0 (−$20), debt basis ↓ to $0 (−$20 of debt). $0 carryforward? The remaining $0—none. Year 2: Ordinary income $30, no distributions. → Restore debt basis first to $10, then stock basis ↑ by remaining $20.

Loss Limitations (Shareholder Level)

  • Basis limitation: deductible only to the extent of stock + debt basis; excess carries forward.
  • At‑risk (Sec. 465): generally excludes nonrecourse debt; carries forward until at‑risk increases.
  • Passive activity (Sec. 469): passive losses offset only passive income unless exception (e.g., real estate professional); suspended until disposition or sufficient passive income.
  • Ordering: apply basis → at‑risk → passive tests in that order.

AAA & Distributions

  • AAA tracks accumulated undistributed S‑corp income (post‑S election). Start with 0 at election.
  • With no accumulated C E&P: Distributions reduce stock basis (tax‑free) to extent of basis; any excess is capital gain.
  • With accumulated C E&P: Ordering is (1) AAA (basis reduction, tax‑free), then (2) dividend from Accumulated E&P, then (3) return of capital to extent of basis, then (4) capital gain.
  • AAA is adjusted annually (increase for income items; decrease for losses, then for distributions, but not below zero by distributions).
Illustration — Distribution with C E&P
AAA 60; Accumulated E&P 25; Stock basis at start 40; Distribution 80. Ordering: 1) AAA portion 60 → tax‑free; basis ↓ to (40 − 60) = 0 but not below zero. 2) Dividend from E&P up to 25 → shareholder dividend income 20 (limited to 25; see basis already at 0). 3) Remainder (if any) → capital gain.

BIG, PII, LIFO Recapture

  • Built‑in gains (BIG) tax: Applies when a former C corp elects S; corporate‑level tax at corporate rate on net recognized built‑in gain during the 5‑year recognition period.
  • Passive investment income (PII) tax: If S corp has accumulated C E&P and PII > 25% of gross receipts, a tax may apply; if this occurs for 3 consecutive years, S status may terminate beginning the next year.
  • LIFO recapture: C corp electing S must include LIFO reserve in income (spread over 4 years); pay additional tax like a C corp on the inclusion year.

Practice Questions

1. Eligibility — single class of stock

Which of the following would create a prohibited second class of stock?

  1. Voting and non‑voting common with identical liquidation rights.
  2. Different dividend rates based on shareholder group agreements.
  3. Restricted stock subject to forfeiture.
  4. Options/warrants outstanding.
Answer & Rationale

Answer: B. Different dividend preferences can violate the single‑class rule. Voting differences and reasonable buy‑sell restrictions typically don’t.

2. Per‑share, per‑day allocation

S corp earns $365 ordinary income in a non‑leap year. On July 1, X sells half of her shares to Y. No interim book close is made. What amount is allocated to X?

  1. $273
  2. $183
  3. $365
  4. $182.50
Answer & Rationale

Answer: A. X held 100% for 181 days and 50% for 184 days: 181/365 × 365 + 0.5 × 184/365 × 365 = 181 + 92 = 273.

3. Basis vs distributions

At Jan 1, T’s stock basis is $10. During the year, ordinary income is $5 and a distribution of $20 is made. No debt basis. Tax result?

  1. $5 dividend; $5 capital gain
  2. $0 dividend; $5 capital gain
  3. $0 dividend; $10 capital gain
  4. $5 dividend; $10 capital gain
Answer & Rationale

Answer: B. Increase basis by income to $15, then reduce by distribution: basis goes to $0 and excess $5 is capital gain. S corp distributions generally aren’t dividends unless there is accumulated C E&P.

4. Loss limitations ordering

Shareholder has $3,000 stock basis, $0 debt basis, is at‑risk for $2,000, and activity is passive. K‑1 loss is $4,000. How much is currently deductible?

  1. $2,000
  2. $3,000
  3. $4,000
  4. $0
Answer & Rationale

Answer: A. Basis test allows up to $3,000, but the at‑risk test limits to $2,000. Passive rules may further suspend the $2,000 unless passive income exists.

5. BIG tax

A former C corp with built‑in gain at election sells appreciated assets in year 3 of S status. Which statement is correct?

  1. No corporate‑level tax applies to built‑in gains after the first year.
  2. Corporate‑level tax may apply to net recognized BIG during the 5‑year recognition period.
  3. BIG tax applies only if the corporation also has passive investment income >25% of gross receipts.
  4. BIG tax rate equals the shareholder’s top marginal rate.
Answer & Rationale

Answer: B. A corporate‑level BIG tax can apply during the 5‑year recognition period, using the corporate tax rate.
