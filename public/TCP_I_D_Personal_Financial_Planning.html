Personal Financial Planning for Individuals

Learning Objectives

  • Build a cash‑flow plan and emergency‑reserve target; prioritize goals.
  • Design retirement accumulation and distribution strategies (401(k)/IRA/HSA; Roth vs Traditional; tax‑efficient withdrawal order).
  • Plan for education funding (529/Coverdell) and large goals (home purchase, college).
  • Assess risk management: life/disability/health/long‑term care; determine coverage needs.
  • Develop investment policy basics (risk tolerance, asset allocation, rebalancing, asset location).
  • Apply tax‑efficient tactics (loss/gain harvesting, bunching deductions, Roth conversions when advantageous).
  • Integrate Social Security/Medicare/Estate basics (beneficiary designations, titling, wills/POAs).

One‑page framework

1) Discover → cash flow, balance sheet, risk tolerance, time horizon 2) Decide → set SMART goals; choose accounts & allocation; define contribution plan 3) Do → automate saving, invest per policy, protect with insurance 4) Diagnose → review annually: rebalance, tax‑optimize, update estate docs

Cash flow & reserves

  • Track fixed vs variable; commit to pay‑yourself‑first savings.
  • Emergency fund: typically 3–6 months of essential expenses (more for one‑income or variable‑income households).
  • Debt ratios to watch: DTI (debt‑to‑income) and housing costs as % of gross income.

Retirement strategy — keys

  • Order of operations: 1) capture full employer match; 2) max HSA if eligible; 3) Roth or Traditional IRA/401(k) as rate‑arbitrage dictates; 4) taxable investing.
  • Roth vs Traditional: prefer Roth when current marginal rate < expected retirement rate; otherwise Traditional.
  • Rule‑of‑thumb targets: first‑year retirement spending ÷ safe initial rate ≈ nest egg target (e.g., $2,000,000 for $$80,000 at 4%).

Education funding

  • 529 plan: tax‑free growth/withdrawals for qualified education; may offer state tax benefits; high contribution ceilings.
  • How much to save? Calculate future cost and back into periodic contributions.

Insurance planning (quick hits)

  • Life insurance: match to capital needs (debts + education + income replacement − resources); term coverage is often most efficient for pure protection.
  • Disability: own‑occupation definitions and adequate benefit periods matter.
  • HSA + HDHP can complement disability and retirement savings (triple‑tax advantage).

Investment policy & tax efficiency

  • Allocation follows goal horizon & risk capacity. Rebalance when bands are exceeded (e.g., ±5 percentage points).
  • Asset location: hold tax‑inefficient assets in tax‑deferred/Roth, tax‑efficient in taxable.
  • Use broad, low‑cost funds; harvest losses/gains thoughtfully within wash‑sale and bracket rules.

Practice Questions

1) Monthly expenses are $4,000. What is a reasonable emergency reserve?

  1. $4,000
  2. $12,000
  3. $24,000
  4. $48,000
Answer & Explanation

C — 3–6 months is typical; $4,000 × 6 = $24,000 for higher stability.

2) Desired first‑year retirement income = $$80,000 (before taxes). Using a 4% initial rate, approximate nest egg?

  1. $1,200,000
  2. $1,600,000
  3. $2,000,000
  4. $3,000,000
Answer & Explanation

C — Target ≈ income ÷ 0.04 = $2,000,000.

3) Employer matches 50% of employee contributions up to 6% of salary. Salary is $$100,000. Minimum you should contribute to capture full match?

  1. $3,000
  2. $6,000
  3. $12,000
  4. $9,000
Answer & Explanation

B — Contribute 6% = $6,000 to receive a 3% match ($3,000).

4) Tax rate now 22%; expected retirement rate 12%. Which account is generally preferable?

  1. Traditional (deduct now)
  2. Roth (tax now, tax‑free later)
  3. Neither makes a difference
  4. Taxable brokerage only
Answer & Explanation

B — Pay tax at the lower rate (22% now vs 12% later suggests Roth; you trade a smaller current tax for tax‑free withdrawals).

5) 529 plan: Contribute $$300 per month for 18 years at 6% annual (monthly compounding). Future value?

  1. $80,000
  2. $100,000
  3. $116,206
  4. $160,000
Answer & Explanation

C — FV of ordinary annuity: $$300/mo → $116,206 after 18 years at 6%.

6) Life‑insurance need (capital‑needs): Income replacement $$60,000 for 20 years at 4% real, plus debts/college $$150,000 and $$120,000; resources $$200,000. Required coverage?

  1. $500,000
  2. $900,000
  3. $885,420
  4. $1,500,000
Answer & Explanation

C — PV of income need + other goals − resources = $885,420 (using 4% real discount).

7) Mortgage: $$400,000 at 6% APR for 30 years. Monthly principal & interest?

  1. $2,200
  2. $2,398
  3. $2,398
  4. $3,000
Answer & Explanation

C — Payment = P·r / (1 − (1+r)^−n) = $2,398.

8) Rebalance a $$500,000 portfolio from 70/30 to 60/40. Trades?

  1. Sell stocks $50,000; buy bonds $50,000
  2. Buy stocks $50,000; sell bonds $50,000
  3. No trades needed
  4. Sell bonds $50,000; buy stocks $50,000
Answer & Explanation

A — Current stocks $350,000 → target $300,000: sell $50,000 and buy bonds $50,000.

9) HSA contribution $$3,600 at a 24% marginal rate. Federal income‑tax savings?

  1. $540
  2. $864
  3. $900
  4. $1,200
Answer & Explanation

B — Savings ≈ contribution × marginal rate = $864 (payroll contributions may also avoid FICA; plan‑specific).

10) Which withdrawal order is generally most tax‑efficient for retirees (all else equal)?

  1. Roth → tax‑deferred → taxable
  2. Tax‑deferred → Roth → taxable
  3. Taxable → tax‑deferred → Roth
  4. Taxable → Roth → tax‑deferred
Answer & Explanation

C — Spend taxable first (capital‑gain rates and basis recovery), then tax‑deferred, preserving Roth for last (highest long‑term value / estate efficiency). Real‑life planning varies.

11) Two‑year bunching: Standard deduction $27,000. Year 1 itemizable $12,000; Year 2 itemizable $29,000. What’s the additional deductions vs taking standard both years?

  1. $0
  2. $2,000
  3. $10,000
  4. $2,000
Answer & Explanation

B — Max(Std, Item) per year: $27,000 + $29,000 − 2×Std = $2,000 more deductions.

12) College cost: Tuition $30,000 today, inflation 5%, needed in 18 years. Estimated first‑year cost?

  1. $45,000
  2. $60,000
  3. $72,199
  4. $120,000
Answer & Explanation

C — Future cost ≈ 30,000 × (1.05^18) ≈ $72,199.
