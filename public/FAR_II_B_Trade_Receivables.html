Trade Receivables

Learning Objectives

  • Recognize and measure accounts receivable at transaction price less allowance for credit losses under the Current Expected Credit Losses (CECL) model.
  • Account for trade and cash discounts, sales returns and allowances, and credit card sales.
  • Prepare aging schedules, compute allowance and bad debt expense, and record write‑offs and recoveries.
  • Distinguish pledging/assigning receivables from factoring and securitization; apply sale vs. secured borrowing criteria.
  • Present and disclose receivables, including nontrade receivables and related‑party balances.

1) Discounts, Returns & Variable Consideration

  • Trade discounts reduce the listed price; record receivable net of trade discount.
  • Cash (sales) discounts such as 2/10, n/30 — use either the gross method (record at invoice amount; discounts taken reduce sales) or the net method (record at discounted amount; missed discounts increase sales).
  • Sales returns and allowances: record refund liability and asset for right to recover inventory (measured at cost minus expected recovery costs); adjust estimates each period.
  • Credit card sales: record receivable from the card network net of the processing fee (selling expense).

2) CECL — Allowance for Credit Losses

  • Estimate lifetime expected losses using historical loss experience adjusted for current conditions and reasonable and supportable forecasts; revert to historical information when forecasts are no longer supportable.
  • Pool receivables with similar risk characteristics; use aging loss rates or other methods consistently and document assumptions.
  • Journal basics: Dr Bad debt expense / Cr Allowance for credit losses; write‑off Dr Allowance / Cr Accounts receivable.

Quick Checks

  1. Does CECL use lifetime or incurred losses? Lifetime expected losses.
  2. Refund liability vs. asset for right to recover — where do they hit? Refund liability reduces revenue; the asset reduces cost of goods sold.
  3. Sale accounting for transfers requires what? Isolation, transferee’s right to pledge/exchange, and no effective control by the transferor.
  4. Write‑off effect on income? None if allowance was adequate; expense recognized when the allowance was recorded.
