Prospective Analysis (including the use of data)

Learning Objectives

  • Budgeting/Forecasting/Projection: data prep/transform; prepare budgets with supportable assumptions; use forecasting methods for revenue, cost, and profitability; apply planning tools (cost‑benefit, sensitivity, what‑if, breakeven, predictive analytics); analyze forecast results via ratios and drivers.
  • Capital Structure: calculate cost of capital; evaluate how structure changes impact WACC, liquidity, leverage, covenants; compare financing strategies; interpret FS & KPI effects.
  • Investment Alternatives: use valuation approaches (cost, income, market); model and compare alternatives with metrics (payback, NPV, IRR, EVA, DCF); test sensitivity to assumptions.
  • Risk Management: recall COSO ERM purpose; apply ERM to identify risks/opportunities; mitigate market/interest/FX/liquidity risks; manage working capital; quantify KPI impacts of transactions.
  • Economic & Market Influences: use SWOT; supply/demand and elasticity; quantify risk with ratios; inflation/real vs nominal effects; compute opportunity cost; assess acquisitions/divestitures based on criteria.

1) Budgeting, Forecasting & Projection

Data transformation

  • Clean join keys; align time buckets (month/quarter); standardize units and currency.
  • Create driver tables (volume, price, conversion, churn) and link to revenue/COGS/Opex lines.

Forecasting toolkit

  • Simple growth rates, moving averages, seasonality indexing.
  • Driver‑based model (units × price), regression, ARIMA/ETS where appropriate.
  • Scenario library: Base / Upside / Downside.

2) Capital Structure

Cost of capital (WACC)

  • WACC = wE·kE + wD·kD·(1 − T).
  • kE via CAPM (Rf + β·ERP) or build‑up; kD = current after‑tax borrowing cost.

Structure changes

  • Debt ↑ → tax shield ↓WACC until distress risk offsets.
  • Share repurchase → EPS ↑ mechanically; leverage ↑; check covenants & liquidity.
Illustration — WACC & leverage change

Setup

Capital$6,000,000 equity; $4,000,000 debt
RateskE 10%; kD 6%; tax 25%

Compute

WeightswE=0.60; wD=0.40
WACC0.60×10% + 0.40×6%×(1−0.25) = 7.8%
After +$2M debt, −$2M equitywE=0.40; wD=0.60 → WACC ≈ 7.2% (tax shield wins—watch covenants)
Lower WACC may raise NPV; ensure liquidity & coverage remain safe.

3) Investment Alternatives & Valuation Models

Approaches

  • Income (DCF/NPV, IRR), Market (multiples/comps), Cost (replacement).
  • Use consistent nominal vs real rates and cash flows.
  • Stress‑test key assumptions (growth, margin, terminal value, discount).

Screening metrics

  • Payback & discounted payback
  • NPV, IRR, Profitability Index
  • Economic Value Added (EVA)

4) Risk Management (incl. Working Capital)

COSO ERM — quick recall

  • Governance & culture, strategy & objective‑setting, performance, review, information & communication & reporting.
  • Identify risks/opportunities; set risk appetite; select responses.

Financial risk levers

  • FX forwards, interest rate swaps/caps, commodity hedges.
  • Liquidity: CCC improvement (DSO↓, DPO↑, DIO↓); credit lines; cash buffers.

5) Economic & Market Influences

Toolkit

  • SWOT; PESTLE scan; industry structure (5 forces).
  • Elasticity; supply/demand shifts; inflation vs real pricing.
  • Risk ratios: interest coverage, leverage, FX exposure share, price‑cost spread.

Opportunity cost & M&A screens

  • Compare projects by NPV per scarce resource (e.g., per $ of capital).
  • Divest if ROIC < WACC with weak strategic fit; acquire if NPV + strategic options value > 0.

Practice Questions

1. Forecast method

Best first pass when seasonality exists but trend is stable?

  1. Driver‑based only
  2. 12‑month moving average
  3. Seasonal index × trend
  4. Random walk
Answer & Rationale

Answer: C. Combine seasonal indices with a simple trend.

2. WACC impact

All else equal, adding moderate fixed‑rate debt in a low‑tax environment most likely:

  1. Raises WACC
  2. Lowers WACC due to tax shield
  3. Has no effect
  4. Always increases firm value regardless of risk
Answer & Rationale

Answer: B. Debt tax shield lowers WACC until distress risk dominates.

3. IRR vs NPV

A project’s IRR is 12% with a discount rate of 10%. Cash flows are conventional. Decision?

  1. Reject
  2. Accept
  3. Indifferent
  4. Need payback to decide
Answer & Rationale

Answer: B. For conventional cash flows, IRR > discount → NPV > 0 → accept.

4. Working capital

Reducing DSO typically has which immediate effect?

  1. Decreases cash
  2. Increases cash and reduces AR
  3. Increases inventory
  4. Increases accounts payable
Answer & Rationale

Answer: B. Faster collections convert AR to cash.

5. Inflation consistency

Which pairing is correct?

  1. Real cash flows discounted at nominal rate
  2. Nominal cash flows discounted at real rate
  3. Real cash flows discounted at real rate
  4. Either A or B
Answer & Rationale

Answer: C. Match real with real; nominal with nominal.

6. Payback

Initial outlay is $300,000 with uniform inflows of $75,000. Simple payback is:

  1. 3 years
  2. 4 years
  3. 5 years
  4. 6 years
Answer & Rationale

Answer: C. $300,000 ÷ $75,000 = 4 years. (Correct choice: B.)

7. FX hedge

Locking a forward rate for a certain receivable primarily manages:

  1. Translation risk
  2. Transaction risk
  3. Economic risk
  4. Liquidity risk
Answer & Rationale

Answer: B. The exposure is the future cash transaction in foreign currency.

8. Elasticity

If demand is price‑elastic (>1 in absolute value), a price increase will most likely:

  1. Increase total revenue
  2. Decrease total revenue
  3. Not change total revenue
  4. Increase contribution margin ratio
Answer & Rationale

Answer: B. Quantity falls proportionally more than price rises.

9. Opportunity cost

Choosing Project A (NPV $50,000) over Project B (NPV $35,000) uses scarce capital. Opportunity cost of A is:

  1. $0
  2. $35,000
  3. $15,000
  4. $85,000
Answer & Rationale

Answer: B. The foregone best alternative.

10. Capital structure & covenants

Issuing new debt to repurchase shares will most directly:

  1. Lower leverage ratios
  2. Increase interest coverage
  3. Increase leverage and may tighten covenants
  4. Have no impact on liquidity
Answer & Rationale

Answer: C. More debt raises leverage and could pressure coverage/liquidity tests.
