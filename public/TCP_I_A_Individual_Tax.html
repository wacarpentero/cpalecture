Individual Tax — Gross Income, AGI, Taxable Income & Estimated Taxes

Learning Objectives

  • Recall the impact of equity compensation awards on taxable income (NSOs, ISOs, RSUs) and possible AMT effects.
  • Recall items included in the determination and computation of Alternative Minimum Taxable Income (AMTI) and AMT.
  • Consider effects on taxable income for certain items of gross income, including imputed interest on below‑market loans and compensation earned outside the U.S.
  • Calculate the tax on a child’s investment (kiddie tax) and other unearned income.
  • Consider the effect of changing tax rates and legislation on the timing of income/expense items for planning purposes.
  • Identify projected tax savings via FSAs and HSAs for planning purposes.
  • Evaluate using either itemized deductions or the standard deduction for a given scenario (e.g., bunching).
  • Calculate estimated tax payments to avoid underpayment penalties under safe‑harbor rules.
  • Calculate potential tax savings and limits when donating noncash property to a charity (holding period, AGI limits, FMV vs basis).
  • Review a client’s projected income/expenses and propose actions to minimize tax for year‑end planning.

Fast framework: From Gross Income → Tax

Gross Income – Above‑the‑line adjustments (educator expenses, HSA, IRA, SE tax, alimony paid pre‑2019, etc.) = Adjusted Gross Income (AGI) – Greater of Standard Deduction or Itemized Deductions = Taxable Income × Rates (ordinary, qualified dividends/LTCG, NIIT where applicable) = Gross Tax – Credits (CTC, education, foreign tax, etc.) + Other taxes (SE tax, NIIT, AMT) = Total Tax → Compare to withholdings & estimated payments

Equity compensation — taxable touchpoints

  • NSO/Nonqual options: Ordinary income at exercise = (FMV − strike) × shares; W‑2 wages; basis = FMV at exercise.
  • ISO/Incentive options: No regular‑tax income at exercise; AMT preference adds the bargain element to AMTI; basis for AMT purposes differs until disposition.
  • RSU: W‑2 income at vest (FMV × shares).
  • Planning: Consider disqualifying ISO sale in same year as exercise to avoid AMT; manage holding periods for LTCG.

AMT essentials

  • Start with taxable income and adjust to AMTI (e.g., ISO bargain element; certain itemized deductions limited/disallowed).
  • Subtract AMT exemption (phase‑outs apply) → apply AMT rates → compare to regular tax; pay the excess as AMT.
  • Credit: Minimum tax credit may recover some AMT in future years.

Gross income hotspots

Below‑market loans — imputed interest (§7872)

  • Interest is imputed at the AFR when stated rate is too low; lender may have deemed interest income and borrower deemed interest expense/compensation or gift.
  • Planning: Watch thresholds/exceptions for de minimis loans and gift loans with limited investment income.

Foreign earned income

  • Qualify via bona fide residence or physical presence tests; eligible wages may be excluded up to the annual FEIE limit (indexed); housing exclusion may apply.
  • Planning: Exclusion reduces AGI; coordinate with foreign tax credit vs exclusion choice.

Kiddie tax — child’s unearned income

  • Applies to certain children’s unearned income above thresholds; taxed at the parent’s marginal rate.
  • Planning: Use 529 plans, timing of capital gains, and family employment strategies.

Timing strategies & rate changes

  • Accelerate or defer income/deductions based on expected future rates (e.g., bunch state taxes into years where deductibility or AMT impact is favorable).
  • Harvest capital gains/losses to manage brackets (0%/15%/20% for LTCG where applicable).

FSAs & HSAs — quick wins

  • FSA: Pre‑tax payroll reductions; use‑it‑or‑lose‑it with limited carry/extension options; great for predictable expenses.
  • HSA: Available with HDHP; triple tax advantage (deductible/pretax in, tax‑free growth, tax‑free qualified withdrawals); portable.

Itemized vs standard deduction

  • Itemize if total exceeds standard deduction. Consider “bunching” charitable gifts/medical expenses/SALT (subject to caps) into one year.
  • Qualified charitable distributions (QCDs) from IRAs can satisfy RMDs (age and limits apply) and reduce AGI.

Estimated tax — safe harbors

  • Avoid penalties by paying the lesser of: 90% of current‑year tax or 100% of prior‑year tax (often 110% if high AGI). Make four equal quarterly payments unless annualized.
  • Due dates typically mid‑April, mid‑June, mid‑September, and mid‑January of the following year.

Charitable gifts — noncash property

  • LTCG property to public charity: Deduct FMV up to generally 30% of AGI; excess carries forward up to five years.
  • Ordinary‑income property/short‑term: Deduct basis (not FMV); higher AGI limits may apply for cash.
  • Substantiation: qualified appraisal >$5,000 (exceptions apply); identify specific shares donated.

Tricky‑area tips (exam‑focused)

  • NSO vs ISO: NSO income at exercise; ISO → watch AMT preference.
  • Kiddie tax: Parent’s rate applies to unearned income above thresholds; earned income still taxed at child’s brackets.
  • Imputed interest: §7872 can create wage/gift interest — track both sides.
  • AGI leverage: Lower AGI can unlock credits/deductions and reduce NIIT/phase‑outs.
  • Safe harbor: 100% (or 110%) prior‑year is a penalty shield even if current‑year tax spikes.

Practice Questions

1) NSO exercise: 1,000 options at $10 strike when FMV = $22. What is ordinary income this year?

  1. $0
  2. $12,000
  3. $22,000
  4. $10,000
Answer & Explanation

B — (22 − 10) × 1,000 = $12,000 W‑2 income; basis equals FMV at exercise.

2) ISO exercise: 500 shares at $8 strike; FMV at exercise $25; no sale this year. Regular‑tax income?

  1. $0 regular‑tax income; AMTI increases by $8,500
  2. $8,500 regular‑tax income and AMTI unchanged
  3. $0 regular‑tax income; AMTI decreases by $8,500
  4. $25 regular‑tax income
Answer & Explanation

A — ISOs create no regular‑tax income at exercise; AMT preference adds bargain element (25 − 8) × 500 = $8,500.

3) Below‑market compensation loan at 0% interest; AFR 4%; annual principal $50,000. Imputed annual interest income to employer?

  1. $0
  2. $1,000
  3. $2,000
  4. $4,000
Answer & Explanation

C — Imputed interest ≈ AFR × principal = 4% × 50,000 = $2,000; treated as compensation to employee and interest income to employer.

4) Kiddie tax: A child (age 16) has $2,400 of unearned interest income and no earned income. Taxed how?

  1. All at child’s rate
  2. All at parent’s rate
  3. Portion sheltered by thresholds; excess at parent’s marginal rate
  4. Not taxable
Answer & Explanation

C — Threshold amounts shelter a slice; unearned income above the threshold is taxed at the parent’s rate.

5) HSA: Which statement is most accurate?

  1. Contributions are after‑tax only
  2. Tax‑deductible (or pretax via payroll), grow tax‑free, and tax‑free if used for qualified medical expenses
  3. Use‑it‑or‑lose‑it at year‑end
  4. Available regardless of health plan type
Answer & Explanation

B — HSAs have triple tax benefits and require HDHP coverage.

6) Itemize vs standard: Taxpayer has $9,000 mortgage interest, $2,000 charitable gifts (cash), and $7,500 medical (AGI $100k, 7.5% floor). Best option?

  1. Itemize $18,500
  2. Itemize $11,000
  3. Standard deduction likely larger unless bunching is used next year
  4. Medical fully deductible
Answer & Explanation

C — Medical above 7.5% AGI threshold only: 7,500 − 7,500 = 0; itemized total $11,000 may be below the standard deduction, so claim standard now and bunch next year.

7) Estimated tax safe harbor: Prior‑year total tax $24,000; current‑year projected $30,000; AGI below high‑income threshold. What avoids penalty?

  1. $27,000 over four payments
  2. $24,000 over four payments
  3. $30,000 over four payments
  4. $12,000 by April 15
Answer & Explanation

B — Paying 100% of prior‑year tax ($24k) meets safe harbor (assuming AGI below the higher threshold).

8) Charitable appreciated stock held > 1 year: FMV $40,000; basis $10,000; AGI $100,000. Max current‑year deduction to a public charity (ignoring other gifts)?

  1. $10,000
  2. $30,000
  3. $40,000
  4. $60,000
Answer & Explanation

B — FMV is deductible but limited to 30% of AGI → 30% × 100,000 = $30,000 (excess can carry forward).

9) Foreign earned income: Assume the FEIE limit is $120,000 for the year. Taxpayer qualifies and earns $150,000 of foreign wages, no housing exclusion. Taxable wages after exclusion?

  1. $0
  2. $30,000
  3. $120,000
  4. $150,000
Answer & Explanation

B — $150,000 − $120,000 = $30,000 remains taxable (simplified assumption for exam math).

10) AMT: Which item most commonly increases AMTI for individuals in equity comp scenarios?

  1. NSO bargain element at exercise
  2. ISO bargain element at exercise
  3. RSU vesting
  4. Charitable cash gifts
Answer & Explanation

B — ISO exercise adds the bargain element to AMTI, potentially triggering AMT.
