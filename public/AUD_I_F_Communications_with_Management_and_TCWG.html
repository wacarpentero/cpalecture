Communications with Management & Those Charged with Governance

Learning Objectives

  • Identify required and appropriate communications to those charged with governance (TCWG) during planning and completion.
  • Distinguish communications to management (e.g., control deficiencies) from communications to TCWG and external parties.
  • Determine the form, timing, and content of communications for fraud, illegal acts, independence, significant risks, and audit results.
  • Recognize documentation requirements and how communications affect the audit report.

1) Communications to TCWG (AU‑C 260)

  • Scope & timing: overall audit strategy, timing of the audit, and significant risks identified.
  • Significant findings: qualitative accounting matters, significant difficulties, uncorrected misstatements, disagreements with management, and independence matters.
  • Internal audit & specialists: how their work will be used; component auditor involvement for group audits.
Tip: If governance is by a board audit committee, communications go to that committee; where governance is by owner‑manager, the owner may be both management and TCWG—document who received what.

2) Control Deficiencies (AU‑C 265)

  • Communicate in writing to TCWG and management significant deficiencies and material weaknesses identified during the audit.
  • Include description, context, and potential effects; avoid providing a design for management’s controls in the letter (to preserve independence).
  • Restrict distribution when appropriate (intended solely for management/TCWG).
Mini‑example

A lack of segregation of duties at a small entity is likely a significant deficiency; if combined with management override and pervasive misstatements, it may be a material weakness.

3) Fraud & Noncompliance (AU‑C 240/250)

  • Fraud by management → report to TCWG; consider resigning if pervasive and unremediated.
  • Material fraud by employees → report to TCWG; immaterial but significant risks → appropriate level of management.
  • Noncompliance with laws & regulations: inquire, inspect, and evaluate effect on the FS; discuss with management/TCWG; consider legal counsel; evaluate reporting obligations to regulators (e.g., when required by law).
Important: Auditor does not provide legal determination of noncompliance, but must obtain sufficient appropriate evidence on identified/suspected noncompliance.

4) Written Representations (AU‑C 580)

  • Letter from management (and TCWG when appropriate) dated the audit report date.
  • Confirms responsibilities for FS and internal control, completeness of information provided, and disclosure of fraud, related parties, and subsequent events.
  • Non‑receipt of representations → scope limitation → consider disclaimer of opinion or withdrawal.

5) How Communications Affect the Report

  • Going concern uncertainty → separate section (or emphasis) in the auditor’s report.
  • Material misstatements uncorrected → modify opinion (qualified/adverse).
  • Scope limitations (e.g., missing representations) → qualified or disclaimer.
  • Significant findings and deficiencies are communicated but do not by themselves mandate a modified opinion unless they affect misstatements or scope.

6) Quick Checks

  1. Who receives written communication of material weaknesses? Management and TCWG.
  2. When must the auditor communicate identified fraud by management? As soon as it is identified — to TCWG.
  3. What is the date of written representations? The auditor’s report date.
  4. Does every significant deficiency require a modified opinion? No — communicate it; modify only if it results in misstatement or scope limitation.
