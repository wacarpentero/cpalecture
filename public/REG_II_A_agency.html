Agency

Learning Objectives

  • Recall the types of agent authority (actual express/implied, apparent, ratification) and how authority is created/terminated.
  • Identify whether an agency relationship exists in a fact pattern (consent, capacity, control; consideration not required).
  • Explain the duties and liabilities of agents and principals (contract & tort, disclosed status, scope of employment).
  • Determine who is liable (agent, principal, both) given a scenario.

At a Glance

Formation & Authority

ConceptKey points
FormationMutual consent + agent acts on principal’s behalf subject to control. Capacity: principal must have capacity; agent needs minimal capacity. No consideration required.
Actual authorityExpress (written/oral) or implied (incidental/necessary to carry out express authority).
Apparent authorityPrincipal’s manifestations cause third party to reasonably believe agent is authorized. Survives secret limits; ends when third parties receive notice.
RatificationPrincipal affirms unauthorized act with knowledge of material facts, accepts entire transaction, and has capacity at time of act and ratification.
Equal dignitiesIf the contract must be in writing (e.g., Statute of Frauds), authority generally must be in writing.
TerminationBy act (revocation/renunciation), by law (death/incapacity, destruction of subject, bankruptcy in some cases). Apparent authority continues until notice.

Who is liable on the contract?

Principal statusLiability to third partyAgent’s liability
DisclosedPrincipal liableAgent not liable (absent agreement)
Partially disclosedPrincipal liableAgent also liable
UndisclosedPrincipal liable (if actual authority)Agent also liable
Exam tip: If the principal is undisclosed or partially disclosed, the agent is liable on the contract.

Tort Liability — Respondeat Superior

IssueKey points
Employee vs. Independent ContractorLook to control factors: right to control manner/means, tools, location, duration, payment method. Generally, no vicarious liability for independent contractors (exceptions: inherently dangerous activities, negligent hiring).
Scope of employmentEmployer liable for employee’s torts within scope (a detour may be in scope; a frolic is not). Intentional torts usually outside scope unless force is authorized/foreseeable (e.g., bouncer).
Agent’s own tortAgent always liable for own torts. Principal may be directly liable for negligent supervision/retention.

Examples — Apply to scenarios

ScenarioAnswer
A store manager is told to buy “what’s needed” for opening day and signs a purchase order for shelves and price guns. Implied actual authority to buy usual and necessary items. Principal bound.
After firing a salesperson, the company forgets to notify regular customers. The salesperson places orders with them. Customers reasonably believe authority continues → apparent authority. Principal bound until notice to third parties.
An agent without authority signs a contract. The principal later accepts the benefits with full knowledge. Ratification binds the principal if the entire transaction is accepted and no material change occurred.
Delivery driver deviates three blocks for coffee and hits a pedestrian. Likely a detour within scope → employer liable.
Principal is undisclosed. Third party sues the agent on the contract. Agent liable; once principal revealed, the third party can elect to sue either (but not both to judgment).

Tricky Areas (Exam Tips)

  • Notice ends apparent authority Give actual notice to prior customers and constructive notice to the public when terminating an agent.
  • Equal dignities If a contract must be in writing, the agent’s authority normally must be written, too.
  • Power vs. right Parties usually have the power to end the agency at will, but may breach the right (contract) by doing so.
  • Unauthorized contract Agent is liable on implied warranty of authority unless the principal ratifies.
  • Independent contractor No vicarious liability—watch for exceptions and for apparent agency fact patterns.
Memory hook: A‑P‑R‑T → Actual, apparent, Ratification, Tort scope. Check authority first, then who’s liable.

Quick Recap

TopicKey point
AuthorityActual (express/implied), apparent from principal’s manifestations, or ratification after the fact.
Disclosure statusAgent liable when principal is partially disclosed/undisclosed.

Quick Practice — 3 Multiple‑Choice Questions (MCQs)

  1. Which statement about apparent authority is correct?
    A. It arises from the agent’s secret beliefs   B. It ends automatically when the principal revokes authority   C. It is created by the principal’s manifestations to third parties   D. It cannot bind the principal
    Show answer
    Answer: C — Apparent authority comes from the principal’s words/conduct causing reasonable belief in third parties and continues until notice.
  2. Who is liable on a contract made by an agent of an undisclosed principal with actual authority?
    A. Principal only   B. Agent only   C. Both principal and agent   D. Neither
    Show answer
    Answer: C — Principal and agent are both liable to the third party.
  3. Which is most likely within the scope of employment?
    A. A driver detours for lunch and rear‑ends a car on the way back   B. A driver borrows the truck on Sunday for a move   C. A salesperson embezzles from a client’s safe   D. A programmer moonlights for a competitor at home
    Show answer
    Answer: A — A brief detour tied to work is generally in scope; the others are outside scope.
